Player's Handbook v1.3

Introduction

You’re about to step into a world shaped by dice, decisions, and shared imagination. Whether you’re completely new to tabletop roleplaying or a veteran learning a new system, this guide will teach you everything you need to create compelling characters, interact with the rules, and work with your group to tell unforgettable stories.

This isn’t just a manual—it’s an invitation to collaborative storytelling. You’ll learn how to build characters with depth and mechanical teeth, navigate the fiction through meaningful choices, and engage with a system designed to support bold action and dramatic consequences. The game adapts to your group’s preferred style, whether you crave tactical combat, tense investigation, social intrigue, or a mix of everything.

What You Need to Know First

Before we dive into the details, let’s establish some core concepts:

The GM (Game Master) is the person who facilitates the game. They describe the world, portray non-player characters (NPCs—characters not controlled by players), adjudicate rules, and frame situations. Think of them as your collaborative partner, not an opponent. The GM sets scenes and challenges, but everyone at the table works together to create compelling stories.

Your PC (Player Character) is the character you create and control. You decide what they do, how they approach problems, and what drives them. Your PC is your lens into the game world and your primary means of engaging with the story.

Sessions and Campaigns: A session is a single play session, typically 2-4 hours. Multiple connected sessions form a campaign—a longer story told over weeks or months. Some campaigns are episodic (self-contained stories each session), while others build toward major narrative arcs.

Collaborative Storytelling: This game thrives on collaboration. The GM frames situations and interprets outcomes, but you drive the action through your choices. Everyone contributes to the emerging narrative. When you roll dice, you’re not just checking for success—you’re determining how the story shifts in response to your actions.

What This Game Is About

At its core, this is a game about meaningful choices and their consequences. You make decisions that matter. Outcomes reshape the world. Pressure builds toward resolution. The system handles uncertainty and translates your actions into dynamic results that drive the story forward.

Genre Flexibility

The game is deliberately genre-flexible. The core mechanics adapt to support:

  • Heroic Fantasy: Bold adventurers facing dangerous quests, ancient evils, and larger-than-life stakes. Think legendary heroes, epic battles, and world-threatening dangers.
  • Cozy Slice-of-Life: Low-stakes stories focused on community, relationships, and personal growth. Run a tavern, solve neighborhood disputes, or navigate the drama of small-town life.
  • Mystery & Horror: Tense investigations, atmospheric dread, and dangerous secrets. Uncover conspiracies, survive supernatural threats, or solve crimes in the shadows.
  • Modern Settings: Contemporary crime, espionage, urban fantasy, or everyday drama. Navigate complex social systems, corporate intrigue, or street-level conflicts.
  • Science Fiction: Space exploration, cyberpunk intrigue, transhumanism, or post-apocalyptic survival. The mechanics scale from intimate personal stories to galaxy-spanning conflicts.

You bring the setting and tone. The system provides structure for action, consequence, and momentum. The same rules that govern a wizard’s arcane duel work equally well for a detective’s interrogation or a pilot’s desperate maneuver through an asteroid field.

Core Themes

Across all genres and tones, the game emphasizes:

Risk and Consequence: Your actions have weight. Success creates opportunities. Failure introduces complications. Every roll shapes the fiction.

Momentum: The game pushes forward. Even failures move the story rather than stalling it. Partial successes give you progress with a cost. The narrative builds toward climactic moments.

Agency: You control your character’s choices and approach. The GM frames situations, but you decide how to engage. Your decisions drive the story.

Meaningful Outcomes: Mechanics translate into fiction. A good roll isn’t just “you succeed”—it’s “you pick the lock silently, find a hidden compartment, and recognize the maker’s mark as belonging to a notorious guild.”

Supported Playstyles

The system flexibly supports different ways to play. Most groups blend multiple styles, but understanding these approaches helps you communicate preferences and choose the right tools for your table.

Activity-Based Styles

Combat-Focused Play: Tactical encounters with meaningful terrain, positioning, and dynamic actions. Each fight creates choices and escalation. You’ll care about the battlefield’s tags (environmental descriptors like “Elevated,” “Fragile Cover,” “Cramped”), your position relative to allies and enemies, and the tactical options available each turn.

Investigation-Heavy Play: Clue gathering, deduction, and problem-solving. Progress clocks track your investigative leads. Partial successes reveal information with complications—you find the evidence but alert the conspirators, or learn a crucial fact but misinterpret its significance.

Social Intrigue: Negotiation, deception, relationship management, and political maneuvering. Social contests resolve conflicting goals. Reputation and leverage matter as much as combat prowess.

Exploration: Discovery, navigation, and survival. Momentum mechanics keep pacing tight while still capturing the sense of journey and the dangers of unknown territory.

Time Commitment Styles

Casual Play (2-3 hours monthly): You want quick setup, low prep, and satisfying progress even with irregular sessions. The system supports this through:

  • Fast character creation: Use archetypes (Chapter 20) for ready-to-play characters in 10 minutes. No optimization anxiety—archetypes are balanced and functional out of the box.
  • Session-based advancement: Progress every 3-4 sessions regardless of narrative pacing, so irregular attendance doesn’t stall growth.
  • Episodic structure: Self-contained scenarios let you miss sessions without losing narrative thread. Each session resolves its core conflict.
  • Minimal bookkeeping: No encumbrance, ration tracking, or detailed inventory management. Focus on story, not spreadsheets.

Weekend Play (3-4 hours weekly): Standard campaign pacing. Most content in this guide assumes weekly play. You’ll build deeper character arcs, engage with longer story threads, and have time for downtime activities between adventures.

Hardcore/Marathon Play (6+ hours, multiple sessions per week): You want depth, complexity, and optimization. The system rewards this through:

  • Optimization paths: Build synergies between skills, proficiencies, and tactics (see Chapter 04 for build guidance).
  • Complex scenarios: The advanced combat and social examples (Chapters 06-07) provide templates for intricate tactical situations.
  • Interconnected arcs: Multiple progress clocks tracking simultaneous goals let you juggle faction relationships, personal quests, and campaign objectives.
  • Downtime depth: Use downtime activities (Chapter 12) to pursue side projects, build influence, and develop your character between adventures.

Player Count Styles

Solo Play (1 player, no GM): You’re the player and referee simultaneously. The system provides:

  • Oracle mechanics: Use the Solo Play tools (Chapter 12: Alternative Play Modes) to answer questions when uncertain. “Does the guard notice me?” Roll dice, consult tables, interpret results.
  • NPC behavior tables: Simple/medium/complex enemy behavior generators help you adjudicate opponent actions fairly without GM bias.
  • Scene structure: Frameworks for pacing solo sessions—exploration beats, action beats, resolution beats—keep narrative momentum.
  • Self-sufficient builds: Character archetypes flagged for solo viability balance offense, defense, and utility so you can handle diverse challenges alone.

Solo play works best with lower stakes (investigation, personal goals) than epic group campaigns. You’re writing interactive fiction more than traditional TTRPG play.

Duet Play (1 GM + 1 player): Intimate, character-focused stories with deeper relationships and personal stakes. See Chapter 12 for character design, pacing adjustments, and companion NPC guidance.

GM-less Play (rotating authority): Distribute GMing responsibilities across all players using structured turn-taking and collaborative adjudication. See Chapter 12 for authority rotation models and tools.

Online Play (virtual tabletops or voice chat): Play remotely using digital tools. Requires adapted communication, pacing, and technical setup. See Chapter 12 for platform choices, best practices, and common pitfalls.

Standard Group (1 GM, 3-5 players): The sweet spot for this system. Party composition balances naturally—you don’t need rigid roles (tank, healer, DPS). Overlap in capabilities creates interesting tactical choices rather than redundancy.

Large Group (1 GM, 6+ players): Possible but challenging. The GM needs strong spotlight management. Popcorn initiative (each player names the next to act) prevents alpha players from dominating. Keep encounters dynamic—static fights with 6+ players drag unless environmental pressure (spreading fire, collapsing structure) forces urgency.

Engagement Intensity Styles

Optimization-Focused (theorycrafting, build synergies): You love maximizing effectiveness. The system rewards this through:

  • Skill synergies: Combine complementary skills for advantage (Stealth + Subterfuge for infiltration).
  • Proficiency stacking: Select proficiencies that compound effectiveness in your specialty.
  • Tactical positioning: Mastering terrain tags, flanking, and environmental effects gives significant advantages in combat.
  • Build archetypes: Chapter 04 provides optimization paths (Tactician, Social Manipulator, Technical Specialist) with worked progression examples.

Narrative-Focused (story, character arcs, drama): You prioritize compelling fiction over mechanical optimization. The system supports this through:

  • Meaningful outcomes: Partial successes create dramatic complications, not just binary pass/fail.
  • Fiction-first resolution: The GM adjudicates based on your described approach, not rigid skill lists.
  • Character progression tied to arcs: Advancement reflects narrative change (Chapter 04), not grinding XP.
  • Thematic choices: Mechanics support choosing “what my character would do” over “what’s tactically optimal.”

Balanced (both mechanics and story): Most tables blend optimization and narrative focus. Build mechanically effective characters, then make thematically appropriate choices during play. The system doesn’t force you to choose between effectiveness and drama—you can have both.

The GM tailors scenes to your group’s interests. Don’t feel locked into one style—most campaigns naturally blend multiple approaches as the story develops.

What This Game Is Not

Setting expectations helps avoid frustration. This system prioritizes certain design goals, which means deliberately excluding others:

Not Granular Simulation: The game doesn’t track detailed inventory weights, precise economics, complex physics, or minute-by-minute timekeeping. If you love calculating encumbrance and managing rations, this system will feel loose. We prioritize narrative momentum over simulationist detail.

Not Zero-to-Hero Progression: Characters are competent from session one. You’re not playing a helpless apprentice who spends twenty sessions becoming capable. Progression focuses on narrative arcs, new capabilities, and expanding your character’s influence rather than grinding stat increases.

Not GM-vs-Players: The GM facilitates and adjudicates, but everyone collaborates. The GM’s goal isn’t to “beat” the players or protect their meticulously planned plot. Instead, they frame interesting situations, present meaningful challenges, and interpret outcomes fairly. If your group prefers adversarial play, this system will frustrate those expectations.

Not Combat-Only: While the system handles tactical combat well, it gives equal mechanical support to social contests, investigative progress, exploration challenges, and downtime activities. If you only care about optimized combat builds, you’ll find plenty of depth—but you’ll also encounter robust systems for non-combat play.

Your Core Responsibilities as a Player

Understanding what you bring to the table helps everyone collaborate effectively. As a player, you have three primary responsibilities:

Engaged Choices

Make decisions that matter. Don’t wait for the GM to prompt you—drive your character forward. When the GM describes a situation, consider what your character would do and declare it. “I want to check the room for traps” beats silence or passively waiting for instructions.

Engaged choices mean: - Declaring actions with clear intent: Tell the GM what you’re trying to accomplish, not just what dice to roll. - Taking initiative: When scenes feel static, push them forward. Investigate the suspicious detail, confront the evasive NPC, or propose a risky plan. - Embracing consequences: Accept outcomes, even unfavorable ones. Roll with failures and complications rather than arguing for do-overs. - Making thematic choices: Sometimes the dramatically appropriate choice isn’t the tactically optimal one. That’s okay—compelling stories emerge from characters making choices true to who they are.

Clear Intent

When you declare an action, communicate your goal and approach. “I want to sneak past the guard using the shadows near the pillars” is vastly more useful than “I roll stealth.”

Clear intent allows the GM to: - Set appropriate DCs based on your approach and the situation - Grant advantage or disadvantage based on your chosen method - Frame meaningful stakes for what happens on different outcomes - Interpret results narratively in ways that connect to your goal

Example of clear vs. unclear intent:

Unclear: “I want to persuade the merchant.”

Clear: “I want to convince the merchant to give us a discount by appealing to her greed—mentioning we’ll be repeat customers if the price is right and subtly implying we have lucrative connections.”

The clear version gives the GM everything they need to adjudicate the action fairly and narrate a compelling outcome.

Collaborative Problem-Solving

Work with your group. Share the spotlight. Build on each other’s ideas. If someone’s stuck, offer support or create openings for them.

Collaborative problem-solving looks like: - Supporting other characters’ actions: When a teammate creates an opportunity, take it. “While she distracts the guard, I’ll slip through the side entrance.” - Offering tactical assistance: Set up allies for success. “I’ll pin the enemy down with covering fire so you can flank them.” - Contributing to group planning: Share your character’s expertise and perspective during discussions. Don’t dominate, but don’t stay silent either. - Rotating spotlight: If you’ve had several consecutive scenes focused on your character, create space for others. Ask another player what their character thinks or does. - Building on contributions: When someone adds a detail to the fiction, accept and expand on it rather than contradicting or ignoring it.

Collaborative Dynamics

Understanding how players and GM work together helps create a positive table culture.

The Player-GM Partnership

This game works through player-GM collaboration, not opposition. The GM frames situations and adjudicates outcomes. You describe actions and face consequences. Together, you create compelling stories.

The GM’s role: - Frame situations with interesting challenges and meaningful choices - Adjudicate outcomes fairly based on the fiction and mechanics - Portray the world and its NPCs with consistency and depth - Maintain pace by knowing when to zoom in for detail and when to summarize - Support player agency by respecting their choices and building on their contributions

Your role: - Drive action through your character’s choices - Engage with the world by asking questions and investigating details - Accept outcomes gracefully, even when they complicate your plans - Collaborate with other players to create satisfying group dynamics - Communicate your preferences, comfort levels, and character goals

Neither role is subordinate. You need each other to make the game work.

Safety and Consent

Tabletop roleplaying can explore intense themes, but it should always feel safe for everyone at the table. Use safety tools to ensure comfort and respect everyone’s boundaries without question.

X-Card: The X-Card is a simple tool anyone can use at any time to say “I’m not comfortable with this content.” How it works:

  • Place an index card marked with an “X” in the center of the table (or use a digital equivalent in online play)
  • Anyone can tap or reference the X-Card when content makes them uncomfortable
  • When the X-Card is used, immediately stop the current content. No questions asked. No explanation required.
  • The GM either rewinds the scene to before the uncomfortable content or fast-forwards past it
  • The game continues without discussing what prompted the X-Card use unless the person who used it wants to discuss it privately

The X-Card creates safety because it requires zero justification. You don’t need a “good enough” reason. Your discomfort is reason enough.

Lines and Veils: Lines and Veils establish content boundaries before play begins:

  • Lines are hard boundaries—content that won’t appear in the game at all. Examples: sexual violence, harm to children, graphic torture, specific phobias.
  • Veils are content that can exist in the game world but “fades to black” rather than being described in detail. Examples: sexual content (it happens off-screen), violence (described in outcome but not gory detail), substance abuse.

Establish Lines and Veils during Session Zero (the first session where you discuss expectations rather than playing). Write them down. Respect them absolutely. Lines and Veils can be added or modified during play if someone realizes additional boundaries are needed.

Check-Ins: Especially when playing intense or emotionally challenging content, periodic check-ins help maintain safety:

  • The GM can pause between scenes to ask “Everyone good to continue?”
  • Players can request a brief break if they need to decompress
  • After sessions with particularly intense content, debrief as a group

Safety tools aren’t about censorship or restricting creative expression. They’re about ensuring everyone can engage with the game comfortably. A player who feels unsafe can’t engage fully with the story.

Reading the Table

Pay attention to your group’s energy and adjust accordingly:

When someone’s disengaged: Create space for them. Ask their character what they think or do. Set up an opportunity that plays to their character’s strengths.

When tension’s high: Offer levity or a moment to breathe. Sometimes a joke, a brief aside, or a shift in scene tone helps.

When pacing drags: Push forward. Declare a bold action. Force a decision. Ask the GM a pointed question that demands resolution.

When someone’s uncomfortable: Even without an explicit X-Card use, watch for body language, disengagement, or visible discomfort. It’s okay to privately check in with someone during a break.

Reading the table is a skill that develops with practice and attention. Start by simply paying attention to others’ faces and engagement levels.

Building Fiction Through “Yes, And”

Fiction at the table builds through acceptance and addition:

Accept what’s established: If the GM says the hallway’s dark, it is. If another player’s character is cautious, respect that. Don’t contradict established facts unless the group explicitly embraces collaborative world-building where players contribute setting details.

Add meaningful details: Enrich scenes through your contributions. “I notice scorch marks on the walls” adds texture. “I draw my blade quietly” shows your approach. These details give the GM material to build on.

Support others’ contributions: When someone creates an opportunity—“I distract the guard”—take it. “I slip past while they’re focused on you” builds momentum. When a player adds a detail about their character’s backstory or motivation, incorporate it into your character’s perspective.

“Yes, and” builds momentum. “No, but” stops it. Even when characters disagree, you as players should be adding to each other’s contributions rather than negating them.

Example of “Yes, And” in action:

GM: “The noble’s study is lavishly decorated. Expensive art on the walls, thick rugs, a desk made of imported wood.”

Player 1: “I notice the desk has a hidden compartment—there’s a slight seam in the wood grain that doesn’t match.”

GM: “Yes, and when you examine it closely, you smell a faint chemical odor from that seam. Something was recently placed in or removed from that compartment.”

Player 2: “I recognize that smell—it’s Serpent’s Breath, a rare poison. My character dealt with it once before.”

GM: “Yes, and that memory reminds you: Serpent’s Breath only remains potent for about six hours after exposure to air. Whatever was poisoned is recently gone.”

Each contribution builds on the previous one, enriching the scene and creating narrative momentum.

How to Read the Rules

Understanding the rules means understanding how uncertainty resolves at the table. When you declare an action with an uncertain outcome, you roll dice. The result determines not just whether you succeed, but how the fiction changes in response. This chapter breaks down the core mechanical framework: how dice work, what DCs mean, how outcomes shape the story, and how the environment affects your actions.

Rolling Dice

This game uses 4d6 (four six-sided dice) as its core resolution mechanic. When the GM calls for a check, you roll 4d6, sum all the dice, add your relevant bonus, and compare the total to a Difficulty Class (DC) set by the GM.

The Basic Roll

Here’s how a check works:

  1. Declare your action with clear intent: what you’re trying to accomplish and how
  2. GM sets the DC based on the fiction: how difficult is this given the circumstances?
  3. Roll 4d6 and sum all four dice
  4. Add your bonus from your relevant attribute or skill
  5. Compare to the DC: your total determines your outcome tier
  6. Calculate margin: subtract the DC from your total (Roll - DC = Margin)
  7. Interpret the result: your margin determines how well or poorly you did

Example: You’re trying to climb a wall under pressure. The GM sets DC 16 (Tough—it’s slippery and time is short). You have +3 from your AGI attribute. You roll 4d6 and get 3, 5, 4, 6 = 18. Add your +3 bonus = 21 total. Your margin is 21 - 16 = +5, which is a Critical Success—you not only climb quickly, but you find a secure position that gives you advantage on your next action.

Key Statistics

Understanding the dice helps you assess risk:

  • Average roll: 14 (before bonuses)
  • Range: 4 to 24 (before bonuses)
  • Distribution: Bell curve centered on 14. Most rolls cluster near the average. Extremes (very low or very high) are rare but possible.

This bell curve means: - Routine tasks (DC 12-14) are likely to succeed if you have any positive bonus - Tough challenges (DC 16-18) require good rolls or favorable circumstances - Legendary tasks (DC 22) demand both skill and luck

Dice Notation

You’ll see dice notation throughout the rules: - 4d6 means “roll four six-sided dice and sum them” - d6 means “a single six-sided die” - 2d6 means “roll two six-sided dice and add them together”

When you see “roll 4d6 + bonus,” that’s the core resolution mechanic. Nearly everything in this game uses that same foundation.

Advantage and Disadvantage

Sometimes circumstances tilt in your favor (or against you). Instead of adjusting the DC, the game uses advantage and disadvantage to modify your dice pool:

Advantage means you roll extra dice and keep the best 4: - +1 Advantage: Roll 5d6, keep the best 4 dice - +2 Advantage: Roll 6d6, keep the best 4 dice

Disadvantage means you roll extra dice and keep the worst 4: - -1 Disadvantage: Roll 5d6, keep the worst 4 dice - -2 Disadvantage: Roll 6d6, keep the worst 4 dice

Stacking: Multiple sources of advantage or disadvantage don’t stack beyond ±2 levels. If you have both advantage and disadvantage, they cancel out—net them (e.g., +1 advantage and -1 disadvantage = roll normally with 4d6).

When do you get advantage or disadvantage? The GM grants these based on the fiction: - Advantage: Favorable positioning (elevated ground), superior tools (masterwork lockpicks), helpful circumstances (enemy distracted), or supportive tags (Dim Light when sneaking) - Disadvantage: Poor positioning (cramped space), inadequate tools (improvised weapon), hindering circumstances (wounded), or hostile tags (Slick floor when climbing)

Example (Advantage): You’re tracking someone through fresh mud (+1 advantage from favorable circumstances). You roll 5d6: 2, 4, 5, 3, 6. You keep the best 4: 3, 4, 5, 6 = 18. Add your +2 Survival bonus = 20 total against DC 16. Margin +4, a strong Full Success.

Example (Disadvantage): You’re climbing a slick wall (-1 disadvantage from the Slick tag). You roll 5d6: 2, 4, 5, 3, 6. You keep the worst 4: 2, 3, 4, 5 = 14. Add your +3 Athletics bonus = 17 total against DC 16. Margin +1, a narrow Full Success—you make it, but barely.

Understanding DCs

When you declare an action with uncertain outcome, the GM sets a Difficulty Class (DC)—the target number you need to meet or exceed. The DC comes from the fiction: it reflects risk, opposition, tools, positioning, and time pressure.

Here’s what each DC tier means in practice:

  • DC 12 (Easy): You have good position, adequate time, and proper tools. This might still matter because of what happens if you fail, but success is likely for anyone competent.
    • Example: Picking a simple lock with proper tools and no pressure
  • DC 14 (Routine): Standard challenge under normal conditions. If you’re trained and have a moment to focus, you’ll usually succeed.
    • Example: Persuading a neutral merchant, climbing a wall with handholds
  • DC 16 (Tough): Notable obstacles, poor angle, or time pressure. You’ll need skill or favorable circumstances to reliably succeed.
    • Example: Disabling a trap while guards approach, navigating through a storm
  • DC 18 (Hard): Steep odds, strong opposition, or tight timing. Even skilled characters face real risk of failure.
    • Example: Treating an exotic poison, infiltrating a heavily guarded facility
  • DC 20 (Heroic): Exceptional challenge with complications stacked against you. Success requires both skill and luck (or advantage from setup).
    • Example: Tracking through a blizzard, convincing a hostile crowd
  • DC 22 (Legendary): Near-impossible without perfect setup, significant advantage, or extraordinary luck. The GM should rarely set DCs this high—most “impossible” tasks shouldn’t have a roll at all.
    • Example: Predicting a rare celestial event, disarming an ancient magical mechanism

The GM sets DCs based on circumstances, not your character’s ability. Your bonuses represent your skill. The DC represents the task’s inherent difficulty given the situation. A DC 16 lock doesn’t become DC 14 because you’re good at lockpicking—instead, you’re more likely to beat DC 16 because of your bonus.

Outcome Tiers

Your margin (Roll - DC) determines how well or poorly you did. The game has five outcome tiers:

Critical Success (Margin ≥ +5)

You succeed spectacularly with extra benefit. Not only do you achieve your goal, you gain something additional: extra information, improved position, a cleared obstacle, or narrative advantage.

Examples: - Picking a lock: You open it silently, find a hidden compartment, and recognize the maker’s mark - Persuading an NPC: They agree enthusiastically and offer to do more than you asked - Combat attack: You deal damage and create an opening for an ally or knock the enemy off balance

Full Success (Margin ≥ 0)

You achieve your goal cleanly. Progress ticks, you gain position, or you clear an obstacle. This is the default “success” outcome—you did what you set out to do without complication.

Examples: - Picking a lock: You open it efficiently and enter - Persuading an NPC: They agree to your request - Combat attack: You deal damage as intended

Partial Success (Margin -1 to -2)

You succeed, but with a cost, complication, or reduced effect. The GM typically pre-states what the cost might be before you roll: “You can leap the gap, but you’ll land hard and take the Winded condition” or “You convince the guard, but she’ll want a favor later.”

Partials keep the story moving forward while maintaining tension. You make progress, but the situation gets more complicated.

Examples: - Picking a lock (Partial): You open it, but your tools break or the mechanism jams—you’re getting through, but you can’t lock it behind you - Persuading an NPC (Partial): They agree, but demand immediate payment or an uncomfortable compromise - Combat attack (Partial): You deal reduced damage or the enemy counterattacks

Failure (Margin ≤ -3)

You don’t achieve your goal, and the situation complicates. The GM introduces a new obstacle, escalates danger, or fills a pressure clock. Failures move the story forward—they change what happens next rather than stalling play.

Examples: - Picking a lock (Failure): The lock stays shut and you hear footsteps approaching—the guards are coming - Persuading an NPC (Failure): They refuse and grow suspicious of your motives - Combat attack (Failure): You miss and leave yourself exposed, or the enemy seizes the initiative

Critical Failure (Margin ≤ -7 or all 1s)

A disaster occurs—the worst possible result with major complications. The GM amplifies the failure: things break, attention is drawn, or dangerous consequences land immediately.

Special Rule: Rolling all 1s (1, 1, 1, 1) is always a Critical Failure regardless of bonuses or margin. Even the most skilled character can have catastrophically bad luck.

Examples: - Picking a lock (Critical Failure): Your pick breaks off in the lock, jamming it permanently, and the noise alerts nearby guards - Persuading an NPC (Critical Failure): They take offense and call for security, or spread damaging rumors about you - Combat attack (Critical Failure): You drop your weapon, hurt yourself, or create an opening the enemy exploits viciously

Reading Results in Play

The GM interprets your margin into fiction. A margin of +8 isn’t just “you rolled high”—it’s “you pick the lock silently, find a hidden compartment, and have time to search it before anyone notices.” The better your margin, the more favorable the outcome.

Similarly, a margin of -5 isn’t just “you missed the DC”—it’s “you make noise, the patrol rounds the corner, and you have seconds to decide: run, hide, or talk fast?”

Margins translate mechanical results into narrative consequences. Every roll shapes what happens next.

Tags & Conditions

Tags and Conditions describe how the world and your character work right now. They’re visible state that everyone can see and reference.

Tags

Tags describe environments or temporary situational states. The GM names 1–2 meaningful tags per scene and makes their effects explicit.

Common tags include: - Environmental: Dim Light, Cramped, Elevated, Obscured, Slick - Situational: Fragile Cover, Hazardous, Crowded, Unstable

Tags grant advantage or disadvantage based on how you interact with them: - Dim Light gives advantage to Stealth, disadvantage to ranged attacks - Elevated gives advantage to ranged attacks from above - Slick imposes disadvantage on movement and balance checks

You can create, change, or clear tags through actions: - “I kick over the brazier to create Thick Smoke” - “I spend my turn clearing rubble to remove the Obstructed tag” - “I use an action to stabilize the Unstable scaffolding”

Conditions

Conditions describe what’s happening to your character right now. They change your capabilities—imposing disadvantage, reducing movement, or preventing certain actions.

Common conditions include: - Exhausted: Disadvantage on physical actions, reduced movement - Restrained: Movement severely limited, disadvantage on physical actions requiring mobility - Bleeding: Time pressure—the condition worsens if not treated - Dazed: Disadvantage on perception and reaction checks - Frightened: Disadvantage on actions while the threat is visible

When the GM applies a condition, they explain: - What it does: How it affects your capabilities - How to clear it: What actions or resources remove it (first aid, rest, tactics, removing the source)

Conditions have clear mechanical effects and clear resolution paths. You’re never stuck with a condition you can’t eventually address.

Clocks & Progress

Clocks track momentum—yours and the opposition’s. They’re circles divided into segments (usually 4, 6, or 8) that fill as actions succeed or fail. The GM shows clocks when they matter, making stakes visible.

Progress Clocks

Progress Clocks track your goals: escaping the ruin, convincing the council, disarming the ritual, investigating the mystery.

  • Full Successes tick progress (usually 1 segment)
  • Critical Successes tick more progress (usually 2 segments)
  • Partial Successes might tick progress with a cost
  • Failures don’t tick progress

Fill the clock completely, you achieve the goal. Progress Clocks show how close you are to success and help pace extended challenges.

Pressure Clocks

Pressure Clocks track escalating danger: guards arriving, fire spreading, poison taking hold, enemy reinforcements, ritual completion.

  • Failures tick pressure (as pre-stated by the GM)
  • Partial Successes often tick pressure (as pre-stated)
  • Full Successes usually don’t tick pressure

Fill the pressure clock completely, the complication lands: guards arrive, the building collapses, the poison reaches lethal toxicity, the enemy’s plan succeeds.

How Clocks Work Together

Most interesting scenes have both clocks running simultaneously. You’re racing to fill your Progress Clock before the Pressure Clock fills. Each roll matters because it affects both clocks differently depending on the outcome.

The GM typically uses paired clocks for: - Chases (Progress: Escape; Pressure: Caught) - Investigations (Progress: Solve Mystery; Pressure: Conspiracy Realizes You’re Investigating) - Infiltrations (Progress: Reach Objective; Pressure: Security Alert Level) - Disasters (Progress: Escape Building; Pressure: Structure Collapse)

Clocks make stakes visceral. You see progress accumulating and pressure building in real time.

Design Philosophy: Why Clocks Work

Clocks create visible tension. Traditional TTRPGs often hide progress (“how many more clues do we need?”) and threats (“when will reinforcements arrive?”). This creates uncertainty, but it also breeds frustration—players can’t gauge pacing or make informed choices.

Clocks solve this by making progress and danger transparent. You see exactly how close you are to success (Progress Clock filling) and how much time you have (Pressure Clock ticking). This creates informed urgency: “We’re at 3/6 progress, but pressure is 2/4—do we push forward or retreat?”

Psychologically, filling segments is satisfying. Each tick forward feels like meaningful progress, even when the goal is distant. Partial successes that tick progress with a cost become interesting trade-offs, not just failures. You’re always moving the story forward—either toward success or toward complication.

From a GM perspective, clocks eliminate arbitrary time tracking. Instead of “the guards arrive whenever I feel like it,” the GM has a clear framework: “Failures and Partial Successes tick pressure.” This keeps pacing consistent and fair. Players trust that the world responds to their actions, not GM fiat.

In tactical terms: Clocks reward decisive action. Stalling doesn’t stop pressure clocks from filling. Taking risks (accepting Partial Success) at least advances progress. The game pushes you forward, creating momentum even in uncertain situations.

Worked Example

Let’s see all these concepts working together in a complete scene:

Setup: You’re sneaking through a manor’s hallway to reach the study before guards return. The GM establishes: - Tag: Dim Light (advantage to Stealth, disadvantage to seeing details) - Pressure Clock: “Guard Patrol Returns” (4 segments, currently 2/4 filled) - Goal: Reach the study and find the incriminating ledger

Scene Play

GM: “The hallway’s dim—sconces burn low. You hear boots echoing two rooms away. The study door is ahead, maybe thirty feet. The patrol’s getting close. What do you do?”

You: “I move quickly but quietly, using the shadows along the wall. I want to reach the study before they round the corner.”

GM: “Stealth check, DC 14. The Dim Light tag gives you +1 advantage (roll 5d6, keep best 4). If you succeed, you reach the study. Partial means you make it but knock something over—I’ll tick pressure. Failure means they spot you or you have to freeze and waste time—I’ll tick pressure and you won’t make progress.”

You roll 5d6 with advantage: 2, 4, 5, 3, 6. Keep best 4: 3, 4, 5, 6 = 18. Add your +2 Stealth bonus = 20 total. Margin: 20 - 14 = +6 (Critical Success).

GM: “You slip into the study silently, pulling the door almost closed behind you. The patrol passes in the hallway, completely oblivious. You even had time to notice a loose floorboard near the desk—might be useful.” (No pressure tick; you gained a tactical detail from the critical success)

Inside the study, you search for the ledger:

GM: “Papers everywhere, files stacked haphazardly. This is going to take time you might not have. Let’s use a Progress Clock: ‘Find Ledger’ (4 segments). Each success ticks progress. The guard patrol is doing their rounds—they’ll eventually circle back. DC 16 for the search—tough because of the disorganization and time pressure.”

You roll 4d6: 3, 3, 4, 4 = 14. Add your +2 Investigation bonus = 16 total. Margin: 16 - 16 = +0 (Full Success).

GM: “You systematically check the desk drawers. Third one down, you find a ledger with recent entries. Tick progress (1/4 segments filled). As you’re examining it, you hear a door creak downstairs. The patrol’s still moving. What do you do?”

You: “I want to photograph the key pages quickly with my phone, then put it back so they don’t know I was here.”

GM: “DC 14 to do it fast and accurately. Partial means you get the photos but smudge ink or leave the desk obviously disturbed—tick pressure. Failure means you fumble with the phone and definitely alert them—tick pressure and no progress.”

You roll 4d6: 2, 2, 5, 3 = 12. Add your +1 bonus = 13 total. Margin: 13 - 14 = -1 (Partial Success).

GM: “You snap photos of three key pages, but in your haste you knock over an inkwell on the desk. It’s obvious someone was here. Tick progress (2/4 on Find Ledger) and tick pressure (3/4 on Guard Patrol). You hear footsteps on the stairs now—they’re coming up. You have the photos, but they’ll know there was an intruder. What’s your move?”

The scene continues. Each roll shaped the fiction: Critical Success gave you a clean entry and a bonus detail. Full Success made progress. Partial Success gave progress with a complication. Tags affected your rolls (Dim Light advantage). Clocks made the pressure visible and escalating.

That’s how the rules work in play: dice determine outcomes, margins shape how good or bad those outcomes are, and everything feeds back into the fiction to create the next situation.

Character Creation

Creating a character means building three interlocking layers: concept (who they are and what makes them compelling), mechanical framework (what they can do and how they engage with the game’s systems), and narrative hooks (why they matter to the story and what pulls them into the action). This chapter walks you through that process step by step, with concrete examples and guidance for creating characters that feel both mechanically competent and dramatically engaging.

Character creation is fundamentally a collaborative act. You’re not building a character in isolation—you’re creating someone who will interact with other PCs, engage with the GM’s world, and drive stories forward through their choices. Before you dive into mechanics, talk with your GM and fellow players about the campaign’s themes, tone, and setting. Discuss what kind of stories your group wants to tell. A character built for gritty survival horror won’t mesh well with a group playing heroic adventure fantasy. Alignment on expectations creates richer, more satisfying play.

Think of character creation as building the foundation for a living, evolving person. You’re not writing their entire biography—you’re establishing who they are at the start of the campaign. Play will reveal depths, contradictions, and growth you can’t predict now. Start with a clear, evocative core and let the character develop through actual decisions at the table.

Step 1: Concept & Attributes

Start with concept: a short, evocative description of who this character is, what they do, and what drives them. Think in terms of character archetypes and roles, but give yours a specific twist or detail that makes them yours.

Good concepts are specific and actionable: - “A street-smart investigator haunted by her missing partner” - “A grizzled mercenary trying to retire peacefully but pulled back by old debts” - “A brilliant but naive scholar uncovering secrets that powerful people want buried”

Weak concepts are vague or passive: - “A fighter” (what kind? what motivates them?) - “Someone who wants adventure” (too broad, no dramatic tension) - “A person with magic powers” (no personality, no conflict)

Your concept should immediately suggest narrative possibilities. When you describe your character, the GM and other players should instantly see story hooks—relationships to build, conflicts to explore, goals to pursue.

Once you have your concept, translate it into attributes. Attributes are the four core capabilities that define your character’s baseline competencies:

MIG (Might): Physical power, endurance, and resilience. Might governs lifting, smashing, enduring physical hardship, and resisting bodily harm. High Might characters are physically imposing—warriors, laborers, athletes, brawlers.

AGI (Agility): Speed, precision, and reflexes. Agility governs dodging, climbing, shooting, sleight of hand, and any action requiring finesse or quick reactions. High Agility characters are nimble and coordinated—thieves, scouts, duelists, acrobats.

RSN (Reason): Logic, knowledge, perception, and analytical thinking. Reason governs analyzing evidence, recalling information, noticing details, and solving problems through intellect. High Reason characters are sharp-minded—scholars, investigators, engineers, tacticians.

PRE (Presence): Charisma, willpower, and social influence. Presence governs persuading, commanding, intimidating, and resisting mental pressure. High Presence characters are compelling and forceful—leaders, diplomats, performers, con artists.

Assigning Attribute Bonuses

Your GM will tell you how to assign attribute bonuses. A common starting array is +2, +1, +1, +0—assign these four values to your four attributes in any order based on your concept.

Example (Kira, the investigator): - MIG +0: Not physically imposing, relies on other tools - AGI +1: Quick reflexes, good for tailing suspects and staying mobile - RSN +2: Sharp analytical mind, her primary strength - PRE +1: Socially competent, can talk her way into places

Attributes add directly to your 4d6 rolls. When you make a check, you roll 4d6, sum all four dice, and add the relevant attribute bonus. If Kira makes a Deduction check (typically RSN-based), she rolls 4d6+2. If she tries to intimidate someone (typically PRE-based), she rolls 4d6+1.

Higher bonuses make success more likely, but they’re not everything. A character with +0 in an attribute can still succeed through clever approaches, environmental advantage, or teamwork. Attributes establish your baseline, but play reveals how you use those capabilities.

Step 2: Skills & Proficiencies

Skills define what you’re trained in—the specific areas where you excel beyond raw attributes. Instead of choosing individual skills piecemeal, you’ll select a skill package that fits your concept and the campaign’s genre.

Skill packages are thematic bundles designed for specific character archetypes and settings. For example:

Fantasy Packages: - Blades & Brawling: Melee combat, intimidation, physical prowess - Arcane Lore: Magic theory, ancient languages, mystical perception - Wilderness Survival: Tracking, foraging, navigation, animal handling

Modern Packages: - Urban Investigation: Research, surveillance, deduction, streetwise - Firearms & Tactics: Shooting, combat tactics, military protocols - Social Engineering: Persuasion, deception, reading people, networking

Science Fiction Packages: - Piloting & Navigation: Starship operation, astrogation, zero-g maneuvering - Hacking & Systems: Computer intrusion, electronic warfare, system analysis - Xenobiology: Alien life study, exobotany, medical xenoscience

Each package gives you bonuses or advantage when using those skills. Consult your GM or the Mechanics Reference for complete skill lists and packages appropriate to your campaign’s genre and setting.

Proficiencies grant narrative permissions and mechanical edges beyond skills. While skills govern what you’re trained in, proficiencies define what you can do that others can’t and special advantages you bring.

Examples of Proficiencies: - Master Duelist: Advantage when fighting one-on-one in melee, narrative permission to challenge opponents to formal duels - Alchemist: Can craft potions and identify compounds, narrative permission to have specialized alchemical equipment - Polyglot: Speak and read multiple languages fluently, advantage on checks involving translation or linguistics - Tech Savant: Advantage when working with unfamiliar technology, narrative permission to jury-rig improvised devices

You typically start with 2–3 proficiencies that enhance your concept. Choose proficiencies that create interesting narrative opportunities, not just mechanical bonuses. “I’m a Polyglot” opens doors (literally and figuratively) in a way that pure mechanics can’t capture.

Skills and proficiencies together define your fictional positioning—what you can attempt, how likely you are to succeed, and what narrative permissions you have. When you say “I’m picking the lock,” having Thievery skill means you’re competent. Having the Master Locksmith proficiency means you can attempt locks others couldn’t even try.

Urban Investigator Skill Package At-a-Glance

The Urban Investigation package is built for modern noir settings—detectives, journalists, fixers, or anyone navigating city streets, corruption, and crime. It emphasizes information gathering, risk management, and navigating urban power structures. Here’s how these skills work together during play and what challenges they’re designed to handle.

Core Skills:

Research (RSN): Digging through records, archives, databases, and paper trails to find connections others missed. You’re assembling timelines, cross-referencing names, identifying patterns in bureaucratic noise. Research takes time and access—you need either physical presence at archives or digital access to databases.

Typical DCs: - DC 14: Standard searches with good access (public records, library archives, mainstream databases) - DC 16: Information is scattered or partially redacted (cross-referencing multiple sources, old case files, obfuscated corporate records) - DC 18+: Buried, protected, or deliberately hidden information (sealed court documents, encrypted files, scrubbed records)

Example: You’re investigating a suspicious death. Research at DC 14 pulls up the official report—accidental overdose. Research at DC 16 cross-references pharmacy records and finds the victim never filled prescriptions for that drug. Research at DC 18+ digs into deleted financial transactions showing payments to a known fixer the day before death.

Surveillance (RSN/AGI): Tailing suspects, staking out locations, capturing evidence without detection. Surveillance is patient, methodical work—watching patterns, documenting movements, identifying contacts. It requires good positioning, the right equipment (cameras, listening devices), and the discipline to stay invisible.

Typical DCs: - DC 14: Routine surveillance in public spaces with good cover (busy streets, cafes with sightlines, parks with concealment) - DC 16: Target is cautious or environment is challenging (residential areas with few crowds, limited cover, hostile neighborhoods) - DC 18+: Target actively countersurveillance or environment is hostile (professional criminals, secure facilities, locations where you stand out)

Example: You’re staking out a dockside warehouse. DC 14 surveillance from a nearby rooftop with binoculars captures comings and goings. DC 16 requires getting closer or working in bad weather—you get photos but risk exposure. DC 18+ means tailing a target through hostile territory where locals notice outsiders immediately.

Deduction (RSN): Connecting clues, identifying patterns, reading crime scenes, reconstructing events from incomplete evidence. Deduction is synthesis—taking disparate facts and building coherent narratives. It’s Sherlock Holmes logic: “When you eliminate the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth.”

Typical DCs: - DC 14: Straightforward deductions from clear evidence (timeline reconstruction from witness statements, identifying obvious inconsistencies) - DC 16: Complex patterns requiring multiple data points (profiling suspects from behavior patterns, identifying connections between seemingly unrelated events) - DC 18+: Incomplete or contradictory evidence requiring intuitive leaps (reconstructing events from forensic traces, identifying deception in carefully crafted alibis)

Example: You examine a crime scene. DC 14 deduction identifies the entry point and sequence of events. DC 16 deduction analyzes blood spatter and body position to determine the killer was left-handed and approximately six feet tall. DC 18+ deduction spots the micro-staging—furniture moved slightly to suggest struggle, but dust patterns prove it was repositioned after the death.

Streetwise (PRE): Reading social dynamics, finding contacts, navigating underground networks, knowing who to ask and how to ask without drawing unwanted attention. Streetwise is social capital in places where official authority doesn’t reach—criminal underworlds, marginalized communities, gray-market economies.

Typical DCs: - DC 14: Finding reliable informants in your home territory, basic navigation of local underground networks - DC 16: Gaining trust in unfamiliar neighborhoods, approaching contacts with valuable or dangerous information - DC 18+: Operating in actively hostile territory, convincing high-level criminals or paranoid contacts to talk, navigating factional conflicts

Example: You need information about illegal shipments at the docks. DC 14 streetwise finds a dockworker willing to talk over drinks. DC 16 streetwise gets you an introduction to a mid-level smuggler who can confirm routes and schedules. DC 18+ streetwise convinces the smuggler to reveal his employer’s identity despite the risk to his own neck.

Worked Example: Kira Investigates

Kira is investigating a murder tied to her missing partner. The victim was a low-level accountant who worked for a shell corporation. Here’s how her investigation unfolds using the Urban Investigation package:

Scene 1: Research

Kira starts with Research (DC 14) to pull police reports and public records. She rolls 4d6+2 (RSN +2) = 16. Full Success. She finds the victim was last seen leaving a dockside warehouse owned by Meridian Imports, a company with thin financials and a PO box address. The official cause of death was “accidental fall,” but the coroner’s notes mention “inconsistent bruising.”

Scene 2: Streetwise

Kira uses Streetwise (DC 16) to find a contact at the docks who might know about Meridian Imports. She rolls 4d6+1 (PRE +1) = 17. Full Success. Her contact, an old dockworker named Sal, tells her about late-night shipments—unmarked crates, regular crew, and a man in an expensive suit who shows up every Tuesday. Sal’s seen people get hurt for asking too many questions.

Scene 3: Surveillance

Kira stakes out the warehouse with Surveillance (DC 16). She rolls 4d6+1 (AGI +1) = 14. Partial Success (margin -2). She photographs suspects loading crates and identifies the suited man from Sal’s description, but one of the crew notices her camera flash. She’s got evidence, but she’s burned her cover—now they know someone’s watching.

Pressure Escalates:

The GM ticks a pressure clock: “Meridian Aware.” Kira has valuable intel (photos, confirmation of illegal activity) but the opposition knows someone’s investigating. She’ll need to move fast or they’ll cover their tracks.

When to Use This Package

Urban Investigation excels at information gathering, but every check carries risk. Successes reveal clues. Partials reveal clues with exposure—you learn something but tip your hand, burn a contact, or draw attention. Failures alert the opposition or close leads entirely.

Use this package when your campaign involves uncovering secrets, navigating urban power structures, and managing the consequences of poking into dangerous truths. It’s perfect for noir mysteries, conspiracy investigations, crime procedurals, or any game where information is power and knowledge is dangerous.

For complete skill details and DC guidance: See Mechanics Reference, Chapter 10 (Modern Skills).

Step 3: Starting Gear & Resources

Your GM will guide starting gear and resources. Unlike some RPGs that track every coin and ounce, this system prioritizes narrative significance over simulationist detail. You don’t need exhaustive equipment lists—you need tools that matter to your character’s approach and story.

Core Tools

Most characters begin with equipment relevant to their concept and skill packages:

Weapons and armor: If combat is central to your concept, you’ll have appropriate armaments. A mercenary starts with a sword and shield or firearm. A duelist has a rapier and light armor. A brawler might just have brass knuckles and attitude. Your gear should match your concept and proficiencies.

Professional equipment: Investigators have lockpick sets, cameras, and notebooks. Scholars have reference books and research tools. Hackers have laptops and custom software. Alchemists have portable labs. Your professional tools create narrative permission—you can attempt tasks because you have the right equipment.

Resources (Narrative Tools)

Resources are intangible assets that give you leverage in the fiction:

Contacts: People who owe you favors, provide information, or offer safe harbor. “I know a fence in the Rivertown district” or “My old squad mate works port security now.”

Reputation: How you’re known in relevant circles. “I’m respected among the Thieves’ Guild” or “Nobles remember I saved the Duke’s daughter.”

Safe houses and havens: Places you can retreat to, hide, or regroup. “I have a bolt-hole in the warehouse district” or “The monastery will shelter me if I need sanctuary.”

Debts and obligations: Things owed to you or that you owe. “The crime lord owes me a favor for past work” or “I owe the information broker three jobs for the intel he provided.”

Resources aren’t tracked with numbers—they’re fictional positioning you can leverage during play. When you invoke a contact, the GM decides what they can do and what it costs. When you use a favor, it gets expended. Resources create story, not mechanical bonuses.

Example: Kira’s Starting Gear

  • Core tools: Pocket notebook (for notes and sketches), concealed camera (for surveillance), lockpick set (for accessing restricted areas), burner phone (for anonymous contacts)
  • Resources:
    • Contact in the police department (Detective Rourke, who feeds her information off the books)
    • Contact at the docks (Sal, the dockworker from the example above)
    • Favor owed by a fixer named Voss (dangerous to invoke, but powerful when needed)

This gear doesn’t make Kira mechanically stronger, but it shapes how she approaches problems. She’s not kicking down doors—she’s gathering evidence, leveraging contacts, and operating in gray areas where official authority doesn’t reach.

Step 4: Connections & Hooks

The difference between a good character and a great one often comes down to connections and hooks—the relationships and motivations that tie your character to the world and pull them into the story.

Connections: Who Do You Know?

Define your character’s relationships to other PCs, important NPCs, and factions:

Other PCs: How do you know each other? What’s your history? “We fought together in the war” or “She’s my estranged sister” or “I hired him for a job that went sideways—we’ve been watching each other’s backs ever since.”

NPCs: Who matters to you? Mentors, rivals, lovers, family, enemies. “My mentor was killed under mysterious circumstances” or “I have a rivalry with the city’s other top investigator” or “My brother works for the people I’m investigating.”

Factions: Where do you stand with organizations and power structures? “I’m on good terms with the Thieves’ Guild but owe them a favor” or “The city guard distrusts me after I exposed corruption in their ranks” or “I’m secretly working for the rebellion while maintaining cover as a loyal citizen.”

Connections create immediate story opportunities. They’re sources of information, complications, drama, and support. When you introduce a connection, you’re handing the GM a tool to create compelling scenes.

Motivations: What Drives You?

What does your character want? Not just short-term goals, but deeper drives:

  • Revenge: Someone wronged you or someone you loved. You won’t rest until accounts are settled.
  • Justice: You believe in fairness and law (official or personal). You can’t abide injustice.
  • Discovery: You need to know—secrets, truths, mysteries. Knowledge drives you.
  • Survival: You’re staying alive in a dangerous world. Everything else is secondary.
  • Wealth: Money is freedom, security, power. You pursue it relentlessly.
  • Redemption: You’re atoning for past mistakes. You need to prove you’re better than you were.
  • Protection: You’re keeping someone or something safe, whatever it costs.

Good motivations create conflict and choice. When your motivation clashes with someone else’s, drama happens. When pursuing your motivation requires sacrifice, meaningful decisions emerge.

Hooks: Why Are You Here?

Hooks answer the critical question: “Why is your character in this story, right now?” They tie you to the campaign’s opening situation and justify your involvement in the plot.

Strong hooks are immediate and compelling: - “My partner vanished investigating this case. The new murder has the same MO.” - “I’m being blackmailed to infiltrate this group. If I don’t deliver, they ruin my family.” - “I witnessed the inciting event. They know I saw. I can’t run forever.”

Weak hooks are passive or generic: - “I’m looking for adventure” (no urgency, no stakes) - “Someone hired me” (no personal investment) - “I was passing through” (no reason to stay when things get dangerous)

Work with your GM to create hooks that immediately pull you into the story. If the campaign opens with a murder, maybe you knew the victim. If it’s a heist, maybe you’re in debt to the person organizing it. If it’s a rebellion, maybe the regime killed your family.

Hooks should make it harder to leave than to stay. When things get dangerous (and they will), your hook is why you don’t walk away.

Example: Kira’s Connections and Hooks

Connections: - Missing partner (Marcus): They were investigating the Meridian crime family together. He vanished six months ago. No body, no leads, just gone. - Detective Rourke (police contact): Old friend who feeds Kira information off the books. He’s sympathetic but can’t act officially. - Voss (fixer): Dangerous contact who owes Kira a favor from past work. Calling it in comes with strings. - Other PCs: Maybe one PC is a former informant Kira protected, creating loyalty. Maybe another PC works for Meridian and doesn’t know Kira’s investigating them yet—instant tension.

Motivations: - Justice: Kira believes in finding truth and holding powerful people accountable, even when official channels fail. - Loyalty: Marcus was more than a partner—family. She won’t stop until she knows what happened to him.

Hooks: The campaign opens with a high-profile murder—a city councilman found dead in suspicious circumstances. The official investigation is moving toward “suicide,” but Kira recognizes details that match Marcus’s last case notes. She’s pulled in immediately because this murder might be the thread that leads to Marcus. She can’t not investigate, even though it puts her in danger.

Kira’s mechanically competent (high RSN, investigation skills) and narratively hooked (partner’s disappearance tied directly to the opening mystery). She’s ready to play—she has tools to contribute, relationships to leverage, and burning reasons to engage with the story.

This immediately suggests: - Investigation and information-gathering focus - Personal stakes (the missing partner) - Noir tone—moral complexity, urban danger, secrets - Likely modern or urban fantasy setting

She’s not solving problems with strength. - AGI +1: Quick reflexes, good for tailing suspects and getting out of tight spots. - RSN +2: Her primary strength—sharp analytical mind, connects clues others miss. - PRE +1: Socially competent, can talk her way into places and read people effectively.

The Complete Picture

Kira is a character built for a modern noir mystery campaign. She’s mechanically focused on investigation and social navigation (high RSN, Urban Investigation package, social proficiencies). She’s narratively driven by personal loss and justice (Marcus’s disappearance, commitment to holding powerful people accountable). She has immediate story engagement (the opening murder connects directly to her partner’s case).

Character Progression

Progression Overview

Character progression means two things: mechanical growth (new skills, proficiencies, attribute improvements) and narrative evolution (character arcs, changing motivations, deepening relationships).

Both matter. Mechanics give you new tools. Narrative gives you context for why you’ve changed and where you’re headed.

Advancement Mechanics

Your character grows through advancement opportunities tied to play. Common triggers:

  • Completing arcs: Resolve a personal story beat, achieve a major goal, or overcome a defining challenge.
  • Milestones: Reach narrative checkpoints set by the GM (end of an adventure, major turning point).
  • Session-based: Advance after a set number of sessions (every 3–4 sessions).

When you advance, you typically gain:

  • New skill: Add a skill from an adjacent package or expand your expertise.
  • New proficiency: Gain narrative permission and mechanical edge in a new area.
  • Attribute improvement: Rare; tied to major milestones or transformative arcs.

Work with your GM to choose advancements that fit your character’s story.

Narrative Arcs

Progression shines when mechanical growth reflects narrative change.

If your character learns to distrust authority after betrayal, gain a proficiency like Skeptical Investigator. If they train with a master duelist, gain Blades or improve MIG/AGI. If they uncover arcane secrets, expand into Arcane Lore.

Character arcs give progression meaning. Ask:

  • What has my character learned?
  • How have they changed?
  • What new goals or conflicts have emerged?

Mechanical advancements should answer these questions.

Milestone vs. Session-Based

Tables use different progression models:

Milestone-Based: Advance at story checkpoints (end of an arc, major victory, transformative event). Ties progression tightly to fiction. Pacing varies by campaign.

Session-Based: Advance every X sessions (typically 3–4). Predictable, ensures regular growth regardless of narrative pacing.

Your GM chooses the model that fits your campaign. Both work well. Milestones emphasize story. Session-based ensures steady growth.

Worked Example

Character: Kira, the street-smart investigator (from Chapter 05)

Arc Completed: After six sessions, Kira exposed the crime family responsible for her partner’s disappearance. She didn’t get revenge—her partner was already dead—but she brought the killers to justice and learned to trust her new crew.

Narrative Change: Kira entered the campaign distrustful and isolated. She now values her allies and has begun letting go of guilt over her partner’s death.

Advancement Choice: Kira gains the Tactical Coordination proficiency, reflecting her growth as a team player. She also adds Street Combat to her skill set after multiple dangerous confrontations. Both choices tie directly to her arc: she’s learned to fight alongside others and trust them in tight spots.

Result: Mechanically, Kira is more effective in group combat. Narratively, her character has evolved from lone wolf to team player. Both dimensions reinforce each other.

Optimization and Build Paths

For players who enjoy theorycrafting and maximizing effectiveness, this section provides frameworks for building synergistic character capabilities. You don’t need to optimize—competent characters emerge from following your narrative instincts. But if you enjoy the puzzle of capability stacking, these patterns help.

Specialization vs. Generalization

Specialists excel dramatically in one area but have gaps elsewhere:

  • Advantages: Mastery grants reliability. High skill bonuses mean consistent success on checks in your specialty. Proficiencies compound your edge. You become the go-to expert for specific challenges.
  • Disadvantages: Dependency on party support for gaps. Limited versatility when your specialty isn’t relevant. Risk of redundancy if another PC shares your focus.
  • Best for: Group play with complementary specialists. Solo play requires generalization—avoid narrow specialization if you’re the only PC.

Generalists handle diverse challenges adequately but rarely excel:

  • Advantages: Flexibility. You contribute meaningfully across combat, social, and exploration challenges. No dead weight—always have relevant skills.
  • Disadvantages: Never the best at anything. Specialists outperform you in their domain. Mediocrity in optimization-heavy tables.
  • Best for: Solo play, small groups (2-3 PCs), or tables that value versatility over mastery.

Hybrid Approach (recommended): Specialize in 2-3 areas, maintain competence in others. Example: Combat primary, social secondary, basic exploration capability. You dominate your focus while contributing elsewhere.

Skill Synergies

Certain skill combinations create compound advantages:

Investigation + Social (Detective/Interrogator): - Research (find clues) + Insight (read suspects) + Persuasion (extract confessions) - Why it works: Information gathering flows naturally from evidence to people. Each skill advances your investigative goals. - Proficiency pairing: Keen Observer (notice details) + Silver Tongue (manipulate conversations)

Combat + Athletics (Mobile Warrior): - Melee/Ranged Combat + Athletics (climb, jump, maneuver) - Why it works: Positioning is combat advantage. Athletics lets you claim high ground, leap obstacles, or pursue fleeing enemies. - Proficiency pairing: Acrobatic Fighter (move and attack) + Parkour (navigate terrain freely)

Stealth + Technical (Infiltrator): - Stealth (move unseen) + Security (bypass locks) + Subterfuge (disguise, forgery) - Why it works: Infiltration requires layered capabilities. One skill gets you in, others sustain the deception and handle obstacles. - Proficiency pairing: Shadow Walker (blend with environment) + Lock Specialist (open anything)

Social + Deception (Manipulator): - Persuasion (convince) + Deception (lie convincingly) + Insight (read motives) - Why it works: Social manipulation requires reading people, crafting believable stories, and selling them. Each skill supports the others. - Proficiency pairing: Con Artist (elaborate deceptions) + Poker Face (unreadable intentions)

Technical + Lore (Problem Solver): - Technical skills (Engineering, Medicine, etc.) + Relevant Lore (understand context) - Why it works: Technical competence without context is guesswork. Lore lets you anticipate problems and apply technical skills meaningfully. - Proficiency pairing: Engineer (build/repair) + Scholar (deep knowledge)

Build Archetypes

These templates provide optimization paths. You don’t need to follow them rigidly—use them as inspiration for synergistic capability sets.

Tactician (Combat Specialist)

Core Concept: Dominate tactical situations through positioning, terrain use, and action economy mastery.

Primary Skills: Melee or Ranged Combat, Athletics, Tactics Secondary Skills: Perception (battlefield awareness), Intimidation (control opponents) Key Proficiencies: Combat Veteran (bonus in fights), Tactical Mind (advantage on setup actions), Mobile Combatant (move and strike)

Advancement Path: 1. Early (Sessions 1-6): Establish combat dominance. Take Combat skill and Combat Veteran proficiency. 2. Mid (Sessions 7-12): Add Athletics for positioning. Take Mobile Combatant to combine movement with attacks. 3. Late (Sessions 13+): Add Tactics for team coordination. Take Tactical Mind to grant allies advantages.

Optimization Notes: Stack advantage sources—flanking + elevation + setup actions = consistent success. Focus on creating opportunities for allies, not just personal damage output. Learn enemy weaknesses through Tactics checks before engaging.

Social Manipulator (Social Specialist)

Core Concept: Control social situations through persuasion, deception, and information gathering.

Primary Skills: Persuasion, Deception, Insight Secondary Skills: Relevant Lore (context for manipulation), Streetwise (navigate social networks) Key Proficiencies: Silver Tongue (bonus on social checks), Poker Face (hide intentions), Network (contacts everywhere)

Advancement Path: 1. Early (Sessions 1-6): Establish social dominance. Take Persuasion and Silver Tongue. 2. Mid (Sessions 7-12): Add Deception for flexibility. Take Poker Face to prevent Insight checks against you. 3. Late (Sessions 13+): Add Insight to read opponents. Take Network for access to information and resources.

Optimization Notes: Multi-round social contests favor you—each round builds advantage. Leverage NPC traits (Greedy, Honorable) by framing offers to align with their values. Use Insight before negotiating to identify leverage points.

Technical Specialist (Problem Solver)

Core Concept: Handle technical challenges, crafting, and specialized knowledge.

Primary Skills: Engineering, Medicine, or Science (choose 2-3 technical domains) Secondary Skills: Relevant Lore, Perception (notice technical details) Key Proficiencies: Engineer (build/repair), Field Medic (stabilize injuries), Inventor (create solutions)

Advancement Path: 1. Early (Sessions 1-6): Establish technical mastery. Take 2 technical skills and Engineer. 2. Mid (Sessions 7-12): Add Lore for context. Take Inventor to create custom solutions. 3. Late (Sessions 13+): Add third technical domain or broaden to investigation skills. Take Master Craftsman for exceptional quality.

Optimization Notes: Technical specialists shine in preparation. Use downtime to craft gear, research problems, or prototype solutions. Pre-game planning lets you bring the right tool for anticipated challenges.

Avoiding Common Pitfalls

Over-Specialization: Taking only combat skills leaves you useless in social or exploration scenes. Aim for at least 2 capability domains (e.g., combat + social, or investigation + technical).

Redundancy: If another PC is the party’s face, adding Persuasion as your primary creates overlap without adding value. Choose complementary capabilities instead.

Neglecting Narrative: Optimization without character integration feels hollow. Why does your character have these skills? How do they reflect who they are? Mechanics should support story, not replace it.

Chasing Perfection: “Optimal” builds assume perfect information and controlled conditions. Real play is messy. Competent, flexible characters outperform hyper-specialized optimizations when plans go sideways.

Actions & Checks

When to Roll

You roll when the GM calls for a check—when your action has uncertain outcomes and meaningful stakes. If you’re competent with time and safety, the GM might call it auto-success. If failure would stall play, the GM might offer success with cost (Partial) or reframe failure to keep momentum.

Ask yourself: “Could this go multiple ways?” and “Does it matter now?” If yes to both, expect a roll. If no, the GM narrates forward.

When to Roll (and When Not To) At-a-Glance

The GM (Game Master—the person who facilitates the game and describes the world) calls for rolls when three criteria align:

1. Uncertain Outcome: Success isn’t guaranteed. A trained locksmith opening a simple door doesn’t roll. A thief picking a complex lock under pressure does.

2. Consequential Stakes: Failure introduces complications, costs, or escalation. If failure just means “try again,” don’t roll—either it succeeds with time or it’s impossible.

3. Player Agency: The character can meaningfully influence the result. Don’t roll for things completely outside control (like whether a distant NPC makes a decision).

Examples of When TO Roll:

  • Sneaking past alert guards (uncertain, you could be spotted; consequential, detection changes everything; agency, your approach matters)
  • Convincing a skeptical merchant to extend credit (uncertain, she’s cautious; consequential, failure means finding another source; agency, your persuasion matters)
  • Climbing a crumbling wall during a storm (uncertain, conditions are poor; consequential, falling causes injury and delays; agency, your technique matters)

Examples of When NOT to Roll:

  • An expert chef making simple bread (certain for their skill level, no stakes)
  • Searching a room when you have unlimited time and no threats (inevitable success, no pressure)
  • Whether a random NPC (Non-Player Character—controlled by the GM) arrives at a location (no player agency)

When all three criteria are met, roll a check: 4d6 (four six-sided dice) + modifiers vs DC (Difficulty Class—target number to meet or exceed). Otherwise, the GM narrates forward without dice.

For complete when-to-roll guidance: See Mechanics Reference, Chapter 3 (Check Resolution).

Declaring Actions

When you declare an action, state two things:

Intent: What you’re trying to accomplish. “I climb the wall” or “I convince the captain to let us pass.”

Approach: How you’re doing it. “Using handholds, moving quietly” or “Appealing to her duty, offering coin.”

The GM uses intent and approach to pick the DC, determine which skill or proficiency applies, and decide on Advantage or Disadvantage. Be specific—your approach shapes fiction and mechanics. If the GM asks for clarification, they’re helping you find the best angle or exposing risks.

Advantage & Disadvantage

The fiction grants mechanical shifts through advantage and disadvantage. Instead of adjusting the DC, the game modifies your dice pool to represent favorable or unfavorable circumstances.

Advantage means you roll extra dice and keep the best 4: - +1 Advantage: Roll 5d6, keep the best 4 dice - +2 Advantage: Roll 6d6, keep the best 4 dice

Disadvantage means you roll extra dice and keep the worst 4: - -1 Disadvantage: Roll 5d6, keep the worst 4 dice - -2 Disadvantage: Roll 6d6, keep the worst 4 dice

You gain Advantage when:

  • You have superior positioning (high ground, cover, surprise, flanking)
  • You’re using expert tools or specialized gear
  • An ally is actively helping you (and the GM agrees their help is meaningful)
  • The environment favors your approach (quiet space for stealth, good lighting for searching, stable footing for athletics)
  • You took a setup action first (scouted ahead, cleared obstacles, coordinated with allies)

You have Disadvantage when:

  • Positioning works against you (cramped space, unstable footing, exposed position)
  • You lack proper tools or are improvising with unsuitable equipment
  • You’re rushed, distracted, or under significant pressure
  • The environment hinders you (dim light for precise work, loud noise for stealth, rough terrain for movement)
  • You have a relevant condition (Exhausted, Dazed, Bleeding, Frightened)

Important: Advantage and disadvantage come in levels (±1 or ±2), but they cap at those levels. Multiple sources of +1 advantage don’t stack to become +2—you need a particularly strong source for +2. If you have both advantage and disadvantage, they cancel out—net them (e.g., +1 advantage and -1 disadvantage = roll normally with 4d6; +2 advantage and -1 disadvantage = +1 advantage, roll 5d6 keep best 4).

You can create Advantage by taking a setup action first: scouting ahead, clearing obstacles, or coordinating with allies. This is often tactically smart—spend one turn creating advantage for a critical roll.

Advantage & Disadvantage Quick Reference

Core Rule: They Net Out at ±2 Levels

Advantage and disadvantage come in two levels each: - +1 Advantage: Roll 5d6, keep best 4 - +2 Advantage: Roll 6d6, keep best 4 - -1 Disadvantage: Roll 5d6, keep worst 4 - -2 Disadvantage: Roll 6d6, keep worst 4

Multiple sources of the same type don’t stack beyond these levels. You either have +1 advantage, +2 advantage, normal, -1 disadvantage, or -2 disadvantage.

Competing Sources Cancel Out

When you have both advantage and disadvantage from different sources, they cancel partially or completely: - +1 Advantage + -1 Disadvantage = Roll normally (4d6) - +2 Advantage + -1 Disadvantage = +1 Advantage (5d6, keep best 4) - +1 Advantage + -2 Disadvantage = -1 Disadvantage (5d6, keep worst 4) - +2 Advantage + -2 Disadvantage = Roll normally (4d6)

Example:

You’re shooting at an enemy from high ground (Elevated tag grants +1 Advantage) but visibility is poor (Dim Light tag imposes -1 Disadvantage). The two effects cancel completely—you roll 4d6 normally.

Common Sources of Advantage:

  • Superior positioning: High ground, cover for ranged attacks, flanking in melee
  • Specialized tools or gear: Masterwork lockpicks, precision instruments, quality climbing equipment
  • Ally actively helping: Someone distracts the guard while you sneak, holds the rope while you climb
  • Environmental favor: Good lighting for detailed work, stable footing for athletics, quiet space for stealth
  • Setup actions: You scouted the route first, you cleared obstacles beforehand, you coordinated signals with allies

Common Sources of Disadvantage:

  • Poor positioning: Cramped quarters, unstable footing, no cover while exposed
  • Improvised tools: Using a dagger as a pry bar, rope instead of proper climbing gear
  • Time pressure or distraction: Guards approaching, building collapsing, ally calling for help
  • Environmental hindrance: Dim light for ranged attacks, rough terrain for movement, loud noise for stealth
  • Conditions: Exhausted (physical actions), Dazed (perception and reactions), Frightened (actions while threat visible), Bleeding (strenuous activity)

Edge Case: Multiple Sources of Same Type

Even if you have three sources of +1 advantage (high ground, expert tools, and an ally helping), you still only get +1 advantage—roll 5d6, keep best 4. Advantage doesn’t compound beyond ±2 levels.

Design Philosophy: Why Advantage Caps at ±2

Advantage and disadvantage are capped at ±2 levels for a crucial design reason: bounded accuracy. The system is built on a predictable range of outcomes (rolling 4d6, average ~14, range 4-24). If advantage stacked without limit, characters could engineer guaranteed success by layering multiple benefits, breaking the tension that makes rolls meaningful.

Capping at ±2 preserves meaningful risk. Even with every advantage (+2 advantage = 6d6 keep best 4, average ~17), you can still roll badly. Even with significant disadvantage (-2 disadvantage = 6d6 keep worst 4, average ~11), you can still succeed through luck or player resourcefulness. The dice still matter.

This cap also prevents “advantage farming”—the temptation to delay action while stacking every possible benefit. The game rewards smart positioning and preparation (setup actions grant advantage), but doesn’t let you optimize away all risk. Competence improves your odds; it doesn’t eliminate failure.

In tactical terms: Get advantage when you can, but don’t chase perfection. +1 advantage (~+1.5 to average roll) is meaningful. +2 advantage (~+3 to average roll) is strong. But you’ll never reach a point where rolls become trivial. The fiction remains dynamic, and outcomes stay uncertain.

To get +2 advantage, you typically need an exceptionally favorable situation or a particularly powerful source (magic, special abilities, or the GM judging the circumstances are overwhelmingly in your favor).

Common Misconception: “If I have two advantages and one disadvantage, do I still get advantage?”

It depends on the levels. If you have +1 advantage from two sources (which doesn’t stack, so it’s just +1) and -1 disadvantage from one source, they cancel out—you roll normally (4d6).

If you have +2 advantage from an exceptional circumstance and -1 disadvantage from a condition, you net to +1 advantage (roll 5d6, keep best 4).

Always net out advantage and disadvantage levels before rolling.

For complete Advantage/Disadvantage mechanics: See How to Read the Rules (Chapter 2) and Mechanics Reference, Chapter 2 (Rolling Dice).

Reading Results

Compare your total (4d6 + modifiers) to the DC. Calculate your margin (Roll - DC). The margin determines your outcome tier and tells you what changed.

Critical Success (Margin ≥+5): You achieve your intent spectacularly with extra benefit—extra information, improved position, a cleared obstacle, or narrative advantage.

Full Success (Margin ≥0): You achieve your intent cleanly. The situation shifts in your favor—progress ticks, obstacles clear, or you gain position.

Partial Success (Margin -1 to -2): You achieve your intent with a complication. The GM pre-stated this: a cost, trade-off, or wrinkle. You moved forward, but so did pressure or danger.

Failure (Margin ≤-3): You don’t achieve your intent. The situation escalates or reveals a problem. The GM describes what changed—guards heard you, the ledge crumbles, the NPC shuts down. Failure changes what happens next.

Critical Failure (Margin ≤-7 or all 1s): A disaster occurs—the worst possible result with major complications. Things break, attention is drawn, or dangerous consequences land immediately.

Ask yourself: “What’s different now?” Results always shift the fiction.

Group Actions

When the party works together, the GM picks one:

Group Check (Majority): Everyone rolls. If most succeed, the group succeeds. If split, treat as Partial. Use when each contributes independently (sneaking, searching).

Group Check (All‑Must‑Succeed): Everyone rolls; a single failure breaks the attempt. Use when chained together (fragile bridge, coordinated ritual).

Helping: If not rolling but assisting, describe how. The GM might grant Advantage to the lead character.

Contests: Both sides roll. Higher total wins. The GM pre-states outcomes for each side (chase, debate, arm-wrestling).

Group Checks Quick Reference

When multiple PCs (Player Characters—the characters you control) collaborate on a shared goal, use group checks to streamline resolution and share risk.

How Group Checks Work:

  1. Identify the leader—the character most qualified or driving the effort.
  2. The leader rolls the check against the DC (4d6 + modifiers).
  3. Assistants provide Advantage by contributing meaningfully. Each assistant describes how they help. If their contribution makes sense, the GM grants advantage levels (usually +1, sometimes +2 if the help is exceptional).
  4. Everyone shares the consequences. If the check results in a Partial or Failure, assistants take on costs too.

Example: Climbing a Cliff

Your party climbs a steep cliff to reach a fortress. The GM sets DC 16 (Tough—rocky, unstable handholds). The character with highest MIG or AGI leads. Another character with rope and climbing experience describes securing anchors and guiding the route—the GM grants +1 Advantage. The leader rolls 5d6 (keep best 4) + modifiers.

  • Critical Success (21+, margin ≥+5): Everyone reaches the top safely, and you find a hidden ledge that provides a perfect vantage point for scouting the fortress.
  • Full Success (16-20, margin ≥0): Everyone reaches the top safely. No complications.
  • Partial Success (14-15, margin -1 to -2): Everyone makes it, but the rope frays badly. It’ll break if used again without repair.
  • Failure (11-13, margin ≤-3): The leader slips. Everyone stops to stabilize them, losing time. Pressure clock ticks (guards patrol closer).
  • Critical Failure (≤10 or all 1s): The leader falls, dragging others down. Everyone takes damage and makes noise—guards are alerted immediately.

Key Points:

  • Only those who can meaningfully contribute should assist. Don’t let the whole party help with a Hacking check if only one character has the skill.
  • Assistants share the risk. If the check fails or goes Partial, everyone faces consequences (not just the leader).
  • The leader’s skill determines the roll, but assistance grants advantage levels (not multiple rolls).

When to Use Group Checks:

Use them for collaborative efforts where everyone contributes: climbing, sneaking, searching, coordinating a distraction, or executing a plan together. Don’t use them when characters have truly independent goals—those require separate rolls.

For complete group check mechanics: See Mechanics Reference, Chapter 3 (Group Checks).

Worked Examples

Example 1: Exploration (Climbing a Wall)

You: “I want to climb the outer wall to reach the second-floor window.”

GM: “How are you doing it?”

You: “I’m using the stonework gaps as handholds and moving slowly to stay quiet.”

GM: “DC 16—it’s steep and the stone is old. Partial means you make it but dislodge rubble and alert the guards inside. Roll AGI.”

You roll 4d6: 3, 5, 4, 6 = 18. Add your +3 AGI bonus = 21 total. Margin: +5 (Critical Success).

GM: “You scale the wall smoothly, finding solid grips with practiced ease. You reach the window ledge without a sound, and as you peer inside, you spot a guard’s patrol schedule pinned to the wall—valuable intel. You’re in position with extra information.”

Example 2: Social (Persuading a Guard)

You: “I want to convince the guard to let us into the restricted wing.”

GM: “What’s your approach?”

You: “I’m appealing to her sense of duty, saying we’re investigating threats to the city and need access now.”

GM: “She’s skeptical but not hostile. DC 14. Partial means she lets you in but assigns an escort who’ll watch you. Roll PRE.”

You roll 4d6: 2, 3, 3, 4 = 12. Add your +1 PRE bonus = 13 total. Margin: -1 (Partial Success).

GM: “She narrows her eyes, then nods slowly. ‘Fine. But Corporal Vance goes with you. Don’t try anything.’ She whistles, and a stern-looking soldier appears. You’re in, but you have a suspicious escort watching your every move.”

Example 3: Using Advantage

You: “Before I try to pick the lock, I want to examine it carefully to understand its mechanism.”

GM: “Smart. Roll Investigation, DC 14.”

You roll and succeed.

GM: “You notice it’s a three-pin tumbler, and the third pin sticks slightly—you can use that. When you pick it, you’ll have +1 advantage.”

You: “Great. Now I’m picking the lock, using what I learned.”

GM: “DC 16 to pick it quietly. Roll 5d6, keep the best 4, add your Thievery bonus.”

You roll 5d6: 2, 3, 5, 4, 6. Keep best 4: 3, 4, 5, 6 = 18. Add your +2 Thievery = 20 total. Margin: +4 (Full Success).

GM: “The lock clicks open smoothly, the sticky pin giving you perfect feedback. You ease the door open—no sound, no alert. You’re in.”

The setup action created advantage, which significantly improved your chances and turned what could have been risky into a smooth success.

Combat Basics

Combat Overview

Combat starts when violence erupts and everyone’s choices matter moment to moment. The GM calls for initiative when the fiction demands it—usually when surprise breaks, threats emerge, or negotiation fails. Combat uses the same core mechanics you know (DCs, outcome tiers, tags, clocks), but with tighter focus on positioning, immediate danger, and tactical choices.

No Hit Points: Resolve and Clocks

This system doesn’t track hit points. Instead, the GM uses resolve clocks and conditions to represent how much punishment characters and enemies can take before they’re taken out of the fight.

Resolve Clocks are segmented progress trackers (typically 4-6 segments for enemies, more for major threats). When you land a successful Strike or create pressure, the GM ticks segments on the enemy’s resolve clock. When the clock fills completely, they’re out—unconscious, fleeing, surrendering, or dead, depending on the fiction.

Why Clocks Instead of HP?

  • Fiction-First: Characters are “taken out” when it makes narrative sense, not at an arbitrary number. A skilled duelist might fold after 4 solid hits, while a raging beast takes 8.
  • Visible Progress: You see the clock filling and know you’re wearing them down. No hidden HP pools—the GM describes their state (“staggering,” “defensive,” “desperate”) as segments fill.
  • Flexible Stakes: The GM can adjust clock size on the fly based on pacing. Minor enemies get smaller clocks (2-3 segments), major bosses get larger ones (6-8 segments).

What You Do: Focus on the fiction. Describe your attacks and tactics. The GM handles the clock math behind the screen. When you land hits or create pressure, you’ll see progress. When the clock fills, they’re done.

Your Resolve: The GM tracks your resolve similarly. Taking damage, failing checks, or suffering consequences ticks your resolve. When your clock fills, you’re taken out—you might be unconscious, captured, or forced to retreat. The GM will warn you when you’re in danger (“You’re hurt bad—two more solid hits and you’re out”). Use Defend/Withdraw to reset position and buy time to recover.

Order & Your Turn

The GM picks an ordering mode based on the scene:

Conversational: The GM asks “Who acts?” and rotates spotlight. Natural for small skirmishes or when surprise isn’t a factor.

Popcorn: You act, then name who goes next (alternate sides). Prevents one side from dominating. The GM might say: “You’re up, then pick who acts next.”

On your turn, you describe your action (Strike, Maneuver, Set Up, Defend/Withdraw), the GM sets the DC and pre-states outcomes, you roll, and the GM narrates the result. Then the next person acts.

Core Actions

Strike: Attack an opponent. Roll contest or passive DC. On Full, deal damage or apply pressure. On Partial, connect but take a cost (exposed, pressure ticks). On Fail, they counter or you lose position.

Tactical Notes: Strike is your most direct option but not always optimal. Against tough enemies (high DC), consider Set Up first to create Advantage, then Strike with better odds. Against multiple weaker foes, Strike to eliminate threats fast. Partial Success on Strike often means trading blows—viable if you’re tougher than they are, risky if you’re already wounded.

Maneuver: Shove, trip, disarm, reposition. On Full, achieve your goal (they’re prone, disarmed, moved). On Partial, trade something (you go prone too, lose grip). Use to create openings or control space.

Tactical Notes: Maneuver shines when positioning matters more than damage. Shove an enemy off high ground (removing their Advantage). Trip a charging brute (apply Prone, making them vulnerable). Disarm a gunman in close quarters (forcing them to draw a backup weapon or flee). Maneuver changes the battlefield—use it when the situation needs changing, not when you just need to hurt someone.

Set Up: Create advantage for yourself or allies—cover fire, feint, flank. On Full, grant Advantage or apply a helpful tag. On Partial, create advantage but expose yourself or tick pressure.

Tactical Notes: Set Up is the team player’s action. Flanking an enemy grants Advantage to everyone attacking them. Suppressing fire pins down a sniper while allies advance. Feinting draws attention, letting your Rogue slip behind for a backstab. Set Up sacrifices your immediate impact for greater team efficiency. Best used when: (1) your Strike would be low odds, (2) an ally can capitalize better than you, or (3) you need to control enemy actions without committing to combat.

Defend/Withdraw: Change position to safety, clear a condition, or break contact. On Full, reposition cleanly and clear a minor tag (Suppressed, Exposed). On Partial, move but give up your next action. Use when you need breathing room.

Tactical Notes: Defend/Withdraw is not cowardice—it’s smart positioning. Retreat to cover when you’re Exposed. Fall back to force enemies through a chokepoint. Clear Suppressed so you can act freely next round.

Design Philosophy: Why Action Economy Matters

Combat limits you to one action per turn for a crucial design reason: meaningful choices under pressure. If you could Strike AND Maneuver AND Set Up every turn, combat would become a rote optimization puzzle—“do all the best things in sequence.” Limiting actions forces you to prioritize: What matters most right now?

This creates tactical tension. Do you Strike the wounded enemy to finish them, or Set Up to help your ally against the bigger threat? Do you Maneuver to high ground for long-term advantage, or Strike while you have the opening? Every choice means sacrificing alternatives.

Action economy also balances numerical advantage. Three enemies don’t just have more total capabilities—they get three actions per round to your one. This makes outnumbering dangerous and forces players to use terrain, positioning, and coordination to offset numerical disadvantage. It’s not “you’re outnumbered so you lose”—it’s “you’re outnumbered, so you need to be smarter.”

From a pacing perspective, limiting actions keeps combat dynamic. Fights resolve in 3-6 rounds typically, not 10+ slugfests. Each round matters because you can only do one thing. There’s no downtime between significant choices—every turn is a decision point.

In tactical terms: Action economy favors efficiency. Eliminate threats fast (Strike). Create lasting advantages (Maneuver/Set Up). Don’t waste turns on low-impact actions when the clock is ticking. Your action is your most valuable resource each round—spend it wisely.

If you’re outnumbered or wounded, resetting position is more valuable than one more desperate Strike. Know when to disengage and live to fight another round.

Using Terrain & Tags

Environmental tags shape your options:

Cover (Fragile vs Solid): Fragile (crates, thin walls) grants Advantage but degrades on Partial. Solid (stone, thick barriers) is reliable. Use Set Up to create or improve cover.

Elevation: High ground grants Advantage on ranged attacks and forces opponents to climb to reach you.

Dim/Obscuring Light: Hinders vision. Grants Disadvantage to ranged attacks but Advantage to stealth. Use to slip away or reposition.

Cramped/Slick Terrain: Cramped spaces make movement risky. Slick surfaces impose Disadvantage on maneuvers. The GM names tags; you decide how to use or avoid them.

Common Combat Tags At-a-Glance

These tags appear frequently in combat. Understanding their effects helps you make quick tactical decisions.

Cover Tags:

  • Fragile Cover (wooden crates, thin walls): Grants +1 Advantage on defense checks. Degrades on Partial Success (becomes no cover). Can be destroyed by focused attacks or environmental effects.
  • Solid Cover (stone walls, thick barriers): Grants +1 Advantage on defense checks. Does not degrade. Can only be removed by extreme force or repositioning.

Positioning Tags:

  • Elevated (high ground, rooftop, platform): Grants +1 Advantage on ranged attacks against lower targets. Forces opponents to climb (DC 14 Maneuver) to reach you, exposing them during ascent.
  • Flanked (surrounded, caught between enemies): Grants +1 Advantage to attackers positioned on opposite sides. Created by coordinated Set Up actions.
  • Exposed (caught in open, off-balance): Grants +1 Disadvantage on defense checks. Applied by Partial Success costs or failed positioning. Cleared by Defend/Withdraw or reaching cover.

Environmental Tags:

  • Dim Light (shadows, fog, smoke): Grants +1 Disadvantage to ranged attacks and perception checks. Grants +1 Advantage to stealth and evasion. Can be created (smoke grenades, kick up dust) or used (existing conditions).
  • Cramped (narrow corridor, dense forest): Restricts movement and weapon use. Larger weapons (two-handed, polearms) may suffer +1 Disadvantage. Grappling and close-quarters attacks gain +1 Advantage.
  • Slick (oil, ice, wet stone): Grants +1 Disadvantage on Maneuver checks (shove, trip, reposition). Failed checks result in Prone condition. Can be created intentionally (spill oil, freeze water).

Condition Tags:

  • Prone (knocked down, fallen): Grants +1 Disadvantage on defense and attack checks. Standing costs your action. Ranged attackers shooting at prone targets suffer +1 Disadvantage (harder to hit low targets).
  • Suppressed (pinned by fire, intimidated): Grants +1 Disadvantage on offensive actions. Created by Set Up actions (covering fire, threatening presence). Cleared by breaking line of sight or eliminating the suppressor.
  • Stunned (dazed, off-balance): Skip next action entirely. Applied by critical effects (flash grenades, head trauma, falling damage). Severe penalty requiring time or aid to clear.

Using Tags Tactically:

  • Create advantage: Use Set Up to apply beneficial tags (Flanked, Suppressed) to enemies or environment (Slick, Dim Light).
  • Deny enemy advantage: Use Maneuver to remove their cover or reposition away from Flanked situations.
  • Stack effects: Combine tags for multiple sources of Advantage (e.g., Elevated + Flanked = +2 Advantage).

Damage & Resolve

As explained in Combat Overview, the GM tracks damage using resolve clocks rather than hit points. This section focuses on conditions—persistent states that impose penalties until cleared.

Conditions represent lasting effects from combat: injury, exhaustion, or impairment. Unlike resolve clock progress (which the GM tracks), conditions are visible to everyone and impose mechanical penalties.

Common Combat Conditions:

  • Bleeding (injury, open wound): Take 1 pressure at the start of each round until stabilized. Cleared by first aid (DC 14 check) or magical healing. Multiple bleeding wounds stack—each ticks pressure separately.

  • Exhausted (fatigue, overexertion): Suffer -1 Disadvantage on physical checks (Strength, Agility). Cleared by rest (short rest clears 1 level, long rest clears all). Exhaustion stacks—multiple levels impose cumulative penalties.

  • Stunned (dazed, disoriented): Skip your next action entirely. Applied by critical effects (head trauma, flash grenades, sonic attacks). Cleared automatically after 1 round or by ally assistance (DC 14 check).

Clearing Conditions: Conditions require specific actions to clear. The GM will tell you what’s needed—first aid for Bleeding, rest for Exhausted, time or assistance for Stunned. Multiple conditions stack, making continued fighting dangerous. If you’re Bleeding and Exhausted, you’re ticking pressure every round AND rolling with Disadvantage. Use Defend/Withdraw to break contact and get help.

Fiction vs Mechanics: The GM describes your state based on resolve clock progress and conditions. “You’re staggering, bleeding from a gash on your arm (Bleeding), and you can barely keep your sword up (Exhausted).” Focus on the fiction—the mechanics support the narrative, not the other way around.

Worked Example

Scene: You’re in an alley (Dim Light, Cramped). Two thugs block the exit. You’re behind Fragile Cover (stacked crates).

Turn 1 (You):

You: “I use Set Up—I’m laying down cover fire to pin the front thug.”

GM: “DC 14. Full means you Suppress him (Disadvantage on his next action). Partial means you Suppress him but degrade your Fragile cover. Roll Agility.”

You roll 16—Full.

GM: “You fire twice, forcing him to duck. He’s Suppressed. Next?”

Turn 2 (Thug):

GM: “The Suppressed thug tries to advance anyway. He rolls with Disadvantage—fails. He stays pinned. The second thug circles left, trying to flank you. DC 16 to intercept with a Maneuver.”

You roll 14—Fail.

GM: “He closes the gap. He’s next to you now—your cover’s useless at this range. You lose Advantage, and he’s in position to Strike.”

Turn 3 (You):

You: “I use Defend/Withdraw—I shove past him and fall back to the solid wall at the alley mouth.”

GM: “DC 14. Full means you break clean. Partial means you get there but he grabs your coat—pressure ticks. Roll Strength or Agility.”

You roll 17—Full.

GM: “You twist free and sprint back. You’re at the wall, solid cover, and both thugs have to cross open ground to reach you. What now?”

The example shows Set Up creating advantage, a failed Maneuver costing position, and Defend/Withdraw resetting the scene. Tags (Dim, Cramped, Fragile/Solid cover) shaped every choice.

Advanced Combat Scenarios

These scenarios demonstrate tactical depth: multi-round positioning, using terrain creatively, and handling multiple opponents. Each shows both competent play (achieving goals) and optimization opportunities (maximizing efficiency).

Scenario 1: Rooftop Standoff (Multi-Round Tactical Play)

Setup: You and two allies face three guards on a flat rooftop. Crates (Fragile Cover) are scattered. The roof edge is 20 feet away (Fall Risk).

Round 1—Establishing Position:

Ally 1 (Ranger): “I use Set Up—moving to flank the left guard.”

GM: “DC 14. Full means you get there and grant Advantage to anyone attacking him. Partial means you expose yourself getting there—you’re Exposed (Disadvantage on defense). Roll Agility.”

Ally 1 rolls 18—Full.

GM: “You circle wide. Anyone attacking that guard has Advantage now.”

Guard 1: “I Strike the Ranger with my crossbow.”

GM (rolling for guard): “He rolls 12 vs your DC 14—Fail. The bolt goes wide. You’re safe.”

You (Fighter): “I Strike the center guard while he’s reloading.”

GM: “DC 16—he’s alert. Full deals 2 pressure, Partial 1 pressure but you’re Exposed. Roll Strength.”

You roll 19—Full.

GM: “You land a solid hit. He staggers back, 2 pressure. His clock is half full now.”

Round 2—Pressing the Advantage:

Ally 2 (Rogue): “I Strike the left guard—the one who’s flanked.”

GM: “You have Advantage from the Ranger’s flank. DC 14. Full is 2 pressure, Partial 1 pressure and you’re Exposed.”

Ally 2 rolls 21 (with Advantage)—Full.

GM: “Your blade finds the gap. He drops—out of the fight. Two guards left.”

Guard 2 (center): “I use Defend/Withdraw—falling back to Solid Cover behind the rooftop door housing.”

GM: “He breaks contact cleanly. He’s behind solid cover now—you’ll need to close or flush him out.”

You: “I advance on him, using Maneuver to kick the crates in his way.”

GM: “DC 16—you’re trying to deny his cover. Full means you scatter the crates and he loses cover. Partial means you move them but expose yourself.”

You roll 15—Partial.

GM: “You kick the crates aside, but he fires as you move. You’re Exposed and take 1 pressure.”

Round 3—Finishing the Fight:

Ally 1 (Ranger): “I use Set Up—covering fire on the last guard to pin him.”

GM: “DC 14. Full Suppresses him (Disadvantage). Partial Suppresses him but you’re out of arrows after this.”

Ally 1 rolls 17—Full.

GM: “Your arrows force him flat. He’s Suppressed.”

You: “I rush him while he’s pinned—Strike with my sword.”

GM: “He’s Suppressed so you have Advantage. DC 14. Full drops him, Partial deals 2 pressure.”

You roll 18 (with Advantage)—Full.

GM: “You close fast and strike hard. He goes down. Fight’s over.”

Tactical Breakdown:

  • Round 1: Positioning mattered. Flanking created Advantage, aggressive Strike built pressure.
  • Round 2: Used Advantage immediately (Rogue’s Strike). Guard adapted by retreating to better cover. Fighter pressed but paid a cost (Exposed).
  • Round 3: Combined Set Up (Suppress) with Strike to finish efficiently.

Optimization Notes: Ally 2 could have used Set Up in Round 1 to create even more Advantage for Round 2, but the aggressive Strike ended the threat faster. Both approaches work—it depends on your risk tolerance.

Scenario 2: The High Ground (Elevation & Positioning)

Setup: You’re on a stone platform (Elevated, Solid Cover). Two archers are below in the courtyard (Open Ground). One tries to climb up.

Turn 1 (You):

You: “I Strike the climber with my bow while he’s exposed on the ladder.”

GM: “You have Advantage (Elevation + he’s climbing). DC 12. Full deals 2 pressure and knocks him off. Partial deals 1 pressure but he keeps climbing.”

You roll 19 (with Advantage)—Full.

GM: “Your arrow catches him in the shoulder. He loses grip and falls 10 feet—he’s Stunned and takes 2 pressure. He’s out for a round.”

Turn 2 (Archer 1):

GM: “The other archer shoots at you from below. He has Disadvantage (Elevation + your Solid Cover). He rolls 10 vs your DC 14—Fail. His arrow clatters off the stone.”

Turn 3 (You):

You: “I Strike the standing archer.”

GM: “Advantage from Elevation. DC 14. Full is 2 pressure, Partial 1 pressure.”

You roll 16 (with Advantage)—Full.

GM: “Clean hit. He’s hurt bad—2 pressure, clock nearly full.”

Turn 4 (Archer 1):

GM: “He uses Defend/Withdraw—running for the archway (Solid Cover). DC 14 to intercept with a shot.”

You roll 12—Fail.

GM: “He makes it. He’s behind cover now, and the climber is getting up.”

Tactical Breakdown:

  • Elevation gave consistent Advantage, making every shot easier.
  • Solid Cover protected you from return fire (enemy Disadvantage).
  • Falling damage (from knocking climber off) turned one threat into a non-factor for multiple rounds.
  • Positioning let you control the fight—they had to come to you, and doing so exposed them.

Optimization Notes: You could have used Set Up on Turn 3 to Suppress the archer (preventing his retreat) instead of dealing damage. That’s the tradeoff: immediate pressure vs denying enemy actions.

Scenario 3: Outnumbered (Three vs One)

Setup: You’re alone in a workshop. Three thugs close in. The room has Cramped spaces (workbenches), a Slick oil spill, and a door (Exit) 15 feet away.

Turn 1 (You):

You: “I use Set Up—kicking over the lantern to spread fire and create chaos.”

GM: “DC 14. Full creates Burning terrain (Disadvantage to anyone crossing it, 1 pressure per round if you stay). Partial creates Smoke (obscures vision, Disadvantage to ranged). Roll Agility.”

You roll 16—Full.

GM: “The oil ignites. There’s a wall of fire between you and two of them now. They’ll have to go around or risk crossing. The third thug is on your side of the fire.”

Turn 2 (Thug 1, on your side):

GM: “He rushes you—Strike with a club. You’re in Cramped terrain. He rolls 15 vs your DC 14—Partial Success for him.”

GM: “He swings wild, connects but stumbles into a workbench. You both take 1 pressure.”

Turn 3 (You):

You: “I use Maneuver—shove him into the Slick oil spill.”

GM: “DC 14. Full knocks him Prone in the oil (Disadvantage on his next action). Partial moves him there but you lose balance—Exposed.”

You roll 17—Full.

GM: “You heave him backward. He goes down hard in the slick, struggling to stand. He’s Prone.”

Turn 4 (Thug 2 & 3, across fire):

GM: “One tries to circle left around the fire. The other tries to leap it. Leaping is DC 16 or he takes 2 pressure and ends Prone on your side.”

GM (rolling for Thug 3): “He rolls 13—Fail. He stumbles into the flames, takes 2 pressure, and lands Prone at your feet. Thug 2 is still circling.”

Turn 5 (You):

You: “I use Defend/Withdraw—I sprint for the door and bolt it behind me.”

GM: “DC 14. Full means you get out clean. Partial means you get out but one follows you through.”

You roll 18—Full.

GM: “You’re out, door slammed and barred. They’re trapped in a burning room. You win.”

Tactical Breakdown:

  • Set Up (creating Burning terrain) split the enemies, turning 3v1 into 1v1 temporarily.
  • Maneuver (shove into Slick terrain) neutralized the immediate threat (Prone = Disadvantage).
  • Defend/Withdraw (escape) was the win condition—you didn’t need to fight, just survive and leave.
  • Environmental tags (Cramped, Slick, Burning) were more effective than direct combat.

Optimization Notes: You could have used Strike to finish Thug 1 while he was Prone, but escaping was safer and faster. Against overwhelming odds, changing the situation (environment, escape) beats standing and fighting.

Exploration & Social Play

Exploration Overview

Exploration covers everything outside combat: investigating crime scenes, navigating dangerous terrain, deciphering ancient texts, picking locks, tracking suspects, or solving puzzles. You use the same mechanics—DCs, outcome tiers, tags, clocks—but the focus shifts from immediate violence to problem-solving, discovery, and momentum building. The GM frames stakes, you declare actions, you roll, and the fiction moves forward.

Investigation & Discovery

When investigating, describe what you’re doing and seeking. “I search the desk for hidden compartments” or “I interview witnesses for inconsistencies.”

The GM sets a DC based on how well-hidden or complex the information is. On Full, you find clear evidence or breakthrough. On Partial, you find something useful with a complication—time passes, someone notices, clue incomplete. On Fail, learn nothing new or trigger a complication.

Progress Clocks track investigation goals: “Identify Culprit” or “Decode Ritual.” Successful checks tick progress. Fill the clock, achieve breakthrough. Pressure Clocks track danger: “Guards Return” or “Evidence Destroyed.” Partial/Fail tick pressure, raising stakes.

Use relevant skills—Perception for details, Research for lore, Insight for reading people. The GM might grant Advantage for expert tools, contacts, or prep time.

Social Interaction

Social scenes—persuasion, deception, negotiation—use the same structure. Declare intent and approach. “I convince the merchant to lower his price by appealing to shared history” or “I deceive the guard by forging confidence.”

Persuasion: On Full, change their mind or gain trust. On Partial, they’re swayed but want something in return. On Fail, they reject or grow suspicious.

Deception: On Full, they believe you. On Partial, they believe but doubt a detail or ask follow-ups. On Fail, they see through the lie.

Reading NPCs: Use Insight to read intentions, detect lies, gauge mood. On Full, GM tells what they want and hide. On Partial, partial read or they notice you studying them.

Social Contests: When both have goals (debate, negotiation), both roll. Higher wins. GM pre-states outcomes for each side. Use when neither dominates.

Negotiation Tactics

Negotiation is about finding leverage and reading the room. Effective negotiators identify what the other party values, then frame their offer to align with those values.

Identify Leverage: Before negotiating, use Insight to read the NPC’s priorities. Are they Profit-Minded? Offer ongoing revenue. Honor-Bound? Appeal to duty or reputation. Desperate? Offer immediate relief. Leverage isn’t always gold—information, access, or protection can be more valuable.

Frame Your Offer: Present your proposal in terms of their goals, not yours. Don’t say “I need safe passage”—say “You get steady intel on caravans if you let us through.” Make it easy for them to say yes by showing mutual benefit.

Multi-Round Negotiations: Complex negotiations unfold over multiple rolls. Use progress clocks to track “Deal Secured” or “Trust Established.” Each round represents an exchange: you make an offer, they counter, you adapt. Winning early rounds grants Advantage later (they’re more receptive). Losing early rounds means you’re negotiating uphill—you’ll need bigger concessions or better leverage to recover.

When to Walk Away: Not every negotiation succeeds. If the NPC’s demands are unreasonable or their terms unacceptable, walking away is valid. Sometimes failed negotiation creates new opportunities—rival factions, alternative routes, or leverage to revisit the deal later with better position.

Deception and Lying

Deception is high-risk, high-reward. Successful lies open doors, failed lies burn bridges and create enemies.

Craft Believable Lies: Simple lies work better than complex ones. “I’m lost” is easier to sell than “I’m the duke’s secret envoy carrying urgent correspondence.” Ground lies in partial truths—real details mixed with fabrication are harder to spot. The GM may grant Advantage if your lie aligns with observable facts.

Commitment: Once you commit to a lie, maintain it. Contradicting yourself or hesitating raises suspicion. If an NPC asks follow-up questions (Partial Success consequence), have answers ready. Improvise details that support the fiction you’ve created.

Failed Deception: When a lie fails, consequences compound. The NPC doesn’t just reject the lie—they remember you lied. Future social interactions with them may face Disadvantage (Distrusted tag). Groups talk—lying to one guard might poison your reputation with the entire faction. Weigh the risk: is the potential gain worth permanently damaging trust?

Alternatives to Lying: Consider misdirection, selective truth, or omission. You don’t have to lie outright—letting them assume something false is often safer than active deception. “I’m here to see someone” (true) lets them assume you have legitimate business (false assumption, their error).

Using Tools & Resources

Outside combat, you can spend resources to shift position:

Gear: Expert tools (lockpicks, climbing gear, medical kit) grant Advantage or reduce DCs. Improvised tools impose Disadvantage.

Time: Spending extra time grants Advantage on investigation, crafting, or preparation. Rushing imposes Disadvantage.

Contacts & Favors: Call in a contact for information, access, or assistance. The GM might grant Advantage or tick a progress clock directly. Using favors has costs—you owe them, or they ask for something in return.

Tell the GM what you’re spending and how it helps. They’ll decide if it grants Advantage, reduces the DC, or bypasses the check entirely.

Pacing & Momentum

Exploration and social scenes use clocks and tags for structure:

Clocks pace progress and pressure. “Locate Safehouse” (progress) vs. “Hunters Close In” (pressure). Clocks make abstract goals concrete—see progress accumulating and danger mounting.

Tags describe environmental or situational states. Crowded Market grants Advantage to blend but Disadvantage to chase. Dim Archives makes research harder but offers privacy. Suspicious Guards raises DCs in that area. GM names tags; you decide how to use or avoid.

Between action and consequence, fiction breathes. GM describes what changed, you react, momentum carries forward.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Investigation (Searching a Crime Scene)

You: “I search the office for evidence—letters, bloodstains, anything that points to the killer.”

GM: “DC 14. The room’s ransacked (Disorganized tag—Disadvantage). Full means you find a key letter. Partial means you find something but knock over a lamp—pressure ticks. Roll Perception.”

You roll 16—Full.

GM: “Beneath papers you find a sealed letter with the rival house’s crest. Progress clock ‘Identify Culprit’ ticks to 2/4. What now?”

Example 2: Social (Negotiating with a Merchant)

You: “I want to convince the merchant to sell us the map at a fair price by pointing out we’re his best chance to recover stolen goods.”

GM: “She’s skeptical but listening. DC 16. Partial means she agrees but wants a cut of what you recover. Roll Presence.”

You roll 14—Partial.

GM: “She leans back. ‘Fine. Fifty silver for the map, but I want ten percent of any treasure you find using it.’ Do you agree, or push back?”

You: “I’ll take it. We shake on it.”

Both examples show intent, approach, DC, outcomes, and how the fiction moves forward. Investigation used clocks; social used trade-offs. Same mechanics, different context.

Social Contest Examples

Social contests occur when both sides have clear, conflicting goals and neither dominates. Both roll, and the higher result wins. The GM pre-states outcomes before rolls to clarify stakes.

Contest 1: Debate (Public Forum)

Setup: You’re debating the town council over resource allocation. They want to fortify the walls; you want to fund the healer’s guild. The crowd is listening (Divided Audience tag—no inherent Advantage).

Round 1—Opening Arguments:

You: “I appeal to compassion—remind them we lost three families to plague last winter while the walls stood untouched.”

GM: “The council speaker counters that walls saved them from raiders last summer. Both roll Presence. Winner sways the crowd—progress clock ticks toward their goal. Full means strong sway, Partial means weak sway but you expose a weakness in your argument.”

You roll 18 (Presence +3 = 21).

Council Speaker rolls 16 (Presence +2 = 18).

GM: “You win this exchange. Your words hit home—several council members nod. Progress clock ‘Sway Council’ ticks to 1/4. But the Speaker isn’t done.”

Round 2—Rebuttals:

Council Speaker: “The Speaker pivots—‘Compassion is noble, but raiders killed six families two years ago. Dead healers can’t help anyone.’”

GM: “He’s using fear now. Both roll Presence again. Winner consolidates their position. If you win, tick progress. If he wins, he ticks his clock ‘Maintain Status Quo.’”

You roll 14 (Presence +3 = 17).

Council Speaker rolls 19 (Presence +2 = 21).

GM: “He wins. The crowd murmurs agreement—fear is powerful. His clock ticks to 1/4. The crowd is split now. You need a stronger approach.”

Round 3—Final Appeals:

You: “I call on a healer from the crowd to testify about preventable deaths. I’m using my Compassion proficiency to grant myself Advantage.”

GM: “Bold move. You have Advantage. Winner takes the debate—fills their clock to 4/4 or gets 3/4 ticks. Loser gets nothing.”

You roll 20 (with Advantage, Presence +3 = 23).

Council Speaker rolls 15 (Presence +2 = 17).

GM: “The healer’s testimony breaks through. Several council members wipe their eyes. Your clock fills to 4/4. The council votes to fund the healer’s guild first, then consider walls later. You won.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Multiple rounds let both sides build arguments and react.
  • Progress clocks track who’s winning the crowd’s favor.
  • Advantage from proficiencies or allies (healer testimony) can turn the tide.
  • Stakes were clear: Council funding priorities, not life-or-death but meaningful to characters and community.

Contest 2: Interrogation (Extracting Information)

Setup: You’re interrogating a captured smuggler. He knows where the shipment is hidden, but he’s loyal to his boss (Loyal tag—Disadvantage to intimidation). You need the location before his crew realizes he’s missing. Pressure clock “Crew Realizes” starts at 0/4.

Round 1—Intimidation vs. Resolve:

You: “I slam my fist on the table and lean in—‘Tell me where the shipment is, or you’ll rot in the duke’s dungeon.’”

GM: “He’s scared but loyal. You roll Presence (intimidation). He rolls Resolve. Winner controls the exchange. If you win, he cracks—reveals partial info and you tick progress. If he wins, he holds firm, and pressure ticks (crew is looking for him).”

You roll 16 (Presence +2 = 18).

Smuggler rolls 17 (Resolve +3 = 20).

GM: “He spits at your feet. ‘I don’t talk to thugs.’ He’s tougher than he looks. Pressure ticks to 1/4—his crew is starting to ask where he went. Try something else.”

Round 2—Deception vs. Insight:

You: “I change tactics—‘Look, your boss left you to hang. He’s already selling the shipment and keeping your cut. I can protect you if you cooperate.’”

GM: “You’re lying, but he doesn’t know that. You roll Presence (deception). He rolls Insight. Winner controls the narrative. If you win, he believes you and reveals the location. If he wins, he sees through it and shuts down completely.”

You roll 19 (Presence +2 = 21).

Smuggler rolls 12 (Insight +1 = 13).

GM: “His face falls. ‘He… left me? That bastard.’ He tells you the shipment is hidden in the old mill by the river. You got what you needed.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Different approaches (intimidation vs. deception) use different skills. Adapt when one fails.
  • Pressure clock added time urgency—failure has escalating costs.
  • NPC traits (Loyal tag) shaped difficulty—intimidation faced Disadvantage, but deception exploited his loyalty (betrayal hurts more when you’re loyal).
  • Stakes escalated: First round was “does he crack,” second was “can you trick him before time runs out.”

Contest 3: Negotiation (Multi-Party Stakes)

Setup: You’re negotiating safe passage through gang territory. The gang leader (Ruthless, Profit-Minded tags) wants payment. You want free passage and future access. A rival merchant is also bidding for the gang’s favor.

Round 1—Establishing Value:

You: “I offer information about a merchant convoy passing through next week—worth more than gold if they move fast.”

Rival Merchant: “I offer 200 gold now, no strings.”

GM: “Both roll Presence. The gang leader picks the better deal based on rolls and fiction. Winner gets Advantage in the next round (gang leader is inclined toward them).”

You roll 17 (Presence +2 = 19).

Rival Merchant rolls 14 (Presence +3 = 17).

GM: “The leader leans forward. ‘Information… that could be worth five convoys if I play it right.’ He’s interested. You have Advantage next round.”

Round 2—Sealing the Deal:

You (with Advantage): “I sweeten it—offer to split the convoy haul 60/40 in his favor, plus guaranteed intel on future shipments.”

Rival Merchant: “I raise to 300 gold and throw in a crate of weapons.”

GM: “Final bids. Both roll Presence. Winner secures the deal. Loser is out.”

You roll 20 (with Advantage, Presence +2 = 22).

Rival Merchant rolls 18 (Presence +3 = 21).

GM: “The leader grins. ‘Ongoing profit beats one-time gold. You’ve got a deal, friend.’ He offers his hand. The rival merchant storms out, furious.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Competing interests (you vs. rival merchant) create three-way tension.
  • Multiple rounds let you build value and respond to competition.
  • Fiction matters: Your offer (future profit) aligned with his Profit-Minded tag, giving you a narrative edge even though the merchant had higher Presence.
  • Advantage from winning Round 1 made the difference in Round 2.

Contest 4: Fast-Talk (Escaping Suspicion)

Setup: You’re caught sneaking through a noble’s estate. A guard confronts you (Suspicious, By-the-Book tags). You need to talk your way out before he calls reinforcements. Pressure clock “Guards Arrive” starts at 0/4.

You: “I immediately act indignant—‘I’m Lady Elowen’s assistant! She sent me to retrieve her correspondence. How dare you question me!’”

GM: “He’s uncertain. You roll Presence (deception). He rolls Insight. Winner controls the situation. If you win, he buys it and lets you go. If he wins, he sees through it and calls for backup—pressure ticks to 3/4 immediately.”

You roll 13 (Presence +2 = 15).

Guard rolls 16 (Insight +2 = 18).

GM: “He narrows his eyes. ‘Lady Elowen’s assistant would know she’s out of town.’ He raises his whistle. Pressure ticks to 3/4. You’ve got one more chance before guards flood the hall.”

You: “I change tactics—‘Fine, you caught me. I’m here for a different reason. Let me show you something valuable.’ I offer a bribe (50 gold) and use Streetwise proficiency for Advantage.”

GM: “Now you’re appealing to greed. You have Advantage. You roll Presence (persuasion). He rolls Resolve. If you win, he pockets the gold and pretends he didn’t see you. If he loses, he takes the gold AND calls the guards (he’s By-the-Book but not immune to greed). Stakes are high.”

You roll 18 (with Advantage, Presence +2 = 20).

Guard rolls 14 (Resolve +1 = 15).

GM: “He looks at the gold, then at you. He pockets it. ‘I didn’t see anyone. You have two minutes to disappear.’ You sprint for the exit.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Failed first round escalated stakes but didn’t end the contest.
  • Adapting approach (deception → bribery) changed the dynamic.
  • NPC traits (By-the-Book) made deception harder, but greed was a universal lever.
  • Pressure clock created urgency—every round mattered.
  • Risk/reward: Offering bribe could have backfired (lose gold AND get caught), but it was your best remaining option.

Extended Exploration Examples

These examples show multi-step exploration scenarios with clocks, environmental challenges, and problem-solving.

Investigation: The Poisoned Heir

Setup: The duke’s son has been poisoned. You have until dawn to identify the culprit before the assassins flee the city. Progress clock “Identify Assassin” (0/6). Pressure clock “Assassins Flee” (0/4, ticks each scene).

Scene 1—Crime Scene Investigation:

You: “I examine the heir’s chambers for evidence—poison vials, signs of forced entry, anything unusual.”

GM: “The room is luxurious but chaotic (Ransacked tag—Disadvantage to search). DC 16, Perception. Full means you find key evidence and tick progress twice. Partial means you find something but waste time—pressure ticks.”

You roll 18 (Perception +3 = 21)—Full Success.

GM: “Under the bed you find a shattered vial with a distinctive seal—House Vyreth’s personal apothecary. Progress ticks to 2/6. The heir’s window is slightly ajar—someone left in a hurry. What now?”

Scene 2—Interviewing Witnesses:

You: “I question the guards who were on duty. Who entered the heir’s chambers tonight?”

GM: “The guards are nervous (Evasive tag—they fear punishment). DC 15, Insight to read whether they’re hiding something or just scared. Partial means they reveal some truth but you have to press them—pressure ticks.”

You roll 13 (Insight +2 = 15)—Full Success.

GM: “The senior guard cracks: ‘A servant brought wine at midnight. I didn’t recognize her, but she had the seal—I thought she was legitimate.’ Progress ticks to 3/6. You have a description now: hooded, slight build, moves quickly.”

Scene 3—Tracking the Assassin:

You: “I head to House Vyreth’s apothecary to confront them about the vial.”

GM: “It’s late, but lights are on. The apothecary is burning records (Panicked tag—Advantage to intimidate). DC 14, Presence (intimidation) to make them talk before they finish. Fail means they destroy evidence—progress lost.”

You roll 16 (Presence +1 = 17)—Full Success.

GM: “You burst in. The apothecary drops the ledger into the fire but you snatch it. Inside: recent sale to ‘Lady Mirelle’—the duchess’s rival. Progress ticks to 5/6. Pressure ticks to 2/4—assassins are preparing to flee.”

Scene 4—Confronting the Culprit:

You: “I rush to Lady Mirelle’s estate and demand entry, waving the ledger.”

GM: “Her guards block you (Loyal tag—Disadvantage to intimidate). But you have hard evidence. DC 17, Presence (persuasion or intimidation) to get past them. Partial means they let you in but alert Mirelle—she has time to prepare her defense.”

You roll 19 (Presence +1 = 20)—Full Success.

GM: “The guards step aside, cowed by the evidence. You find Mirelle in her study, already packing. She freezes when she sees you. Progress ticks to 6/6. Clock complete—you’ve identified and cornered the assassin before dawn. The duke’s guards arrive to arrest her.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Multiple scenes build toward goal through progress clock
  • Pressure clock creates time urgency across scenes
  • Environmental tags modify difficulty and create tactical choices
  • Full vs. Partial outcomes affect pacing—Full advances faster, Partial adds complications
  • Adaptation required—each scene used different approach (search, interview, intimidate, persuade)

Discovery: Exploring the Sunken Temple

Setup: You’re exploring a flooded temple rumored to hold an ancient artifact. Progress clock “Find Artifact” (0/6). Pressure clock “Temple Collapses” (0/4, ticks when you disturb structure).

Scene 1—Navigating Flooded Corridors:

You: “I wade through the water, searching for passages that lead deeper.”

GM: “The temple is dark and waterlogged (Low Visibility, Unstable Footing tags—Disadvantage to Perception and Athletics). DC 15, Perception to find a safe route. Fail means you disturb rubble—pressure ticks.”

You roll 12 (Perception +3 = 15)—Full Success.

GM: “You spot a corridor with relatively intact stonework. Progress ticks to 1/6. As you wade forward, you hear distant groaning—the structure is unstable. Proceed carefully.”

Scene 2—Deciphering Ancient Murals:

You: “I examine the murals on the walls. Do they show where the artifact is kept?”

GM: “The murals are faded (Worn Carvings tag—Disadvantage). DC 18, Research (ancient lore) to interpret them. Partial means you get a clue but misread a warning—pressure ticks.”

You roll 14 (Research +4 = 18)—Full Success.

GM: “The murals depict a ritual chamber on the lowest level, guarded by ‘the Keeper.’ Progress ticks to 3/6. You also notice warnings about ‘the water that does not sleep’—something lives here.”

Scene 3—Bypassing the Guardian Statue:

You: “I reach a chamber with a massive stone guardian blocking the path. Can I climb around it or find a mechanism to move it?”

GM: “The statue’s eyes glow faintly—it’s warded (Magical Sentinel tag). DC 16, Technical (Arcane Lore or Engineering) to disable it. Fail means it activates—you’ll have to fight or flee, and pressure ticks heavily.”

You roll 15 (Technical +2 = 17)—Full Success.

GM: “You identify the warding sigil and trace a counter-pattern in the water. The glow fades. Progress ticks to 4/6. The passage beyond descends into murky darkness.”

Scene 4—The Flooded Ritual Chamber:

You: “I dive into the chamber, searching for the artifact.”

GM: “It’s fully submerged (Underwater tag—Disadvantage to everything, and you’re holding your breath). DC 17, Perception to spot the artifact quickly. Partial means you find it but something disturbs you—pressure ticks and you might face a threat.”

You roll 16 (Perception +3 = 19)—Full Success.

GM: “You spot the artifact on a pedestal—glowing faintly. You grab it and kick toward the surface. Progress ticks to 6/6. The temple shudders violently as you surface. You sprint for the exit as water surges behind you. You emerge just as the entrance collapses. You made it—artifact in hand.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Environmental tags create escalating challenges (low visibility → underwater → collapsing)
  • Pressure clock represents structural instability, not enemies
  • Skill diversity required (Perception, Research, Technical)—generalists shine here
  • Success pacing controlled by DC difficulty and progress clock size
  • Failure consequences clear at each step—creates meaningful tension

Tracking: The Smuggler’s Trail

Setup: You’re tracking a smuggling crew through wilderness to their hidden camp. Progress clock “Locate Camp” (0/4). Pressure clock “Smugglers Spot You” (0/4, ticks if you fail checks or make noise).

Scene 1—Finding the Trail:

You: “I search for tracks leaving the town—wagon ruts, footprints, anything that shows which way they went.”

GM: “The ground is muddy from recent rain (Soft Terrain tag—Advantage to tracking). DC 13, Survival. Full means clear trail, progress ticks. Partial means you find it but lose time—pressure ticks.”

You roll 15 (Survival +2 = 17)—Full Success.

GM: “Deep wagon ruts lead west into the forest. Progress ticks to 1/4. The tracks are fresh—they passed through within the hour.”

Scene 2—Silent Pursuit:

You: “I follow the trail quietly, staying hidden in case they have scouts.”

GM: “The forest is dense (Heavy Cover tag—Advantage to Stealth). DC 14, Stealth to stay undetected. Fail means they hear you—pressure ticks to 3/4 immediately and they set an ambush.”

You roll 18 (Stealth +3 = 21)—Full Success.

GM: “You move like a shadow. Progress ticks to 2/4. Ahead, you hear voices—you’re close.”

Scene 3—Scouting the Camp:

You: “I creep to the edge of their camp and observe. How many are there? What are they guarding?”

GM: “The camp is busy (Crowded, Alert Sentries tags—Disadvantage to Perception). DC 16 to count enemies and spot the cargo without being seen. Partial means you get some info but a sentry glances your way—pressure ticks.”

You roll 13 (Perception +3 = 16)—Full Success.

GM: “Six smugglers, two on watch. The cargo: crates marked with noble seals—stolen goods. Progress ticks to 4/4. You’ve located the camp and gathered intel. Pressure clock at 0/4 still—they don’t know you’re here. Do you retreat to report, or act now?”

Key Takeaways:

  • Progression through stages: Find trail → Follow quietly → Scout camp
  • Pressure clock tracks detection risk, not time
  • Environmental tags shift (Soft Terrain → Heavy Cover → Crowded)—each scene has different tactical considerations
  • Information gathering as goal, not just “find the place”
  • Player choice at end—success creates options (report, ambush, negotiate)

Problem-Solving: The Locked Archive

Setup: You need to retrieve a document from a noble’s archive before morning. Progress clock “Retrieve Document” (0/4). Pressure clock “Guards Patrol Returns” (0/4, ticks each scene or on Partial/Fail).

Scene 1—Bypassing the Lock:

You: “I examine the archive door. It’s a complex mechanical lock. Can I pick it?”

GM: “It’s a high-quality lock (Master Craftsman tag—Disadvantage). DC 17, Technical (Security). Partial means you open it but it takes time—pressure ticks.”

You roll 14 (Technical +4 = 18)—Full Success.

GM: “Your lockpicks find the tumblers. The door opens silently. Progress ticks to 1/4. Inside: rows of shelves in dim light.”

Scene 2—Navigating the Archive:

You: “I search for the document—‘Contract of House Verath, dated 15 years ago.’ Where would that be filed?”

GM: “The archive uses an old filing system (Archaic Organization tag—Disadvantage to Research). DC 15, Research. Fail means you waste time and make noise—pressure ticks twice.”

You roll 16 (Research +2 = 18)—Full Success.

GM: “You decipher the system: contracts are filed by house, chronologically. Progress ticks to 2/4. You locate the Verath section—third shelf from the top.”

Scene 3—Retrieving the Document:

You: “I climb up carefully to retrieve the contract.”

GM: “The shelf is high and old (Unstable Furniture tag). DC 13, Athletics to climb without knocking things over. Fail means loud crash—pressure ticks to 4/4 immediately (guards investigate).”

You roll 15 (Athletics +1 = 16)—Full Success.

GM: “You scale the shelf like a cat, retrieve the contract, and descend. Progress ticks to 4/4. You have the document. Pressure at 0/4 still—guards haven’t noticed. Time to leave.”

You: “I exit the same way I came, locking the door behind me so they don’t notice the break-in.”

GM: “Smart. You relock the door (no check needed—you already proved your skill). You slip into the night with the contract. Mission accomplished without raising alarm.”

Key Takeaways:

  • Layered challenges: Lock → Navigation → Retrieval → Escape
  • Different skills for each obstacle (Technical, Research, Athletics)
  • Pressure clock represents time limit and detection risk combined
  • Full Success pacing allows smooth heist when player succeeds consistently
  • Optional choice to relock door shows player agency (could rush, or be thorough)

Conditions, Tags, and Status

Understanding Conditions & Tags

Tags describe the environment or situation: terrain, lighting, cover. They’re external to your character. Dim Light, Slick, Fragile Cover.

Conditions describe your character’s state: injuries, status effects, temporary afflictions. Exhausted, Restrained, Bleeding, Stunned.

Tags affect what you can do in a space. Conditions affect you wherever you go. Both grant Advantage or Disadvantage, impose narrative limits, or create tactical opportunities.

Common Environmental Tags

  • Dim Light: Advantage to stealth; Disadvantage to ranged attacks and detailed observation.
  • Slick: Disadvantage on movement and maneuvers requiring balance (ice, oil, wet stone).
  • Cramped: Hinders movement; increases fall risk; limits large weapons or sweeping actions.
  • Fragile Cover: Grants Advantage to defense but degrades on Partial Success or repeated use.
  • Solid Cover: Reliable protection; grants Advantage to defense without degrading.
  • Elevated: Advantage on ranged attacks; forces opponents to climb or find alternate routes.
  • Crowded: Advantage to blend in or lose pursuit; Disadvantage to chase or spot targets.
  • Hazardous: Environmental danger (fire, acid, collapsing floor); risk of damage or conditions.
  • Obscured: Vision blocked (fog, smoke, darkness); Disadvantage to ranged attacks and navigation.
  • Unstable: Risk of collapse, shifting terrain, or loss of footing; movement requires checks.

Common Conditions

Conditions represent your character’s state—injuries, status effects, or temporary afflictions that follow you regardless of location.

Physical Conditions

Exhausted: Physical or mental fatigue. Disadvantage on physical actions (combat, athletics, endurance). Clear via rest (full night) or aid (stimulants, magic, medical treatment).

Example: After fighting through the ruins for hours, you’re Exhausted. When the patrol attacks, you have Disadvantage on all Strike and Maneuver actions. Resting at camp for 8 hours clears the condition.

Bleeding: Active wound requiring immediate treatment. Time pressure—worsens if ignored. Clear via first aid (ally uses medkit, DC 14), magic, or rest after stabilization.

Example: The bandit’s blade cuts deep—you’re Bleeding. Each scene that passes without treatment, your resolve clock ticks. An ally uses their medkit (First Aid skill + RSN, DC 14) to stabilize you. Condition clears.

Poisoned: Toxin affecting you. Effects vary by poison type (Disadvantage on actions, ongoing resolve clock damage, paralysis). Duration varies. Clear via antidote, medical aid, or time (if non-lethal).

Example: The trap needle injects venom—you’re Poisoned. Disadvantage on all actions for the next hour unless you find an antidote. Your medic identifies the toxin and administers the cure. Condition clears.

Restrained: Movement limited by bonds (ropes, chains), grapple, or obstacle (pinned under debris). Can’t move freely. Clear via breaking free (MIG check), ally assistance, or time.

Example: The guard grapples you—you’re Restrained. You can’t move or Maneuver. On your turn, roll MIG vs. their MIG to break free (DC 16). Ally Strikes the guard to force them to release you. Condition clears.

Mental/Combat Conditions

Stunned: Dazed or disoriented from impact. Can’t act next turn. Clears automatically after one turn (no action required).

Example: The concussion grenade explodes nearby—you’re Stunned. You lose your next action. The turn after, the condition clears automatically and you can act normally.

Dazed: Disoriented, struggling to focus. Disadvantage on perception, reaction, and complex tasks. Clears after one turn or with ally assistance (snapping you out of it).

Example: The flash-bang disorients you—you’re Dazed. Disadvantage on Investigation and Perception checks. Your ally shouts and shakes you—condition clears immediately.

Frightened: Fear of visible threat. Disadvantage on actions while threat is in sight. Clear by removing/defeating threat, rallying (ally assistance or self-discipline check), or breaking line of sight.

Example: The dragon roars—you’re Frightened. Disadvantage on all actions while it’s visible. Your ally’s rallying speech lets you roll PRE, DC 14, to shake it off. You succeed—condition clears.

Suppressed: Under pressure from covering fire, intimidation, or overwhelming force. Disadvantage on next action. Clears after acting or repositioning to safety.

Example: Automatic gunfire pins you down—you’re Suppressed. Disadvantage on your next action. You use Defend to reposition behind solid cover. Condition clears.

Tactical Conditions

Exposed: Out of cover, vulnerable. Disadvantage to defense. Attackers gain Advantage against you. Clear by moving to cover or breaking line of sight.

Example: You’re caught in the open courtyard—you’re Exposed. Enemies have Advantage on attacks against you. You Sprint to the pillar (Defend action). Condition clears once behind cover.

Marked: Tracked or targeted by enemy. Opponent gains Advantage on attacks against you. Clear by breaking contact (Defend/Withdraw to different zone) or eliminating the marker.

Example: The sniper has you in their sights—you’re Marked. They have Advantage on all ranged attacks against you. You use Defend to break line of sight by ducking into the building. Condition clears.

Using Status in Play

Leverage Tags: Use environmental features to your advantage. Fight in Dim Light for stealth. Use Elevated positions for ranged attacks. Create Fragile Cover with Set Up actions.

Clear Conditions: Prioritize dangerous conditions. Bleeding and Poisoned create time pressure. Restrained and Exposed make you vulnerable. Use actions, resources, or allies to clear them.

Apply Conditions: Set Up and Maneuver actions can apply conditions to opponents. Trip someone to make them Exposed. Suppress them with covering fire. Force them into Hazardous terrain.

Status effects drive tactical choices. They’re tools for creating and solving problems.

Combining Tags for Compound Effects

Tags and conditions stack to create complex tactical situations:

Favorable Combinations (Use These)

Elevated + Solid Cover: Advantage on ranged attacks while protected. Ideal for archers and snipers.

Dim Light + Stealth Proficiency: Advantage to hide and ambush. Use Set Up to create Dim Light (knock out torches, trigger smoke).

Cramped + Ranged Attacks: If enemies are stuck in tight space, ranged attacks from outside avoid the Cramped penalty.

Hazardous + Maneuver: Shove enemies into fire, acid, collapsing floors. Environmental damage without spending Strike actions.

Unfavorable Combinations (Avoid These)

Slick + Cramped: Double Disadvantage on movement and maneuvers. Falling risk extreme. Get out fast or fight defensively.

Exposed + Elevated: Elevated grants Advantage to attackers, not you. If you’re Exposed on high ground, you’re a target.

Obscured + Ranged Combat: Disadvantage to ranged attacks in fog/smoke/darkness. Switch to melee or wait for visibility.

Restrained + Hazardous: Can’t move away from danger. Priority: clear Restrained immediately.

Tactical Uses of Tags

Creating Tags with Set Up Actions

You can create environmental tags to shift tactical balance:

Example 1—Creating Dim Light:

You: “I shoot out the overhead lights with my pistol. Set Up action to create Dim Light.”

GM: “Roll AGI + Ranged, DC 14.”

You roll 16 (Full Success).

GM: “The room plunges into darkness. Dim Light tag applies. Everyone has Disadvantage on ranged attacks and detailed observation. What’s next?”

You: “Perfect. I have Night Vision proficiency—I can see fine. I move to flank the guard.”

Example 2—Creating Hazardous Terrain:

You: “I kick over the brazier, spilling hot coals across the floor. Trying to create a Hazardous zone between us and the enemies.”

GM: “Maneuver action, DC 14 to place it precisely.”

You roll 13 (Partial Success).

GM: “You kick it over, but it scatters unpredictably. Hazardous zone created, but you’re on the edge—take 1 resolve clock tick from the heat. Enemies are now blocked unless they cross fire.”

Exploiting Existing Tags

Use what’s already there:

Slick Terrain: Maneuver enemies into Slick areas, then Shove them. They fall prone (Exposed).

Fragile Cover: If enemies hide behind Fragile Cover, Strike actions degrade it. After 2-3 hits, the cover is gone.

Crowded Spaces: Use Crowded areas to blend in (Advantage to Stealth) or lose pursuers.

Elevated Positions: Claim high ground early. Once there, you force enemies to climb (wasting actions) or take ranged fire with Disadvantage.

Clearing Conditions Strategically

Immediate Threats (Clear Now)

Bleeding: Worsens over time. Use first aid, medkits, or ally assistance to stabilize. Don’t wait—this condition kills.

Exposed: Disadvantage to defense means you’re next. Use Defend/Withdraw to reach cover or break line of sight.

Restrained: Can’t move, can’t escape. Use actions to break free (MIG check typically) or have allies help.

Poisoned (Fast-Acting): Some poisons deal ongoing damage or escalate. Clear with antidote, first aid, or magical healing.

Manageable Threats (Clear When Possible)

Exhausted: Disadvantage on physical actions but survivable. Rest or use stimulants when safe.

Suppressed: Only lasts one action. Accept Disadvantage or use Defend to reposition safely.

Dazed: Clears after one turn. If you can survive the round, condition ends naturally.

Strategic Choices (Weigh Cost)

Marked: Opponent gains Advantage against you. If you can eliminate them fast, ignore Marked. If not, break contact (Defend/Withdraw).

Frightened: Disadvantage while threat visible. If threat is weak, ignore and push through. If threat is strong, flee and regroup.

Worked Example: Tag and Condition Interaction

Setup: You’re fighting mercenaries in a warehouse. The space is Cramped (crates and boxes everywhere) with Fragile Cover (wooden crates). One mercenary has you Suppressed with automatic fire.

Round 1—You (Suppressed, in Fragile Cover):

You: “I’m Suppressed—Disadvantage on my next action. I use Defend to break line of sight and reposition behind Solid Cover (a metal support pillar).”

GM: “Roll AGI, DC 14 to move through the Cramped space without tripping.”

You roll 15 (Full Success).

GM: “Suppressed clears. You’re now behind Solid Cover. The mercenary loses line of sight.”

Round 2—Ally (Archer, Elevated on catwalk):

Ally: “I have Elevated and the mercenary is in the open. I Strike with Advantage.”

GM: “Roll. You have Advantage from Elevated.”

Ally rolls 18 (Full Success).

GM: “Arrow hits clean. Mercenary’s resolve clock ticks to 3/4. He’s wounded and now Exposed (lost his cover position).”

Round 3—You (behind Solid Cover):

You: “He’s Exposed and wounded. I lean out and Strike. Do I have Advantage from him being Exposed?”

GM: “Yes—Exposed grants attackers Advantage. Roll with Advantage.”

You roll 17 with Advantage (Full Success).

GM: “You drop him. Resolve clock fills—he’s out. Warehouse quiet now.”

Analysis: Tags (Cramped, Fragile vs. Solid Cover, Elevated) and conditions (Suppressed, Exposed) shaped every choice. You cleared Suppressed by repositioning. Ally used Elevated for Advantage. You capitalized on Exposed to finish the fight. Status drove tactics.

Genre-Specific Tags

Fantasy Tags

  • Enchanted: Magic aura grants benefits or penalties depending on source.
  • Cursed: Persistent penalty until dispelled or purified.
  • Sacred Ground: Advantage for faithful, Disadvantage for undead/fiends.

Modern/Urban Tags

  • Surveillance: Cameras, sensors—actions are monitored. Stealth difficult.
  • Networked: Devices connected to grid. Hacking can disrupt or aid.
  • Reinforced: Hardened structures resist damage. Explosives may be needed.

Sci-Fi Tags

  • Zero-G: Floating combat. AGI checks to maneuver. Firearms have recoil issues.
  • Pressurized: Breach = explosive decompression. Hazardous if hull damaged.
  • Irradiated: Exposure risk. Suits required. Time-limited operations.

Equipment, Gear, and Inventory

Gear Philosophy

Gear in this system represents narrative permission and fictional positioning, not exhaustive simulation. A lockpick set lets you attempt picking locks. Climbing gear makes scaling walls feasible. A medkit allows first aid.

The GM tracks realism through fiction, not item weights or durability trackers. If you have rope, you can use it. If you run out, the GM will tell you.

Core Equipment Categories

Weapons: Define your approach in combat. Blades for close quarters, bows for range, improvised tools for desperation. Mechanically similar; narratively distinct.

Armor: Provides protection. Heavy armor might grant Advantage to defense but impose Disadvantage on stealth. Light armor keeps you mobile. Narratively, armor shapes how you’re perceived.

Tools: Professional equipment that grants narrative access. Thieves’ tools, medical kits, arcane focuses, hacking rigs. Without the tool, you can’t attempt the action (or face Disadvantage).

Resources: Narrative assets like contacts, favors, safe houses, or information. Spend them to gain Advantage, skip challenges, or unlock options.

Inventory Management

This system avoids micromanagement. Use one of these approaches:

Slots: Carry X significant items (typically 5–10). Small items don’t count. Narratively reasonable.

Narrative Limits: If it makes sense that you have it, you do. If the GM questions it, justify or accept you don’t have it.

GM Discretion: The GM tracks what’s plausible. If you’re fleeing through sewers, you probably don’t have your full kit.

Choose the method that fits your table’s preferences.

When Gear Matters

Gear creates Advantage or narrative access:

  • Climbing gear grants Advantage on scaling cliffs.
  • A disguise kit enables infiltration attempts.
  • A medkit lets you stabilize Bleeding conditions.
  • A contact in the docks gives you access to smugglers.

Gear matters when it changes what’s possible or shifts your odds. The GM calls for specific gear when fiction demands it.

Worked Example

Situation: Marcus (the burned intelligence analyst from Chapter 16) needs to infiltrate a corporate server room to retrieve encrypted files.

Gear Check: Marcus has his laptop (encrypted), fake IDs, and burner phones. The GM asks: “Do you have anything that helps with physical security?”

Marcus’s player: “I have lockpick tools in my kit—picked them up after the safe house job.”

GM: “Good. The server room door has a standard lock. With your Urban Investigation skill and lockpicks, you have Advantage. Roll RSN + skill.”

Result: Gear (lockpicks) granted narrative access (attempting the lock) and mechanical advantage (Advantage on the roll). Without the tools, Marcus would face Disadvantage or couldn’t attempt it at all.

Narrative Limit Example: Later, the GM asks if Marcus has a grappling hook to scale the building. Marcus’s player: “I don’t think I’d have that—I’ve been traveling light since fleeing the city.” The GM agrees. Fiction determines plausibility.

Genre-Specific Gear Examples

Fantasy Gear

Weapons: Longsword, shortbow, staff, daggers. Mechanically similar; fiction determines reach and style.

Armor: Chainmail (heavy, protective, loud), leather (light, mobile, quiet), robes (no protection, unhindered casting).

Tools: Thieves’ tools (lockpicking, trap disarm), healer’s kit (stabilize wounds), arcane focus (spellcasting), climbing gear (ropes, pitons).

Resources: Guild membership (access to fences, safe houses), noble favor (legal immunity, access to courts), contacts in underworld.

Example Loadout—Rogue:

  • Weapons: Paired daggers, hand crossbow
  • Armor: Leather armor (Advantage to stealth, light protection)
  • Tools: Thieves’ tools, grappling hook, smoke bombs
  • Resources: Contact in Thieves’ Guild (can fence stolen goods)

Modern/Urban Gear

Weapons: Pistol (concealable), shotgun (close quarters), rifle (range), knife (backup).

Armor: Kevlar vest (stops bullets, bulky), casual clothes (no protection, blends in).

Tools: Lockpick set, laptop (hacking), smartphone (research, communication), first aid kit, disguise kit.

Resources: Police contact (access to records), journalist ally (publicize info), safe house (temporary hideout).

Example Loadout—Private Investigator:

  • Weapons: Concealed pistol, collapsible baton
  • Armor: Kevlar vest (worn under coat)
  • Tools: Lockpicks, camera, audio recorder, laptop
  • Resources: Contact in city police (Detective Reyes), access to criminal databases

Sci-Fi Gear

Weapons: Plasma rifle (energy weapon, recharge needed), slug thrower (kinetic, breaches hulls), stun baton (nonlethal).

Armor: Enviro-suit (life support, sealed), combat armor (heavy, powered), shipboard clothes (no protection, mobility).

Tools: Neural interface (hacking, data access), portable lab (analysis), medkit (trauma stabilization), grav-boots (zero-G movement).

Resources: Ship access (transportation, refuge), black-market contact (restricted tech), corporate ID (facility access).

Example Loadout—Xenobiologist:

  • Weapons: Stun baton, compact slug thrower
  • Armor: Enviro-suit with biohazard seals
  • Tools: Portable lab, neural interface, sample containers
  • Resources: Access to fringe colony network, black-market bio-samples

Gear Upgrades and Advancement

As campaigns progress, gear improves:

Tier 1 (Starting)—Basic Gear

Function: Narrative permission. You have what you need to attempt actions.

Examples: Standard weapons, basic tools, mundane armor, a few contacts.

Mechanical Effect: Advantage when gear applies. No special bonuses.

Tier 2 (Mid-Campaign)—Specialized Gear

Function: Enhanced capabilities. Gear grants additional benefits or covers edge cases.

Examples: Masterwork weapons (+1 to strikes), advanced tools (hacking rig with AI assist), reinforced armor (grants Solid Cover when stationary), influential contacts.

Mechanical Effect: Advantage plus occasional narrative bonus (bypass minor obstacles, reduce complications).

How to Acquire: Loot, downtime crafting (see Chapter 10), faction rewards, purchase with significant resources.

Tier 3 (Late Campaign)—Legendary Gear

Function: Game-changing capabilities. Gear opens new tactical options or solves problems outright.

Examples: Enchanted blade (ignores armor), cloaking device (invisible in darkness), AI-guided weapon (auto-target), high-level political ally (mobilize resources).

Mechanical Effect: Advantage, narrative bonuses, and occasional automatic success on appropriate checks.

How to Acquire: Campaign rewards, major quest completion, legendary crafting projects, powerful faction membership.

Resource Spending and Leverage

Resources are narrative advantages you spend for effect:

Contacts and Favors

What They Do: Contacts provide information, access, or assistance.

When to Spend: When stuck on investigation, need entry to restricted areas, or require backup.

Example:

You: “I call Detective Reyes—he owes me a favor from the docks case. Can he get me into the precinct evidence locker?”

GM: “Yes, but this burns the favor. He’ll let you in after hours, but you’re on your own if caught.”

You: “Worth it. I need that file.”

Safe Houses and Refuges

What They Do: Temporary havens where you can rest, plan, or hide from pursuit.

When to Use: When heat is on, you need downtime, or you need secure location for planning.

Example:

GM: “Corporate security is sweeping the district. Pressure clock ticks to 3/4.”

You: “I head to the safe house—the old warehouse on Pier 7. We lie low until the sweep passes.”

GM: “Safe. You can rest here. Clear Exhausted condition and plan next move.”

Information and Secrets

What They Do: Leverage for negotiation, blackmail, or bypassing obstacles.

When to Spend: When negotiating from weak position, need to force cooperation, or bypass gatekeepers.

Example:

GM: “The crime boss laughs. ‘Why would I tell you anything?’”

You: “I slide a datastick across the table. ‘Because I have evidence of your deal with the feds. Cooperate, and this disappears.’”

GM: “Roll Persuasion with Advantage—you have serious leverage.”

Narrative Inventory Troubleshooting

Common Questions

“Can I have X?”

  • If it fits your concept and backstory: Probably yes.
  • If it’s expensive or rare: Requires justification (downtime purchase, loot, favor).
  • If it’s implausible: No. Explain why it doesn’t fit.

“Do I run out of ammo/supplies?”

  • Normal usage: No tracking. Assume you restock during downtime.
  • Extended missions without resupply: GM might call for resource checks or introduce scarcity as pressure.
  • Narrative scarcity: If fleeing through wilderness or trapped, the GM might say “You’re low on supplies—what do you prioritize?”

“Can I upgrade my gear?”

  • Downtime crafting: Use Chapter 10 downtime rules to craft or upgrade.
  • Loot: Acquire better gear from defeated enemies or discovered caches.
  • Purchase: Spend resources (money, favors) during downtime to buy upgrades.

“I want gear that gives me a bonus.”

  • Narrative permission: Gear grants narrative access (lockpicks let you pick locks) and Advantage when relevant.
  • Mechanical bonuses: Mid-to-late campaign gear might grant +1 or special abilities. Talk to your GM about progression.

Downtime & Recovery

Downtime Overview

Downtime represents time between adventures: days, weeks, or months when you’re not in immediate danger. Use it to recover, train, pursue projects, and build connections.

Downtime bridges story arcs, gives your character space to grow, and creates opportunities for personal goals beyond the main plot.

Recovery & Rest

Healing: Extended rest clears most injuries and conditions. The GM determines how much time you need based on severity.

Clearing Conditions: Rest removes Exhausted, Bleeding, and similar conditions. Severe conditions (Poisoned, Cursed) may require medical aid or specialized treatment.

Restoring Resources: Replenish gear, contacts, and narrative resources. Re-stock supplies, repair equipment, and rebuild spent favors.

Rest is the default recovery method. Specific conditions may require more.

Downtime Activities

Downtime activities let you invest time in goals beyond immediate adventures. Each activity requires time (days to weeks), may involve checks, and produces tangible results.

Training

What It Does: Learn new skills, improve proficiencies, or expand your capabilities through practice or instruction.

Time Required: 1-4 weeks depending on complexity

Mechanical Effects: - Skill Training: Work toward gaining a new skill from an adjacent package (requires GM approval and narrative justification) - Proficiency Refinement: Deepen expertise in an existing proficiency, potentially unlocking new narrative permissions - Attribute Conditioning: Rare; intensive training might contribute to attribute improvement at major milestones

How It Works: Describe what you’re training in and why. The GM sets a progress clock (typically 4-6 segments). Each week of training, roll relevant attribute + training focus (RSN for studying, MIG for physical conditioning, etc.) vs. DC 13. Full Success ticks 2 segments, Partial ticks 1. Fill the clock to complete training.

Example:

You: “I want to train with the retired duelist, Master Valen, to learn Blades.”

GM: “Valen agrees to teach you. Progress clock ‘Learn Blades’ (0/6). You have 4 weeks before the next adventure. Roll RSN or AGI each week, DC 13.”

Week 1: Roll 15 (Full Success) → Progress 2/6 Week 2: Roll 12 (Partial Success) → Progress 3/6 Week 3: Roll 16 (Full Success) → Progress 5/6 Week 4: Roll 14 (Full Success) → Progress 7/6 (Complete!)

GM: “You’ve mastered the basics. Add Blades skill. Valen invites you to spar with his advanced students—potential future connection.”

Research

What It Does: Investigate mysteries, study ancient texts, gather intelligence, or learn specialized knowledge.

Time Required: Days to weeks depending on obscurity

Mechanical Effects: - Knowledge Gain: Learn crucial information (villain’s weakness, ritual requirements, historical context) - Advantages: Gain narrative Advantage on future related checks (“I researched this cult’s symbols”) - Story Hooks: Uncover leads, plot threads, or complications

How It Works: State your research goal and method (library, interview experts, fieldwork). GM sets a progress clock based on how obscure the information is. Each research session, roll RSN + Research vs. DC (12-18). Full Success ticks progress and may reveal bonus info. Partial Success ticks progress but introduces complications (wrong lead, unwanted attention). Fail might waste time or alert interested parties.

Example:

You: “I research the Crimson Hand cult in the city archives. What’s their origin and current activity?”

GM: “Progress clock ‘Crimson Hand Research’ (0/4). DC 15. Partial means you find info but someone notices your interest.”

Session 1: Roll 18 (Full Success) → Progress 2/4. “You find records of their founding—50 years ago, a rogue mage. They’re active in the docks.”

Session 2: Roll 13 (Partial Success) → Progress 3/4. “You find recent activity reports, but a librarian sees what you’re reading and looks concerned. They slip you a note: ‘They’re watching.’”

Session 3: Roll 16 (Full Success) → Progress 5/4 (Complete!). “You compile a dossier: Their current leader is ‘The Veiled One,’ they operate from a warehouse, and they’re smuggling arcane components. You gain Advantage on future checks to infiltrate or confront them.”

Crafting

What It Does: Create gear, modify equipment, brew potions, or forge tools.

Time Required: Days to weeks depending on item complexity

Mechanical Effects: - Useful Gear: Create items with narrative permissions (lockpicks, climbing gear, poisons) - Custom Equipment: Modify existing gear (reinforce armor, add weapon feature) - Consumables: Brew potions, craft ammunition, prepare explosives

How It Works: Describe what you want to create. GM sets a progress clock and resource cost (materials, workshop access). Roll Technical + relevant skill (Engineering, Medicine for potions, etc.) vs. DC (12-18). Full Success ticks progress efficiently. Partial Success ticks progress but wastes materials or takes longer. Fail might ruin materials or create a flawed product.

Example:

You: “I want to craft a grappling hook launcher—lets me scale buildings quickly.”

GM: “Progress clock ‘Grappling Launcher’ (0/4). Costs 50 gold in materials. DC 14 Technical. Partial means you burn materials (additional 20 gold).”

Week 1: Roll 16 (Full Success) → Progress 2/4. “The trigger mechanism works perfectly.”

Week 2: Roll 12 (Partial Success) → Progress 3/4. “The spring isn’t right—you buy a stronger one (+20 gold).”

Week 3: Roll 17 (Full Success) → Progress 5/4 (Complete!). “It’s done. Grappling launcher: Grants Advantage on climbing checks, can reach 30 feet.”

Social Networking

What It Does: Build contacts, cultivate alliances, or gain influence with factions.

Time Required: 1-3 weeks per contact/faction

Mechanical Effects: - Contacts: NPCs who provide information, favors, or access - Faction Standing: Improve reputation with a group (guards, merchants, nobles) - Story Hooks: Connections create opportunities and complications

How It Works: Describe who you’re networking with and your approach (gifts, favors, shared interests). GM sets a progress clock (typically 4-6 segments). Roll Presence + Persuasion vs. DC (12-16). Full Success builds rapport quickly. Partial Success builds rapport but you owe a favor or reveal something. Fail might offend or waste their time.

Example:

You: “I want to build a contact in the city watch—someone who can warn me about investigations.”

GM: “Progress clock ‘City Watch Contact’ (0/4). You meet Sergeant Kade at a tavern. DC 13 Presence.”

Week 1: Roll 15 (Full Success) → Progress 2/4. “Kade appreciates your stories. You bond over shared dislike of corruption.”

Week 2: Roll 11 (Partial Success) → Progress 3/4. “Kade warms to you but asks: ‘If I tip you off, what’s in it for me?’ You owe a favor.”

Week 3: Roll 16 (Full Success) → Progress 5/4 (Complete!). “Kade agrees to warn you about investigations if you help bust a smuggling ring. Contact established.”

Personal Projects

What It Does: Pursue character-specific goals tied to backstory, beliefs, or ambitions.

Time Required: Varies widely (weeks to months)

Mechanical Effects: - Narrative Closure: Resolve character arcs or backstory threads - New Resources: Establish safe houses, businesses, or strongholds - Character Growth: Evolve motivations, relationships, or goals

How It Works: Propose your project. GM frames scope and sets a long-term progress clock (6-10 segments). Progress happens through downtime activities, side quests, or story events. Projects create narrative depth more than mechanical advantage.

Example:

You: “I want to establish a safe house network for refugees fleeing the war.”

GM: “Progress clock ‘Refugee Network’ (0/8). You’ll need safe locations, trustworthy contacts, and funding. We’ll track progress as you complete related activities.”

Downtime 1: You donate 200 gold and recruit a sympathetic merchant. Progress 2/8.

Downtime 2: Side quest—you rescue refugees from bandits. Progress 4/8.

Downtime 3: You negotiate with a noble for a property. Roll Persuasion, DC 15. Success → Progress 6/8.

Downtime 4: You formalize the network with local clergy. Progress 8/8 (Complete!). Network established: You can shelter allies, gather info from refugees, and have a meaningful legacy.

Side Quests

What It Does: Take on smaller missions during downtime—bounties, delivery jobs, investigation requests.

Time Required: Days to 1 week

Mechanical Effects: - Income: Earn gold (typically 50-200 depending on danger) - Reputation: Build standing with factions or contacts - Story Leads: Uncover hooks for future adventures

How It Works: The GM presents opportunities or you propose ideas. Side quests are mini-adventures—typically 1-2 scenes with a conflict and resolution. Roll relevant skills as needed. Success earns rewards, failure brings complications but no catastrophic consequences.

Example:

You: “I take a bounty: track down a pickpocket who stole from the merchant guild.”

GM: “100 gold reward. Scene 1: Track them (Perception, DC 14). Scene 2: Apprehend them (Combat or Persuasion, DC 15).”

Scene 1: Roll 16 (Full Success). “You track them to the docks. They’re selling stolen goods.”

Scene 2: Roll Persuasion 14 (Partial Success). “You convince them to return the goods, but they flee before you can bring them in. Merchant guild pays 75 gold (partial reward) and asks you to watch for them.”

Side quests are lower stakes but still engaging. They fill time productively and keep your character active between major story arcs.

NPC Relationships & Faction Standing

Building relationships with NPCs and factions creates narrative depth, mechanical advantages, and story hooks. Relationships aren’t just flavor—they provide access, information, and support when you need them.

Relationship Mechanics

Relationship Levels: - Stranger (0): No connection, neutral stance - Acquaintance (1): Recognize each other, willing to talk - Friend (2): Trust and support, small favors - Ally (3): Significant trust, major favors, risk themselves - Sworn (4): Ride-or-die, sacrifice for you

Building Relationships: Progress through levels via social interactions, favors, shared experiences, and downtime activities. GM tracks relationship progress using clocks (typically 4 segments per level). Positive interactions tick progress; betrayals or conflicts tick a “Deterioration” clock.

Relationship Benefits by Level:

Acquaintance: - Provide basic information (“I heard rumors about…”) - Small discounts or preferential treatment - Warn you about obvious dangers

Friend: - Share valuable information (“Here’s what I know about the cult”) - Loan resources (gold, gear, access to locations) - Introduce you to their contacts

Ally: - Take risks for you (lie to authorities, provide alibis) - Offer significant resources (safe house, armed backup) - Go out of their way to help

Sworn: - Sacrifice for you (take a hit, abandon their safety) - Share deeply personal or dangerous secrets - Stand by you even when it costs them everything

Relationship Example: Building an Ally

Setup: You want Sergeant Kade (City Watch) to become an ally, not just a friend.

Current Level: Friend (2). Kade warns you about investigations and shares info.

Goal: Reach Ally (3). Kade will actively help you, even bending rules.

Method: Complete a major favor—help Kade bust a smuggling ring he’s been tracking.

GM: “Progress clock ‘Kade Becomes Ally’ (0/4). You need to prove you’re worth risking his career for.”

Downtime Actions: - Week 1: You research the smugglers (Research check, DC 15). Success → Progress 1/4. - Week 2: You stake out the warehouse (Perception check, DC 14). Success → Progress 2/4. - Week 3: You infiltrate and gather evidence (Stealth check, DC 16). Success → Progress 4/4 (Complete!).

Result: You hand Kade the evidence. He makes the bust, gets promoted, and tells you: “I owe you. Anything you need—within reason—I’ve got your back.” Kade is now an Ally (Level 3).

Benefits: Kade can now provide search warrants, delay investigations, or mobilize guards for backup. He won’t break the law outright, but he’ll stretch it for you.

Faction Standing

Factions are groups with shared goals—guilds, noble houses, criminal syndicates, religious orders. Your standing with them affects access, prices, and story opportunities.

Standing Levels: - Hostile (-2): Attacked on sight, bounties on your head - Unfriendly (-1): Refused service, high prices, suspicion - Neutral (0): Default state, transactional interactions - Friendly (+1): Discounts, favors, introductions - Honored (+2): Major support, access to inner circles, defended by faction

Changing Standing: Complete jobs for the faction, donate resources, or advance their goals. Each significant action moves standing by 1 step (Neutral → Friendly, for example). Betrayals or opposition move standing down.

Example:

Faction: The Merchant Guild

Current Standing: Neutral (0). They’ll trade with you at market prices.

Action: You recover stolen guild shipments worth 500 gold.

GM: “The guild master thanks you publicly. Standing shifts to Friendly (+1).”

Benefits: 10% discount on guild goods, introductions to wealthy merchants, invitations to guild events (social networking opportunities).

Managing Multiple Relationships

Conflicting Loyalties: When two contacts or factions oppose each other, you may need to choose sides or navigate carefully. Betraying one to help another damages the betrayed relationship.

Maintenance: Relationships require occasional interaction to maintain. If you ignore a friend for months, the GM may tick a “Neglect” clock. Fill it, and the relationship downgrades (Friend → Acquaintance).

Example:

You: “I haven’t talked to Marcus (my researcher friend) in three months. Is he still helping me?”

GM: “Marcus feels ignored. Neglect clock (0/4) is at 3/4. If you don’t reconnect soon, he’ll downgrade to Acquaintance.”

You: “I visit him and apologize, bringing his favorite wine.”

GM: “He’s hurt but forgives you. Neglect clock resets. Maintain contact or this happens again.”

Long-Term Projects & Campaign Integration

Long-term projects span multiple downtime periods and integrate with the campaign’s story. They give you agency to shape the world beyond reacting to the GM’s plot.

Long-Term Project Framework

Step 1: Define Goal What do you want to create, achieve, or change? Be specific.

Examples: - Establish a spy network in the capital - Build a stronghold in the wilderness - Write and publish a revolutionary manifesto - Reconcile two warring factions

Step 2: Set Progress Clock GM determines scope: 6-10 segments for major projects, 10-20 for epic ambitions.

Step 3: Identify Milestones Break the project into phases. Each milestone represents a significant step.

Example (Spy Network): - Milestone 1 (0-3 segments): Recruit initial agents - Milestone 2 (4-6 segments): Establish safe houses and communication - Milestone 3 (7-10 segments): Infiltrate key institutions

Step 4: Progress Through Play Progress happens via downtime activities, side quests, or main story events that align with your project.

Step 5: Handle Setbacks Failures or complications may introduce a “Setback” clock. Fill it, and you lose progress or face a crisis.

Long-Term Project Example: Building a Stronghold

Goal: Establish a defensible base in the Wildlands where you and your allies can operate safely.

Progress Clock: “Wildlands Stronghold” (0/12)

Milestones: - Phase 1 (0-4): Secure land and clear immediate threats - Phase 2 (5-8): Construct basic fortifications - Phase 3 (9-12): Establish supply lines and defenses

Downtime 1: You negotiate with the local warlord for land rights. Roll Persuasion, DC 16. Success → Progress 2/12.

Downtime 2: You hire workers and begin construction. Cost: 500 gold. Progress 4/12 (Milestone 1 complete).

Campaign Event: During an adventure, bandits attack your construction site. You defend it. GM: “You’ve proven the site can be held. Progress 6/12.”

Downtime 3: You establish a supply route with a friendly merchant. Roll Social Networking, DC 14. Success → Progress 8/12 (Milestone 2 complete).

Downtime 4: You fortify the walls and train guards. Cost: 300 gold. Progress 10/12.

Campaign Event: A rival faction attacks your stronghold. You repel them, proving its strategic value. Progress 12/12 (Complete!).

Result: Your stronghold is operational. Benefits: - Safe haven for downtime activities - Base of operations for regional adventures - Source of story hooks (defending it, expanding influence)

Campaign Integration Tips

Coordinate with GM: Discuss how your project fits the campaign. The GM might tie it to main story events or use it to create new plot threads.

Accept Complications: Projects rarely go smoothly. Rivals, resource shortages, and unexpected alliances make them interesting. Embrace setbacks as story opportunities.

Share Spotlight: If other players pursue projects, support them. Collaborative projects (shared stronghold, joint business) create party cohesion and shared stakes.

Use Projects for Downtime: Long-term projects give downtime purpose. Instead of “I rest for two weeks,” you say “I advance my spy network while the party recovers.”

Working with Your GM

Propose your goals. Tell the GM what you want to accomplish and why it matters to your character.

Negotiate scope. The GM will frame what’s feasible in the time available and what costs or risks are involved.

Roll when stakes matter. Simple downtime actions (rest, routine training) don’t require rolls. Complex projects (rare research, dangerous networking) may require checks.

Downtime is collaborative. Work with your GM to create meaningful opportunities.

Roleplaying Guidance

Roleplaying Fundamentals

Roleplaying means inhabiting your character and making choices through their perspective. You’re not playing to win; you’re playing to create compelling stories.

Speak as your character. React to the world. Make decisions based on who they are, not just tactical advantage.

Creating Memorable Characters

Distinct Voice: Give your character a recognizable way of speaking or acting. Terse and direct. Verbose and academic. Cautious and skeptical. Voice makes them memorable.

Clear Motivations: What drives your character? Revenge, justice, curiosity, survival, wealth, redemption. Motivations guide choices and create drama when they conflict with others’ goals.

Flaws and Strengths: Perfect characters are boring. Give yours flaws— arrogance, distrust, recklessness, stubbornness. Flaws create complications. Strengths show what they excel at. Both make them human.

Examples: A cynical investigator who doesn’t trust authority. A brave knight who’s too proud to ask for help. A scholar who freezes under pressure but excels with preparation.

Engaging with Fiction

Describe Actions Vividly: Don’t just say “I attack.” Say “I lunge low, aiming for the guard’s knee.” Vivid descriptions make scenes come alive.

React to Outcomes: When the GM describes a result, respond. If you fail to pick the lock, show frustration or improvise a new plan. If you succeed but alert the guard, react to the complication.

Build on Details: When the GM or another player adds a detail, incorporate it. “The tavern smells like smoke”—“I cough and pull my cloak over my face.”

Making Meaningful Choices

Choose What Your Character Would Do: Sometimes the optimal tactical choice isn’t what your character would do. If they’re protective, they’ll risk themselves for allies. If they’re vengeful, they’ll pursue revenge over safety.

Embrace Consequences: Partial Success and Failure drive stories. Don’t avoid risk to prevent complications. Complications create drama and opportunity.

Trust Your Group: Make choices that create space for others. Share spotlight. Support their arcs. Collaborative storytelling means everyone shines.

Scene-Specific Roleplaying

Combat Scenes

Describe Actions with Character Flair: Your fighter charges headlong, roaring a battle cry. Your rogue circles cautiously, looking for openings. Your tactician barks orders while covering allies. Combat is character expression.

React to Wounds and Setbacks: When your resolve clock ticks, show it. Grimace, stagger, show desperation or fury. When an ally falls, react—rage, fear, determination.

Use Tactical Choices to Show Character: Protecting the wounded shows loyalty. Pursuing a fleeing enemy shows determination or vengeance. Holding ground while others retreat shows courage or stubbornness. Mechanics reveal character.

Example:

You (as Elara): “The bandit lunges at the scholar. I can’t let that happen— I dive between them, blades up, and shout ‘You’ll have to go through me first!’”

GM: “He takes the bait, turning on you. Roll to Defend—DC 16.”

You roll 14 (Partial Success).

GM: “You block his strike, but the force drives you back. You’re now Exposed— Disadvantage on defense next round. But the scholar is safe and fleeing.”

You: “Worth it. I grit my teeth and ready for his next strike.”

Social Scenes

Speak in Character Voice: Use phrases or speech patterns that fit your character. A street urchin talks differently than a noble. Consistent voice makes your character recognizable.

Show Don’t Tell Emotions: Instead of “I’m angry,” describe clenched fists or a cold stare. Instead of “I’m scared,” describe shallow breathing or avoiding eye contact.

React to NPC Body Language: When the GM describes an NPC’s hesitation or defensive posture, respond. “I notice he’s avoiding my eyes. I soften my tone and say, ‘You’re not in trouble. I just need to know what you saw.’”

Example:

GM: “The merchant leans back, arms crossed. ‘I don’t know anything about that shipment.’”

You (as Marcus): “I notice the way he glances at the back room. I pull out a datastick and slide it across the table. ‘This has evidence that clears you of involvement. But I need names—who paid you to look the other way?’”

GM: “Roll Persuasion, DC 16.”

You roll 17 (Full Success).

GM: “He hesitates, then nods slowly. ‘Varren Corp. They said it was routine cargo. I didn’t ask questions.’ He’s cooperating now.”

Exploration & Investigation Scenes

Describe Your Approach: Are you cautious or bold? Methodical or intuitive? “I check each crate carefully, listening for movement” versus “I stride in and call out ‘Show yourself!’”

React to Clues and Danger: When you find blood on the floor, show concern or determination. When a trap almost triggers, show relief or heightened caution.

Build Theories Aloud: Share your character’s reasoning. “This doesn’t make sense—why leave the door unlocked unless they wanted us to enter?” Speculation creates engagement.

Example:

GM: “You find a hidden compartment in the desk. Inside is a letter—coded, but you recognize the seal.”

You (as Kaida): “My hands shake. That’s an OmniCorp executive seal—this goes higher than I thought. I photograph it with my neural interface and pocket the original. We need to move—if they’re watching, they know we’re close.”

GM: “As you turn to leave, you hear footsteps in the hall. Pressure clock ticks to 3/4.”

You: “Damn. I motion to the crew—‘Back exit, now.’ We move fast and quiet.”

Maintaining Character Consistency

Track Your Character’s Growth: Characters change through play. The cynical investigator who starts trusting allies. The reckless fighter who learns caution. Let your character evolve, but track why they change.

Revisit Motivations: Check in with your character’s goals. Are they still pursuing revenge? Have they found redemption? Motivations shift—acknowledge it in play.

Use Flaws Consistently: If your character is proud, they don’t suddenly ask for help without cause. If they’re loyal, they don’t abandon allies. Flaws create predictability and dramatic tension when tested.

Avoid Whiplash Shifts: Gradual change feels earned. Sudden shifts (“I’m suddenly ruthless after being kind for six sessions”) break immersion. Let pivotal moments justify changes.

Handling Roleplaying Challenges

When You’re Stuck for Character Voice

Pick One Trait and Lean In: Focus on a single defining trait—cynical, hopeful, cautious, bold. Let that guide your responses until you find more nuance.

Borrow from Fiction: Think of a character from media who fits your concept. What would they say? Adapt it to your character.

Summarize Third-Person: “My character would be suspicious here, so she hangs back and watches.” Narration is valid roleplaying.

When Drama Conflicts with Tactics

Choose Drama if the Stakes Feel Right: If failing dramatically creates better story than succeeding tactically, go for it. Trust your group to support the choice.

Discuss Meta When Needed: “My character would chase them, but that splits the party—should we talk this through?” Collaborating beats rigid adherence.

When Spotlight Feels Unbalanced

Create Openings for Others: “I’m stumped—does anyone else have ideas?” or “I can’t do this alone—who’s with me?”

Step Back When Appropriate: If you’ve been center stage, let others lead. Support their scenes with reactions and assistance.

Check In Out-of-Character: “I feel like I’ve been talking a lot—does anyone want to jump in?”

Alternative Play Modes

The standard table configuration—one GM and 3-5 players, meeting in person—is just one way to play. This chapter covers alternative configurations: solo play (no GM), duet play (1 GM + 1 player), GM-less play (rotating authority), and online play (virtual tabletops). Each mode requires adapting the system’s tools, but the core mechanics remain consistent.

Solo Play

Playing without a GM is possible using oracle mechanics, random tables, and structured decision frameworks. Solo play requires you to wear both hats—player and GM—which changes how you approach challenges, pacing, and narrative structure.

Oracle Mechanics

Oracles answer yes/no questions when you need the world to respond to your actions. Instead of a GM ruling, you roll to determine outcomes or facts.

Basic Oracle (d6):

Roll 1d6 when you ask a yes/no question:

  • 6: Yes, and… (extra benefit)
  • 4-5: Yes
  • 3: Yes, but… (complication)
  • 2: No, but… (silver lining)
  • 1: No, and… (additional problem)

When to Use:

  • “Is the guard asleep?” Roll oracle.
  • “Do I know someone in this city?” Roll oracle.
  • “Is the door unlocked?” Roll oracle.

Oracles don’t replace skill checks. Use them for environmental questions, NPC behavior, or world state. Use skill checks for your character’s actions.

Example:

You: “I approach the merchant’s shop. Is it open this late?”

Oracle Roll: 2 (No, but…)

Interpretation: The shop is closed, but you notice a light on in the back room—someone’s still there. You could knock.

Advanced Oracle: Weighted Questions

For questions with likelihood, adjust the oracle:

Likely (roll with Advantage): “Is the nobleman at the gala?” (He’s a regular—Advantage makes ‘Yes’ more probable)

Unlikely (roll with Disadvantage): “Is there a hidden escape tunnel?” (Rare—Disadvantage makes ‘No’ more probable)

Certain: Skip the roll. If it’s obvious (“Is the sun up at noon?”), don’t roll.

NPC Behavior Tables

When you encounter an NPC, roll to determine their disposition and goals. This prevents you from creating only helpful or hostile NPCs.

NPC Disposition (d6):

  • 1: Hostile (attacks, refuses, sabotages)
  • 2: Unfriendly (suspicious, demands payment, unhelpful)
  • 3: Cautious (neutral, needs convincing)
  • 4: Neutral (willing to talk, no strong opinion)
  • 5: Friendly (helpful, offers info or aid)
  • 6: Enthusiastic (eager to help, volunteers extra)

NPC Goals (d6):

  • 1: Profit (wants money, trade, or valuable info)
  • 2: Safety (avoids danger, seeks protection)
  • 3: Status (cares about reputation, honor, respect)
  • 4: Knowledge (curious, seeks information or secrets)
  • 5: Connection (lonely, wants companionship or allies)
  • 6: Duty (bound by obligation, follows rules or orders)

Example:

You: “I approach the tavern keeper for info about the missing merchant.”

Disposition Roll: 3 (Cautious)

Goal Roll: 1 (Profit)

Interpretation: The tavern keeper knows something but won’t talk for free. They hint at information, then wait for you to offer coin. You can negotiate, intimidate, or walk away.

Surprise and Discovery Tables

Solo play benefits from unexpected twists. When exploring or investigating, roll for surprises to keep play dynamic.

Discovery Table (d6):

  • 1: Setback (obstacle, trap, or complication)
  • 2: Empty (nothing useful, but no harm)
  • 3: Clue (progress toward goal)
  • 4: Resource (useful item, ally, or info)
  • 5: Opportunity (shortcut, advantage, or lucky break)
  • 6: Twist (unexpected revelation that changes context)

When to Use:

  • Searching a room
  • Traveling through wilderness
  • Investigating a mystery
  • Exploring unknown areas

Example:

You: “I search the abandoned warehouse for clues about the smugglers.”

Discovery Roll: 6 (Twist)

Interpretation: You find a ledger, but it’s coded in a cipher you don’t recognize. Worse—the entries mention your employer’s name. They’re connected to the smugglers. The mystery just got more complicated.

Self-Sufficient Character Builds

Solo play rewards generalist characters who handle diverse challenges without party support. Specialists excel in groups but struggle alone.

Recommended Skill Spread:

  • 1 Combat Skill (Melee or Ranged): Handle threats
  • 1 Social Skill (Persuasion, Deception, Insight): Negotiate and gather info
  • 1 Investigation Skill (Perception, Research): Find clues and solve puzzles
  • 1 Technical/Survival Skill (Security, Survival, Medicine): Handle practical challenges

Avoid Narrow Specialization:

If your only skills are combat-focused, non-combat scenes become frustrating. If you specialize in social skills, solo combat becomes deadly. Spread your capabilities across domains.

Proficiencies for Solo Play:

  • Self-Reliant: Bonus to Survival, Medicine, or Crafting (handle injuries and gear maintenance)
  • Streetwise: Access to contacts and information (replaces party information-gathering)
  • Combat Veteran: Bonus in fights (no party backup means you need combat edge)
  • Quick Thinker: Bonus to initiative or rerolls (act first, survive longer)

Example Solo-Friendly Build:

Character: Kade, the Wandering Investigator

Skills: Ranged Combat, Persuasion, Perception, Survival Proficiencies: Streetwise (contacts), Self-Reliant (first aid), Quick Thinker (initiative bonus)

Why It Works: Kade can fight at range (safer than melee), talk his way out of trouble, investigate scenes, and survive wilderness travel. He’s not the best at any one thing, but he’s competent across challenges common in solo play.

Pacing and Clocks in Solo Play

Progress Clocks track goals. Pressure Clocks track dangers. In solo play, you set both clocks yourself based on narrative logic.

Progress Clock Guidelines:

  • Simple goals: 4 segments (find a contact, investigate a scene)
  • Complex goals: 6 segments (solve a mystery, infiltrate a stronghold)
  • Epic goals: 8-10 segments (defeat a major antagonist, uncover conspiracy)

Pressure Clock Guidelines:

  • Immediate threats: 4 segments (guards arrive, enemy closes in)
  • Long-term threats: 6-8 segments (villain’s plan unfolds, disease spreads)

Tick Progress on Full Success, Pressure on Partial/Fail.

Example:

Goal: Locate the hidden rebel base (Progress Clock: 0/6)

Danger: Inquisitors hunting you (Pressure Clock: 0/6)

Scene 1: You question a contact in the market. Roll Persuasion vs. DC 14.

Roll: 16 (Full Success)

Result: Progress ticks to 1/6. The contact gives you a lead—check the old mill. Pressure doesn’t tick.

Scene 2: You sneak to the mill. Roll Stealth vs. DC 15.

Roll: 13 (Partial Success)

Result: Progress ticks to 2/6 (you find evidence rebels were here), but someone spots you leaving. Pressure ticks to 1/6 (inquisitors get a lead).

Continue until Progress fills (success) or Pressure fills (failure/escape).

Solo Play Pitfalls

Over-Generous Oracles: Don’t roll oracle to confirm what you want. If you want the door unlocked, that’s wish fulfillment, not play. Roll oracle when the answer genuinely matters and you don’t know.

Ignoring Consequences: Don’t erase failed rolls or complications. Embrace setbacks—they create story tension. Solo play without failure becomes boring.

Analysis Paralysis: Don’t overthink every decision. Set a timer (2 minutes) for major choices. If you can’t decide, roll a die and commit. Forward momentum beats perfect planning.

Lack of Surprise: Use random tables liberally. If you pre-plan everything, you’re not playing—you’re writing. Let tables surprise you. Embrace unexpected results.

Solo Play Example: Full Scene

Setup: You’re investigating a poisoning. Progress clock “Identify Culprit” (0/4). Pressure clock “Assassin Escapes” (0/4).

Scene 1—Crime Scene Investigation:

You: “I search the victim’s room for clues. Is anything obviously out of place?”

Oracle Roll (Weighted—Likely, with Advantage): 5 (Yes)

Interpretation: You spot a shattered vial under the bed.

Skill Check: Roll Perception + RSN vs. DC 14.

Roll: 17 (Full Success)

GM Hat: Progress ticks to 1/4. The vial has a distinctive seal—House Vyreth’s apothecary.

Scene 2—Tracking the Source:

You: “I head to House Vyreth’s apothecary. Are they open at this hour?”

Oracle Roll: 2 (No, but…)

Interpretation: Closed, but lights are on inside. Someone’s there.

You: “I knock loudly and demand entry, showing my investigator’s badge. Do they let me in?”

NPC Disposition Roll: 3 (Cautious)

NPC Goal Roll: 2 (Safety)

Interpretation: They crack the door, nervous. “What do you want?” They’re scared, not hostile.

Skill Check: Roll Presence (intimidation) vs. DC 15.

Roll: 14 (Partial Success)

GM Hat: Progress ticks to 2/4 (they admit selling the poison), but they panic and try to flee. Pressure ticks to 2/4 (assassin learns you’re closing in).

Scene 3—Chasing the Lead:

You: “I grab them before they bolt. Do they resist?”

Oracle Roll: 1 (No, and…)

Interpretation: They collapse, sobbing. “Lady Mirelle forced me! She’ll kill my family if I talk!”

GM Hat: Progress ticks to 3/4. You have the culprit’s name. Pressure ticks to 3/4 (Lady Mirelle knows you’re hunting her—she’s preparing to flee).

Scene 4—Confrontation:

You: “I rush to Lady Mirelle’s estate. Is she still there?”

Oracle Roll (Unlikely—Disadvantage): 3 (Yes, but…)

Interpretation: She’s there, but packing. You have minutes.

Skill Check: Roll AGI to sprint and intercept her before she escapes, DC 16.

Roll: 18 (Full Success)

GM Hat: Progress ticks to 4/4 (clock complete). You catch her at the door, ledger in hand. Guards arrive. Case solved. Pressure clock stops at 3/4—you got her just in time.

Key Takeaways:

  • Oracles determined world state (shop open? Lady Mirelle still there?)
  • Skill checks determined your success at actions (intimidate, chase)
  • NPC tables created unpredictable behavior (apothecary scared, not hostile)
  • Clocks tracked dual pressures (progress vs. escape)
  • GM Hat adjudicated outcomes fairly, embracing complications

Solo play works when you balance player goals with honest GMing. Don’t cheat yourself—embrace the unknown.

Duet Play (1 GM + 1 Player)

Duet play—one GM, one player—creates intimate, character-focused stories. With only one PC, pacing shifts: downtime matters more, character development deepens, and stakes feel personal. The system adapts easily, but GMs and players need to adjust expectations.

Character Design for Duet Play

Without a party, your character needs broader capabilities:

Generalist Over Specialist: Like solo play, duet characters need combat, social, and investigation skills. Pure specialists (all combat, no social) struggle when challenges fall outside their domain.

Virtual Player Characters (VPCs): The GM might provide a recurring VPC—a companion who travels with you. VPCs are loyal but less capable than full PCs. They handle gaps in your skill set (you’re a fighter, they’re a healer; you’re a socialite, they’re muscle). VPCs follow your lead but aren’t replacements for party dynamics. See GM Toolkit Chapter 09 for VPC creation guidance.

Higher Power Level: Some GMs boost starting attributes (+1 to all) or grant extra proficiencies to compensate for lack of party support. Discuss with your GM.

Pacing Adjustments

Slower Advancement: With one player, sessions focus deeply on your character’s arc. Advancement might trigger every 2-3 sessions instead of 3-4, since individual spotlight time is concentrated.

Shorter Combats: Resolve clocks for enemies should be smaller (2-4 segments instead of 4-6). Duet combat drags without party action economy, so fights resolve faster.

Emphasis on Relationships: NPCs matter more. Your character’s connections— allies, rivals, mentors, enemies—drive the story. Invest in relationships; they create narrative momentum.

Lower Lethality: One PC means no backup. GMs should telegraph danger clearly and offer escape routes. Failure should complicate, not kill, unless stakes were explicitly established.

Duet Play Example

Setup: You’re a freelance investigator hired to find a missing scholar. Your VPC companion, Marcus (loyal assistant, handles research and first aid), accompanies you.

Scene: You arrive at the scholar’s apartment. The door is ajar.

GM: “The apartment is dark. You hear movement inside—someone’s searching the place. Marcus whispers, ‘Could be the scholar, or whoever took them.’ What do you do?”

You: “I signal Marcus to stay back and peek inside. Do I see who’s searching?”

Skill Check: Roll Perception + RSN vs. DC 13.

Roll: 15 (Full Success)

GM: “A cloaked figure rifles through papers. They pocket something—a letter. You notice their belt: City Watch insignia. This isn’t a burglar; it’s a guard covering something up.”

Key Differences from Group Play:

  • Marcus (VPC) provides backup but doesn’t act independently—he follows your lead
  • Pacing is slower: GM describes details because you’re the only observer
  • Stakes are clear: GM telegraphs danger (guard is armed, but unaware of you)
  • Choices matter more: No party debate—your decision drives the scene

Duet play rewards deep character investment and tight GM-player collaboration.

GM-less Play (Rotating Authority)

GM-less play distributes GMing responsibilities across all players. One player might frame a scene, another adjudicates a roll, a third portrays an NPC. This mode requires trust, clear communication, and structured turn-taking to avoid chaos.

Authority Rotation Models

Scene Framing: One player sets the scene (“We’re in a tavern. A hooded stranger approaches”). Others play their characters. Scene framer doesn’t have final say on outcomes—group consensus or dice decide.

Round-Robin GMing: Rotate GM duties each session or scene. Player A GMs Scene 1, Player B GMs Scene 2, etc. The “current GM” adjudicates DCs, portrays NPCs, and frames consequences. When it’s your turn to GM, your PC takes a backseat (VPC status—present but not in spotlight).

Collaborative Adjudication: When outcomes are uncertain, discuss as a group. “Does the guard believe my lie?” Group votes or defers to dice (oracle roll). Avoid arguing—set a 30-second timer for decisions.

Tools for GM-less Play

Oracle Rolls (Group Version): Use solo play oracles (d6 yes/no tables) for world questions. “Is the vault guarded?” Someone rolls, everyone interprets together.

Pre-Set DCs: Before play, agree on DC ranges: Easy (10-12), Moderate (13-15), Hard (16-18). When someone declares an action, the group agrees on difficulty before rolling.

NPC Behavior Tables: Use solo play NPC disposition/goal tables. When a new NPC appears, roll for disposition and goals. Whoever rolled portrays them for that scene.

Progress and Pressure Clocks: Visible to everyone. Any player can suggest ticking a clock; group consensus or GM-for-scene decides.

GM-less Play Pitfalls

Lack of Narrative Drive: Without a GM to push conflict, scenes stall. Assign one player as “conflict advocate” each session—their job is to introduce complications when things feel too easy.

Player Favoritism: Don’t soft-adjudicate for yourself. If it’s your turn to GM and your PC attempts something risky, let someone else set the DC or adjudicate the outcome.

Analysis Paralysis: Group decisions take time. Use timers (30 seconds for minor choices, 2 minutes for major) to maintain momentum.

Unbalanced Spotlight: Track who’s had focus. If one player dominates three scenes, shift spotlight deliberately. “Let’s frame a scene for [quieter player].”

GM-less Example

Setup: Three players (Alice, Bob, Clara) playing freelance mercenaries. They’re negotiating payment with a noble for a bodyguard job.

Alice (Scene Framer): “We’re in the noble’s manor. He’s nervous—tapping his fingers, glancing at the door. Bob, you’re leading negotiations. What do you say?”

Bob: “I lean forward. ‘We’ve heard rumors about threats on your life. We’re the best, but the best costs. Five hundred gold, half upfront.’”

Clara (Current GM): “He hesitates. ‘Three hundred. That’s my limit.’ This is a negotiation contest. Roll Presence vs. his Presence. I’ll roll for him. DC 15.”

Bob rolls: 13 (Partial Success)

Clara (GM): “He agrees to four hundred but adds a condition: ‘You stay until the gala next week, not just tonight.’ Pressure clock ‘Noble’s Enemies Act’ starts at 0/4. You just committed to a longer job.”

Key Differences:

  • Alice framed the scene but didn’t GM it
  • Clara adjudicated the roll and set consequences
  • Group decided together that a pressure clock made sense
  • Bob accepted the Partial outcome without arguing

GM-less play works when everyone commits to collaborative storytelling and fair adjudication.

Online Play

Playing over the internet using virtual tabletops (VTTs) or voice/video chat requires adapting communication, tools, and pacing. The core mechanics stay the same, but technical and social considerations shift.

Platform Choices

Voice/Video Only (Discord, Zoom):

  • Pros: Simple setup, no learning curve, flexible for theater-of-the-mind play
  • Cons: No visual aids, harder to track initiative/clocks, relies on verbal description
  • Best For: Low-prep games, investigation/social-heavy campaigns

Virtual Tabletops (Roll20, Foundry VTT, Owlbear Rodeo):

  • Pros: Visual maps, token movement, integrated dice rollers, shared character sheets
  • Cons: Setup time, learning curve, potential for technical issues
  • Best For: Combat-heavy games, tactical play, groups that like visual reference

Hybrid (Voice + Shared Docs):

  • Pros: Flexibility, shared clocks/notes in Google Docs, voice chat for play
  • Cons: Requires discipline (everyone updates docs), no map visuals
  • Best For: Medium crunch, investigation-focused play

Communication Best Practices

Establish Speaking Order: Without in-person body language, people talk over each other. Use “speaking order” in tense scenes (GM calls on players) or “raise hand” features in video tools.

State Intent Clearly: “I want to sneak past the guard using the shadows” is clearer than “I roll Stealth” in text-only environments. Typing intent before rolling prevents confusion.

Use Shared Resources: Post progress/pressure clocks in shared docs or VTT notes so everyone sees current state. This prevents “wait, how many segments are filled?” questions.

Camera Usage: Video isn’t mandatory but helps. Seeing faces improves engagement and lets the GM read reactions. If someone’s camera-shy, that’s fine—voice-only works.

Break More Often: Online fatigue is real. Take 10-minute breaks every 90 minutes, not just at the 2-hour mark. Stretch, hydrate, rest eyes.

Technical Considerations

Dice Rolling: Use VTT built-in rollers, Discord bots (Avrae, Sidekick), or honor-system self-rolls. Trust your group unless you’ve had problems.

Latency and Lag: If someone has poor internet, simplify visual assets (fewer high-res maps, lighter VTT scenes). Consider text-first play for critical scenes if lag is severe.

Session Zero for Tools: Test your VTT or voice platform in Session Zero. Make sure everyone can connect, roll dice, and hear each other. Fix issues before play starts.

Backup Plans: Have a secondary communication method (Discord backup if Zoom fails). Save VTT sessions frequently to avoid data loss.

Pacing Adjustments

Shorter Sessions: 2-3 hours works better online than 4-hour marathons. Fatigue hits faster without in-person energy.

Clearer Scene Transitions: “We’re moving to a new scene—you’re now at the docks” is more necessary online. Without physical presence, players miss GM body language signaling transitions.

Visual Aids: Use images (Pinterest, AI-generated art, reference photos) for NPCs, locations, or key items. Drop them in chat. Online play lacks in-person gestures; images compensate.

Explicit Recaps: Start each session with a 2-minute recap of last session’s events. Memory fades more online—nobody sees your GM notes between sessions.

Online-Specific Pitfalls

Multitasking Players: It’s tempting to browse, text, or game during online sessions. Establish norms: phones down, no alt-tabbing during play (or at least minimize it).

Mic Discipline: Mute when not speaking (background noise is distracting). Use push-to-talk if your environment is loud.

Tech Troubleshooting Mid-Session: If someone can’t connect, don’t halt play for 20 minutes. Give them 5 minutes to rejoin; if they can’t, narrate their PC as “busy with another task” and loop them in later.

Lack of Social Energy: Online play can feel flat. Compensate with music, ambient sound (rain, tavern noise), and enthusiastic GMing. Energy doesn’t translate automatically through screens—you have to project it.

Online Play Example

Setup: Four players on Roll20 with Discord voice. GM shares a city map and places tokens.

GM (in Discord): “You’re outside the warehouse. Rain is pouring—Disadvantage to Perception checks. I’ve placed your tokens. Mira, what do you do?”

Mira (typing in Roll20 chat): “I move to the side door and check if it’s locked.”

GM: “Roll Security. DC 14.”

Mira (using Roll20 dice roller): [Rolls 16] “Got it. 16 total.”

GM: “The lock clicks open. You’re in. The warehouse is dark—three crates in the center, a figure moving in the back. Everyone roll Initiative.”

GM (sharing an image in Discord): [Posts reference image of a shadowy warehouse interior]

Key Differences:

  • Typed intent (Mira in chat) clarifies actions
  • Shared map shows positions visually
  • Integrated rolls (Roll20 roller) keep dice transparent
  • Visual aids (warehouse image) enhance immersion
  • Clear narration (GM describes explicitly, no body language cues)

Online play requires intentional communication and technical fluency, but the game itself remains unchanged.

Player Tips for Success

Planning & Preparation

Before Sessions: Review your character sheet. Check goals, unresolved plot threads, and active conditions. Know what you want to pursue.

Take Notes: Track NPCs, locations, clues, and promises. You won’t remember everything. Notes prevent missed opportunities.

Set Goals: What does your character want this session? Clear goals focus your choices and give the GM material to work with.

Tactical Tips

Use Terrain: Seek Elevated positions for ranged attacks. Fight in Dim Light for stealth. Create Fragile Cover with Set Up actions. Terrain is a tool.

Manage Resources: Don’t hoard gear or favors. Spend them when they create advantage. Unused resources are wasted resources.

Prioritize Threats: Clear dangerous conditions (Bleeding, Exposed) fast. Focus fire on high-priority enemies. Control space with maneuvers.

Coordinate Actions: Set Up for allies. Create openings. Combine tactics. Group coordination multiplies effectiveness.

Reading the Table

GM Cues: When the GM lingers on a detail, it matters. “The guard seems nervous” invites investigation. “The floor creaks” warns of danger.

NPC Intentions: Watch how NPCs react. Hesitation, eye contact, defensive posture—these signal motivations. Ask Insight checks when uncertain.

Opportunities: Partial Success creates openings. Failure escalates tension but adds new information. Outcomes aren’t dead ends; they’re hooks for next actions.

Collaborative Success

Support Each Other: If someone’s pinned down, create an opening. If someone’s investigating, offer assistance. Shared victories feel better.

Communicate Plans: “I’ll distract the guard; you slip past.” Coordination requires communication.

Celebrate Wins Together: When someone pulls off a brilliant move, recognize it. Table energy builds momentum.

Advanced Tactical Thinking

Action Economy Optimization

Eliminate Threats Fast: The faster you reduce enemy numbers, the fewer actions they get per round. Focus fire on wounded enemies to even the odds.

Use Set Up Actions for Compound Advantage: One Set Up action grants Advantage to multiple allies if they coordinate. “I suppress the sniper—who’s taking the shot?”

Force Disadvantage on Key Enemies: If you can’t kill the boss fast, make them roll with Disadvantage. Maneuvers (blind, suppress, trip) reduce their effectiveness.

Control Space with Positioning: Occupy choke points, hold high ground, block escape routes. Positioning limits enemy options without spending actions.

Resource Management

Spend Resources on Turning Points: Don’t hoard favors or gear for “emergencies.” Emergencies are happening now. Use advantages when they matter.

Leverage Contacts and Relationships: If you’ve built relationships with NPCs (see Chapter 10), call in favors when stuck. NPCs can provide intel, gear, or backup.

Track Conditions and Recovery: Don’t ignore Exhausted or Bleeding. Clear conditions before they compound. One bad condition can cascade into worse outcomes.

Plan for Downtime: Use downtime between missions to train, research, craft, or build relationships. Downtime investment pays off in future missions.

Problem-Solving Strategies

When Combat Goes Badly

Retreat is Tactical: If outnumbered or outmatched, use Defend/Withdraw to break contact. Live to fight another day with better preparation.

Change the Terrain: Create Hazardous zones (knock over oil lamps, trigger traps, collapse structures). Even the odds by making the environment dangerous.

Exploit Enemy Weaknesses: If they’re grouped, use area effects. If they’re reckless, bait them into traps. If they’re cautious, press aggressively.

Call for Help: If you have allies (contacts, VPCs, nearby friendlies), call them in. Don’t die alone when help exists.

When Investigation Stalls

Revisit Known Clues: Look for connections you missed. “The guard mentioned the docks—didn’t we find shipping manifests earlier?”

Ask NPCs Direct Questions: If stuck, go back to NPCs and ask directly. “We know you’re hiding something. What aren’t you telling us?”

Use Downtime to Research: If progress stalls, spend downtime researching (see Chapter 10). Tick progress on investigation clocks during downtime.

Take Risks for Information: Sometimes you need to provoke a reaction. Confront suspects, stake out locations, or follow leads even if dangerous.

When Social Situations Turn Hostile

De-escalate with Concessions: Offer something they want (information, favors, compromise). De-escalation prevents violence when outmatched.

Leverage Reputation or Threats: If you’ve built reputation, use it. “You know who I work for—do you really want this fight?”

Change the Frame: Reframe the negotiation. “We’re not enemies—we both want the same thing.” Find common ground.

Know When to Walk Away: If negotiation fails, disengage. Not every social conflict needs resolution right now.

Session Zero and Table Expectations

Setting Expectations

Discuss Play Style: Are you playing for tactical challenge, narrative drama, exploration, or social intrigue? Align expectations early.

Establish Boundaries: Use safety tools (X-Card, Lines & Veils) to define content boundaries. Everyone should feel safe at the table.

Agree on Tone: Gritty noir? Heroic fantasy? Lighthearted adventure? Tone affects how you approach scenes.

Clarify Spotlight Sharing: How much “screentime” does each player get? Some tables rotate evenly; others follow narrative flow. Discuss preferences.

Building Party Cohesion

Create Shared Backstory: Why is your party together? Shared goals, mutual debts, common enemies, or circumstantial alliance. Define it before play starts.

Establish Trust Early: If characters don’t trust each other, the party fractures. Build trust through early shared challenges or backstory connections.

Respect Character Concepts: Don’t undermine others’ concepts. If someone plays a diplomat, let them negotiate. If someone plays a rogue, let them scout.

Handling Disagreements

Distinguish Character vs. Player Conflict: In-character disagreements are fine. Player disagreements need out-of-character resolution.

Talk Through Impasses: If players can’t agree on a plan, pause and discuss meta. “What are we trying to accomplish here?”

GM as Tiebreaker: If stuck, let the GM frame stakes and move forward. “You can debate, but the pressure clock is ticking—what do you do?”

Spotlight Sharing and Table Dynamics

Recognizing When to Step Forward

Your Expertise is Needed: If the situation matches your skills (combat for fighters, investigation for detectives), step up. That’s your moment.

Others Are Stuck: If the table is silent or uncertain, propose a plan. “I think we should—” breaks paralysis.

Your Character Has Motivation: If the scene aligns with your character’s goals or fears, take the lead. Personal stakes create engagement.

Recognizing When to Step Back

Someone Else’s Expertise Fits: If another character’s skills are relevant, let them lead. Support them with Set Up actions or assistance.

You’ve Been Center Stage: If you’ve dominated the last 2-3 scenes, step back. Let others shine.

The Scene is Someone Else’s Arc: If a scene is about another character’s backstory or motivation, be the supporting cast. Ask questions, offer help, react.

Handling Quiet Players

Create Openings: “What do you think, [Character Name]?” or “Your character has experience with this—what’s your take?”

Offer Assists: “I’ll help you with that—let’s combine our efforts.” Shared actions reduce pressure on quiet players.

Respect Different Play Styles: Some players are quieter by nature. That’s fine. Don’t force them into spotlight they don’t want.

How to Read a Character Sheet

A character sheet captures:

  • Concept & Attributes: Who you are and your core capabilities (MIG/AGI/ RSN/PRE).
  • Skills & Proficiencies: What you’re trained in and what permissions you have.
  • Gear & Resources: What you carry and what narrative tools you can leverage.
  • Connections & Hooks: Who you know and why you’re in the story.

Example Character: Fantasy

Name: Elara Voss Concept: Blade-dancer seeking redemption

Attributes: MIG +1, AGI +2, RSN +0, PRE +1

Skills: Blades & Brawling, Acrobatics, Stealth, Streetwise Proficiencies: Master Duelist, Quick Reflexes

Gear: Paired short swords, light leather armor, lockpick set, rope Resources: Contact in the Thieves’ Guild (owes her a favor)

Connections: Fled noble family after exposing corruption; hunted by House Varren assassins. Traveling with a scholar who knows ancient wards that might protect her.

Hooks: The campaign opens with House Varren hiring mercenaries to track her. She needs allies and a safe haven.

Playstyle: Negotiate, deceive, manipulate. Relies on contacts and charm rather than tech skills. Pairs well with investigators who need social access.

Variant 4: Combat Marcus

Change: Boost MIG to +1, AGI to +2, reduce RSN to +1. Add Tactical Shooting proficiency.

Impact: Becomes an action-hero investigator. Less analytical, more direct confrontation.

Playstyle: Kick down doors, win shootouts, rely on instinct over planning. Pairs well with teams that need firepower.

Common Character Sheet Mistakes

Mistake 1: Over-Specialization

Error: All skills and proficiencies focused on one area (e.g., pure combat, zero social or investigation).

Problem: You’re ineffective when situations fall outside your domain. The party carries you through non-combat scenes.

Fix: Spread skills across 2-3 areas. Combat specialist? Add Perception or Tactics. Social character? Add at least one combat or utility skill.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Attributes

Error: All attributes at +0 or +1. Skills compensate for weak base stats.

Problem: Without strong attributes, your skill bonuses are undercut. You need both to succeed consistently.

Fix: Focus 1-2 attributes to +2 or higher. Let skills and proficiencies build on strong foundations.

Mistake 3: Redundant Skills

Error: Taking overlapping skills that do the same thing (Blades and Brawling both cover melee; Stealth and Infiltration are redundant).

Problem: Wasted picks. You could have diversified.

Fix: Cover different niches. One combat skill, one social, one investigation, one utility. Avoid redundancy.

Mistake 4: No Resources or Connections

Error: Gear and skills defined, but no contacts, no faction ties, no backstory hooks.

Problem: You’re disconnected from the world. No leverage, no narrative ties.

Fix: Define at least 2-3 contacts or faction relationships. These create story hooks and provide resources.

Mistake 5: Unclear Concept

Error: Stats exist, but no clear character concept. “What do you do?” “Uh… I have Blades and Stealth?”

Problem: You don’t know how to roleplay the character. Concept guides choices.

Fix: Write a one-sentence concept: Cynical detective seeking redemption, Reckless smuggler with a heart of gold, Haunted scholar running from the past. Let stats support concept, not define it.

Troubleshooting Character Creation

“My character feels weak.”

Diagnosis:

  • Check if attributes support your skills. High skill without high attribute = inconsistent success.
  • Check if you’re using proficiencies. Proficiencies grant narrative permissions and mechanical edges.
  • Check if you’re leveraging gear and resources. Contacts, tools, and favors create advantages.

Fix: Focus attributes, align skills with attributes, use proficiencies and gear actively.

“I don’t fit with the party.”

Diagnosis:

  • Check if your skills overlap too much (two investigators, no combat) or too little (no shared goals).
  • Check if backstory ties you to the party. No shared history = weak cohesion.

Fix: Adjust skills to fill gaps. Add backstory connection to at least one party member. Discuss party composition in Session Zero.

“I don’t know what to do in scenes.”

Diagnosis:

  • Check if your skills are too narrow. If you only have combat skills, non-combat scenes leave you sidelined.
  • Check if you have clear motivations. No goals = no drive.

Fix: Diversify skills. Define clear motivations (revenge, redemption, curiosity, survival). Ask GM for scenes that engage your character.

DC Ladder & Outcomes

DC Tiers

  • DC 12 (Easy): Good position with time and tools
  • DC 14 (Routine): Standard challenge, normal conditions
  • DC 16 (Tough): Notable obstacles, poor angle, time pressure
  • DC 18 (Hard): Steep odds, strong opposition, tight timing
  • DC 20 (Heroic): Exceptional challenge, stacked complications
  • DC 22 (Legendary): Near-impossible without perfect setup or luck

Outcomes

  • Full Success: Achieve your goal cleanly; progress ticks, gain position
  • Partial Success: Achieve goal with complication; cost or pressure
  • Failure: Don’t achieve goal; situation escalates or new problem emerges
  • Critical (Nat 20): Amplified success
  • Critical (Nat 1): Amplified complication

Your Turn

Order Options:

  • Conversational: GM asks “Who acts?” and rotates spotlight
  • Popcorn: You act, then name who goes next (alternate sides)

On Your Turn:

  1. Describe your action (Strike, Maneuver, Set Up, Defend/Withdraw)
  2. GM sets DC and pre-states outcomes
  3. Roll and add modifiers
  4. GM narrates result and updates state

Common Tags & Conditions

Tags (Environmental/Situational)

  • Dim Light: Advantage to stealth; Disadvantage to ranged attacks/navigation
  • Slick: Disadvantage on maneuvers and movement
  • Cramped: Hinders movement; increases fall risk
  • Fragile Cover: Grants Advantage but degrades on Partial
  • Solid Cover: Reliable protection
  • Elevated: Advantage on ranged attacks; forces opponents to climb
  • Crowded: Advantage to blend in; Disadvantage to chase

Conditions (Character States)

  • Exhausted: Reduce effectiveness until rest/aid
  • Restrained: Limit movement; clear via help, time, or leverage
  • Bleeding: Time pressure; clear via first aid or rest
  • Stunned: Can’t act next turn
  • Suppressed: Disadvantage on next action
  • Exposed: Vulnerable; Disadvantage to defense

Action Cheat Sheet

Strike: Attack opponent. Full = damage/pressure. Partial = connect but take cost. Fail = they counter or you lose position.

Maneuver: Shove, trip, disarm, reposition. Full = achieve goal. Partial = trade something. Use to create openings or control space.

Set Up: Create advantage for self or allies. Full = grant Advantage or apply helpful tag. Partial = create advantage but expose yourself.

Defend/Withdraw: Change position to safety, clear condition, break contact. Full = reposition and clear minor tag. Partial = move but give up next action.

Investigation & Social Quick Reference

Investigation: Describe what you’re doing and seeking. Full = clear evidence. Partial = useful info with complication. Fail = nothing new or trigger complication. Use Progress/Pressure clocks.

Persuasion: Full = change mind/gain trust. Partial = swayed but want something. Fail = reject or grow suspicious.

Deception: Full = believe you. Partial = believe but doubt detail. Fail = see through lie.

Reading NPCs: Use Insight. Full = learn wants and secrets. Partial = partial read or they notice.

Social Contests: Both roll when both have goals. Higher wins. GM pre-states outcomes.

Key Terms

Advantage: Roll two dice, keep the higher result. Granted by favorable positioning, gear, or assistance.

Clock: Progress tracker with segments. Tick forward on successes (Progress Clock) or escalate pressure (Pressure Clock).

Condition: Character status effect (Exhausted, Bleeding, Restrained). Impose mechanical penalties or narrative limits.

DC (Difficulty Class): Target number for checks. Ranges from DC 12 (Easy) to DC 22 (Legendary).

Disadvantage: Roll two dice, keep the lower result. Imposed by poor positioning, conditions, or obstacles.

Failure: Roll below DC. Goal not achieved; situation escalates or new complication emerges.

Full Success: Meet or beat DC. Achieve goal cleanly; tick progress, gain position.

GM (Game Master): Facilitates play, frames situations, adjudicates outcomes.

NPC (Non-Player Character): Character controlled by the GM.

Partial Success: Meet DC by 1–2 or on specific outcomes. Achieve goal with cost or complication.

Proficiency: Narrative permission and mechanical edge in specific area.

Skill: Trained competency; grants Advantage or bonuses to relevant checks.

Tag: Environmental or situational descriptor (Dim Light, Slick, Cover). Affects what you can do in a space.

VPC (Virtual Player Character): Companion character who travels with the party, GM-controlled, fills gaps in party composition. Distinct from NPCs (all other characters). See GM Toolkit Chapter 09 for VPC creation.

Resolve Clock: Progress tracker representing a character’s ability to continue acting before being taken out. Typically 4-6 segments. When filled, character is taken out (unconscious, fleeing, surrendering, or dead depending on fiction). See Chapter 06 for details.

Progress Clock: Tracker measuring advancement toward a goal. Ticks forward on successes. See Chapter 02 for full explanation.

Pressure Clock: Tracker measuring escalating danger or time pressure. Ticks forward on failures or partial successes. See Chapter 02 for full explanation.

Set Up Action: Combat action that creates advantage for self or allies. Grants Advantage or applies helpful tags/conditions. See Chapter 06.

Maneuver Action: Combat action to reposition, trip, disarm, or control space without direct damage. See Chapter 06.

Defend/Withdraw Action: Combat action to change position to safety, clear conditions, or break contact. See Chapter 06.

Chapter 19: Character Creation Overview

Creating a memorable player character requires balancing three elements: identity (who they are), mechanical foundations (what they can do), and narrative purpose (why they matter to the story). This chapter provides the framework for building characters that feel authentic at the table and drive compelling play.

Character Philosophy

Every player character in this system is built on three pillars:

Fiction-First Identity: Start with who the character is—their role, personality, motivations, and place in the world. Mechanics should emerge from and support this identity, not dictate it. A character’s concept drives their skills, proficiencies, and narrative purpose.

Mechanical Coherence: While identity comes first, mechanics matter. Skills and proficiencies should reflect what a character knows and can do. Tags and conditions should capture how they interact with the world. This guide helps you translate fictional concepts into mechanical choices that make sense at the table.

Narrative Purpose: Player characters are protagonists who drive the story forward through choices and actions. Your character needs complex motivations, meaningful relationships, and room to change. Understanding your character’s narrative purpose ensures they contribute to play in compelling ways.

Throughout the following chapters, you’ll see these three pillars in action. Worked examples show characters developing from concept (identity) through mechanical frameworks (coherence) to story integration (narrative purpose).

Identity: The Foundation

Identity is where every character begins. Before you assign a single skill point or choose proficiencies, answer these core questions:

Role: What does this character do in the world? Are they a baker, a knight, a detective, a pilot? Role grounds the character in their setting and immediately suggests skills, knowledge, and social connections.

Personality: How do they approach problems and interact with others? Are they cautious or bold, compassionate or pragmatic, methodical or impulsive? Personality shapes decision-making at the table and creates friction or synergy with other characters.

Motivation: What drives them to act? What do they want, and why does it matter to them? Motivation provides the engine for character choices and creates natural hooks for story integration.

Background: Where did they come from, and what shaped them? Background informs skills (a soldier has combat training; a scholar has academic knowledge), relationships (family, mentors, rivals), and secrets (events they hide or regrets they carry).

These questions create a framework that makes every subsequent choice easier. When you know a character’s role, personality, motivation, and background, selecting skills becomes obvious rather than arbitrary. A cautious detective investigating a murder naturally takes Investigation, Deduction, and Interview skills. A bold pilot seeking glory in the void naturally takes Piloting, Navigation, and Tactics.

Mechanical Foundations

Once you have a clear identity, translate it into mechanical choices that support and express that character at the table:

Skills: Choose 3-5 skills from the Mechanics Reference (Chapters 6-11) that reflect what your character knows and can do. Skills should flow directly from their role and background. A baker takes Cooking, Hospitality, and Negotiation. A mercenary takes Melee Combat, Tactics, and Athletics. Skills are your character’s primary mechanical expression—they determine what situations they can engage with confidently.

Proficiencies: Select 2-4 proficiencies from the Mechanics Reference (Chapters 13-17) that represent specialized knowledge, tools, or cultural competencies. Proficiencies add flavor and create advantages in specific contexts. A knight might have Court Etiquette and Armored Combat proficiencies. A hacker might have Encryption Tools and Corporate Protocols. Proficiencies create moments where your character’s specific background makes them uniquely qualified.

Tags: Tags (see Mechanics Reference, Chapter 18) describe qualities that affect position and effect in scenes. Some tags come from identity (“Helpful,” “Proud,” “Curious”), while others emerge from circumstances (“Exhausted,” “Inspired,” “By-the-Book”). Tags provide narrative texture and mechanical leverage, turning abstract qualities into concrete scene effects.

Starting Gear: Equipment should match the character’s role and resources. A wandering bard carries different tools than a corporate security chief. Gear isn’t just a shopping list—it tells a story about preparation, priorities, and access to resources.

The goal is mechanical coherence: when someone reads your character’s skill list, they should immediately understand who that character is without reading the backstory.

Fiction-First Design Process

Start with fiction, then add mechanics. Here’s the step-by-step process:

  1. Concept: Write 1-2 sentences describing who the character is, what they do, and why they matter. Example: “Tomas is a disgraced knight seeking redemption through protecting the innocent. He carries shame from a past failure but refuses to abandon his code.”

  2. Role & Personality: Define their role in the world and 2-3 personality traits. Example: “Wandering protector. Honorable, Haunted, Compassionate.”

  3. Motivation & Goals: Identify what drives them and what they want. Example: “Wants to prove his honor isn’t dead despite his failure. Seeks a cause worth dying for.”

  4. Skills: Choose 3-5 skills that let them do what their concept requires. Example: “Melee Combat, Intimidation, Investigation.”

  5. Proficiencies: Add 2-3 proficiencies for specialized knowledge. Example: “Knight’s Code, Armored Combat.”

  6. Backstory & Secrets: Create 1-2 hooks that connect them to the world and other characters. Example: “Failed to save his lord from assassination; knows the killer but can’t prove it.”

  7. Narrative Purpose: Define how they function in the story. Example: “Protagonist driven by redemption. Creates hooks around justice, honor, and second chances.”

  8. Refinement: Review the complete character. Do the mechanics support the fiction? Does the character fulfill their narrative purpose? Adjust as needed.

This process creates characters that feel authentic, play smoothly, and contribute meaningfully to your campaign.

Cross-Genre Principles

These design principles work across all genres because they focus on universal character elements rather than setting-specific details:

Role transcends genre: A “protector” archetype works as a knight (fantasy), a bodyguard (modern), a ship’s security chief (sci-fi), a neighborhood watch captain (cozy), or a haunted guardian (horror). The core identity—someone who shields others—translates directly while the mechanical expression changes to fit genre expectations.

Motivation is universal: Characters driven by duty, revenge, curiosity, greed, love, or fear work in any setting. The objects of those motivations change (protecting a village vs. protecting a space station), but the emotional core remains constant.

When adapting a character concept between genres, keep the identity and narrative purpose intact while swapping skills and proficiencies for genre-appropriate equivalents. A fantasy mage becomes a modern hacker or a sci-fi technomancer—the role (specialist who manipulates complex systems) and purpose (solve problems others can’t) remain; the tools and terminology shift.

Chapter 04: PC Archetypes by Genre

Player characters are the heart of every story. Whether baking bread in a cozy village, wielding swords in ancient ruins, investigating supernatural horrors, navigating corporate intrigue, or piloting starships across galaxies, PCs need clear identities that feel both mechanically coherent and narratively rich.

This chapter provides ready-to-use archetypes organized by genre. Each archetype includes concept, core skills, proficiencies, starting gear, backstory hooks, and examples in play. Use these as drop-in templates for quick character creation or as customization starting points for more developed characters.

For mechanical details on skills, see Mechanics Reference Guide Chapters 07-11 (genre skills). For proficiencies, see Chapters 13-17. For backstory development, see Chapter 06.

How to Use Archetypes

Archetypes are fiction-first templates that provide:

  • Concept: A clear character identity and role
  • Core Skills: 3-5 skills from the Mechanics Reference Guide that define the archetype’s capabilities
  • Proficiencies: 2-3 specialized proficiencies that support the archetype
  • Starting Gear: Genre-appropriate equipment to begin play
  • Backstory Hook: A narrative seed for character development
  • Example in Play: A brief vignette showing the archetype in action

Quick Creation: Choose an archetype, note the skills and proficiencies, select starting gear, and begin play.

Customization: Swap one or two skills for others from the same genre chapter, adjust proficiencies to fit your concept, and develop the backstory hook into a full character history (see Chapter 06).

Cross-Genre Play: Many archetypes translate across genres. A Cozy Baker becomes a Sci-Fi ship’s cook. A Fantasy Warrior becomes a Modern soldier. Use the template as a foundation and adjust skills to the new setting.

Cozy Archetypes

Cozy archetypes celebrate domestic mastery, artisan crafts, and community connection. They excel at creating comfort, building relationships, and nurturing growth.

Baker/Chef

Concept: You transform flour, butter, and patience into warmth. Your kitchen is a sanctuary, and your food brings people together. Whether running a bakery, managing an inn, or feeding travelers, you create nourishment that feeds body and spirit.

Core Skills (see Ch. 07): - Baking — Your specialty; bread, pastries, and contest-worthy creations - Cooking — Everyday meals and improvised feasts - Hospitality — Welcoming guests and creating comfort

Proficiencies (see Ch. 13): - Baker — Advantage on baking checks, knowledge of ovens and ingredients - Festival Organizer — Planning community events centered around food

Starting Gear: Professional oven or hearth access, quality baking tools, starter ingredients (flour, butter, sugar, yeast), recipe book, apron, market connections for fresh ingredients.

Backstory Hook: You learned to bake from a family member who taught you that food is love made visible. Now you run your own establishment, but you are still perfecting the one recipe they never shared—their secret signature dish.

Example in Play: The village festival is tomorrow, and the mayor’s birthday cake must be perfect. You roll Baking (DC 18) and succeed with a 19. Your three-tier berry cake becomes the centerpiece of the celebration, earning you the Mayor’s Favor tag and a commission for the upcoming harvest feast.

Librarian/Scholar

Concept: You are the keeper of knowledge and stories. Your library is not just books—it is memory, history, and connection. You help people find answers, organize community records, and preserve what might otherwise be lost.

Core Skills (see Ch. 07): - Research — Finding information in archives and texts (use Research from Ch. 09 if available) - Comfort — Helping patrons process what they learn - Mediation — Resolving disputes over interpretations or access

Proficiencies (see Ch. 13): - Archivist — Knowledge of organization systems and rare texts - Homekeeper — Maintaining the library space and creating welcoming atmosphere

Starting Gear: Personal collection of reference books, catalog system (ledger or cards), reading lamp, comfortable chair, tea service, library key, patron records.

Backstory Hook: A mysterious section of the library was sealed by the previous librarian. You recently found the key and are deciding whether to open it—some knowledge might be better left hidden.

Example in Play: Two farmers argue over a property boundary mentioned in old land deeds. You roll Research (DC 16) and Mediation (DC 16). With a 17 and 18, you find the original survey map and broker a fair compromise. Both farmers leave satisfied, and you gain the Trusted Mediator tag.

Gardener/Naturalist

Concept: You work with the rhythms of seasons, soil, and growth. Whether tending a village garden, managing an orchard, or foraging in the wild, you understand how to coax life from the earth and respect what nature provides.

Core Skills (see Ch. 07): - Gardening — Planting, tending, and harvesting - Foraging — Finding medicinal herbs and wild foods - Animal Husbandry — Caring for chickens, bees, or livestock

Proficiencies (see Ch. 13): - Herbalist — Creating remedies and identifying useful plants - Beekeeper — Managing hives and harvesting honey

Starting Gear: Gardening tools (spade, hoe, pruners), seed collection, foraging basket, herb drying rack, beekeeper’s veil and smoker (if Beekeeper proficiency), field guide to local plants.

Backstory Hook: You inherited the garden from someone who taught you that every plant has a purpose. Recently, a rare seedling appeared—one you have never seen before. You are nurturing it, wondering what it will become.

Example in Play: A blight threatens the village vegetable gardens. You roll Gardening (DC 18) to diagnose and treat the disease. You succeed with a 19, identifying a fungal infection and creating an herbal treatment from foraged ingredients. The gardens recover, and you earn the Village Healer tag.

Fantasy Archetypes

Fantasy archetypes embody heroism, magic, and adventure. They excel at combat, exploration, and wielding supernatural forces.

Warrior

Concept: You are the shield that stands between danger and those who cannot fight. Whether a knight sworn to protect the realm, a mercenary fighting for coin, or a tribal defender, you have dedicated your life to mastering combat and enduring hardship.

Core Skills (see Ch. 08): - Melee Combat — Sword, axe, or mace; you fight at close quarters - Tactics — Reading battlefields and coordinating allies - Athletics — Climbing walls, leaping gaps, and grappling foes

Proficiencies (see Ch. 14): - Weapon Master — Advantage with your chosen weapon type - First Aid — Battlefield medicine to stabilize allies - Armor Specialist — Efficient use of heavy armor

Starting Gear: Longsword or battleaxe, shield, chainmail or splint armor, adventuring pack (rope, rations, bedroll), whetstone, bandages, travel cloak.

Backstory Hook: You failed to protect someone important once. That failure shaped you into the warrior you are today. You carry a token from that person—a reminder of why you fight.

Example in Play: Bandits ambush the caravan you are guarding. You roll Melee Combat (DC 16) to engage the leader while using Tactics (DC 16) to position allies. You succeed on both, cutting down the bandit leader and rallying the guards. The bandits flee, and you earn the Caravan Protector tag.

Mage

Concept: You have learned to see and manipulate the fundamental forces of reality. Whether studying arcane texts in wizard towers, channeling innate sorcerous power, or binding spirits through pacts, magic is both your art and your science.

Core Skills (see Ch. 08): - Arcana — Understanding and identifying magical forces - Ritual — Performing time-intensive spells and ceremonies - Perception — Noticing magical auras and hidden details

Proficiencies (see Ch. 14): - Scribe — Reading arcane texts and inscribing scrolls - Ritualist — Advantage on ritual casting and preparation

Starting Gear: Spellbook or focus item (staff, wand, crystal), component pouch, scholar’s robes, arcane reference tome, chalk for diagrams, lamp, traveling ink and quills.

Backstory Hook: You discovered magic through an unusual source—a dream, a mysterious mentor, or an artifact. That source still influences your power, and you are not entirely sure what it wants from you.

Example in Play: The party encounters a warded door blocking the crypt. You roll Arcana (DC 18) to analyze the ward, then Ritual (DC 18) to dispel it safely. You succeed with a 19 and 17—the ward dissolves, and the party proceeds without triggering traps.

Rogue

Concept: You succeed through cunning, agility, and precision. Whether a guild thief, a scout for hire, or a treasure hunter, you excel at infiltration, finding what others hide, and striking from shadows.

Core Skills (see Ch. 08): - Stealth — Moving unseen and unheard - Thievery — Picking locks, disabling traps, sleight of hand - Perception — Spotting hidden dangers and opportunities

Proficiencies (see Ch. 14): - Lockpicking — Advantage on opening locks and bypassing security - Trapfinding — Detecting and disabling mechanical traps

Starting Gear: Lockpicks, thieves’ tools, leather armor, daggers (2), grappling hook and rope, dark cloak, small mirror for checking around corners, crowbar.

Backstory Hook: You stole something valuable once, and now someone powerful wants it back. You have hidden it well, but the search is closing in. You need allies or leverage to stay ahead.

Example in Play: The party needs to infiltrate the duke’s castle during a festival. You roll Stealth (DC 16) to sneak past guards and Thievery (DC 18) to pick the vault lock. You succeed with a 17 and 19—retrieving the stolen documents without triggering alarms. The party escapes, and you earn the Master Thief tag.

Horror/Mystery Archetypes

Horror and mystery archetypes navigate fear, investigation, and the unknown. They excel at uncovering truth, surviving psychological trauma, and confronting the supernatural.

Detective

Concept: You find truth where others see only shadows. Whether a private investigator, police detective, or amateur sleuth, you piece together clues, interview witnesses, and follow threads until the mystery unravels.

Core Skills (see Ch. 09): - Deduction — Connecting clues into coherent theories - Interview — Extracting information from witnesses and suspects - Research — Finding records, files, and historical context

Proficiencies (see Ch. 15): - Detective — Advantage on investigation checks and evidence analysis - Forensics Kit — Using scientific tools to analyze crime scenes

Starting Gear: Detective’s notebook, forensic kit (magnifying glass, fingerprint powder, evidence bags), flashlight, camera, trench coat, police or PI credentials, lockpicks (unofficial).

Backstory Hook: You solved a case that haunts you. The criminal was caught, but something about the case never added up. You still investigate it in your spare time, chasing the piece that does not fit.

Example in Play: A body is found in the warehouse. You roll Forensics (DC 18) to analyze the scene, then Deduction (DC 18) to connect it to previous cases. You succeed with a 19 and 17—identifying the killer’s signature and narrowing suspects to three individuals.

Occultist

Concept: You have glimpsed the hidden world—spirits, rituals, forbidden knowledge. Whether a dabbler in forbidden texts, a practicing medium, or a reluctant inheritor of arcane secrets, you understand that some doors, once opened, cannot be closed.

Core Skills (see Ch. 09): - Occult Lore — Identifying supernatural entities and phenomena - Ritual Knowledge — Performing protective rites and banishments - Composure — Resisting fear and maintaining sanity

Proficiencies (see Ch. 15): - Occult Scholar — Advantage on occult knowledge checks - Medium — Communicating with spirits and sensing supernatural presence

Starting Gear: Occult reference texts, ritual components (candles, salt, incense, chalk), protective amulet, journal of supernatural encounters, magnifying glass, flashlight, worn leather satchel.

Backstory Hook: You learned occult knowledge from someone who warned you to never use it. But when someone you cared about was threatened by supernatural forces, you broke that promise. Now you cannot unlearn what you know.

Example in Play: A ghost haunts the abandoned mansion. You roll Occult Lore (DC 18) to identify it, then Ritual Knowledge (DC 20) to perform a banishment. You succeed with a 19 on the first, and partial (18) on the second—the ghost is weakened and confined to the attic, buying time for a stronger ritual.

Survivor

Concept: You have endured what breaks others. Whether a trauma survivor, a street-smart fugitive, or someone who escaped a nightmare, you know how to stay alive when everything goes wrong. You trust your instincts and improvise solutions.

Core Skills (see Ch. 09): - Urban Survival — Finding shelter, resources, and safe routes - Evasion — Escaping pursuers and avoiding danger - First Aid — Treating injuries under pressure

Proficiencies (see Ch. 15): - Streetwise — Navigating dangerous urban environments - Field Medic — Emergency medical care with limited supplies

Starting Gear: Multi-tool, first aid kit, sturdy backpack, flashlight, emergency rations, water bottle, duct tape, crowbar, worn jacket with hidden pockets.

Backstory Hook: You survived something terrible—a disaster, an attack, or an encounter with the supernatural. You carry scars, visible or otherwise. You are driven by the need to ensure others do not suffer the same fate.

Example in Play: You are pursued through the subway by a killer. You roll Evasion (DC 18) to lose them in the crowd, then Urban Survival (DC 16) to find a safehouse. You succeed with a 19 and 17—escaping into an abandoned building where you treat your injuries and plan your next move.

Modern Archetypes

Modern archetypes navigate contemporary systems, technology, and urban environments. They excel at professional expertise, technical capability, and tactical operations.

Investigator

Concept: You uncover truth in a world of lies. Whether a journalist, private investigator, or federal agent, you follow evidence, interview sources, and expose corruption. You believe transparency and accountability matter.

Chapter 05: PC Templates and Kits

Templates are plug-and-play mechanical bundles designed for fast, effective character creation. Each template combines skills, proficiencies, and starting gear into a coherent package focused on a specific play style—combat, social interaction, technical expertise, or exploration. Templates work across genres with minor adaptations, providing flexible foundations for any setting.

This chapter provides 12 templates organized by focus area. Each template includes skills, proficiencies, starting gear, genre variants, and example characters. Use templates when you need a mechanically sound character quickly, or mix elements from multiple templates to create hybrid builds.

For skills and proficiencies, see Mechanics Reference Guide Chapters 06-17. For archetypes organized by genre, see Chapter 04. For backstory development, see Chapter 06.

How to Use Templates

Templates serve two purposes:

Complete Packages: Choose a template, note the skills and proficiencies, select starting gear, and begin play. The template provides everything needed for mechanical competence.

Mix-and-Match Components: Select skills from one template, proficiencies from another, and gear from a third. Templates are modular—use them as building blocks rather than rigid frameworks.

Genre Adaptation: Each template includes genre variants showing how to adjust skills and gear for different settings. A Frontline Fighter in Fantasy becomes a Marine in Sci-Fi with minimal changes.

Customization: Swap one or two skills to reflect character backstory or unique talents. Adjust proficiencies to match campaign needs. Templates provide structure, not limitation.

Chapter 06: Backstories and Hooks

Characters are more than skills and proficiencies. They are people shaped by pasts, driven by desires, haunted by failures, and defined by relationships. A compelling backstory gives characters depth, provides narrative hooks for GMs, and creates investment in the story.

This chapter provides tools for creating rich character histories quickly or developing them deeply. Use these tools to create characters who feel real, relatable, and ready to grow.

How to Use This Chapter

Backstory creation operates at two levels:

Quick Hooks: One-sentence narrative seeds that provide immediate character motivation and GM integration points. Roll or choose a hook, note it on your character sheet, and begin play. Develop details during gameplay.

Deep Development: Full backstory templates with motivations, secrets, relationships, and integration hooks. Use these when you want a richly detailed character history before play begins, or develop them during downtime between sessions.

Collaborative Creation: Share backstory elements with your GM before play. GMs can weave backstories into campaigns, create NPC connections, and design story arcs that resonate with character histories.

Evolution During Play: Backstories are not static. Characters grow, discover new truths, and redefine themselves through play. Update backstories as characters change.

Backstory Templates

Templates provide narrative frameworks organized by common character archetypes. Each template includes motivations (what drives the character), secrets (hidden truths), relationships (connections to world and party), genre variants, and integration hooks for GMs.

Orphan Seeking Purpose

Description: You lost family early—to death, abandonment, or circumstances beyond control. You grew up without roots, searching for belonging and identity. Your journey is about finding where you fit and who you are meant to be.

Motivations: - Belonging: Finding a community, family, or cause to devote yourself to - Identity: Discovering who you are beyond your loss - Legacy: Creating something meaningful that lasts beyond you - Protection: Ensuring others do not suffer the same abandonment

Secrets: - You have a keepsake from your lost family, but you do not know what it means or why it matters - You were not abandoned by accident—someone chose to leave you, and you want to know why - You have vague memories or dreams of your family that might be real or imagined - You discovered information about your origins but have not shared it with anyone

Relationships: - Mentor Figure: Someone who helped you survive childhood and taught you skills - Fellow Orphan: A childhood friend who understands your past and shares your search - Rival: Someone from your past who competed for the same limited resources or opportunities - Party Connection: You were taken in by another party member’s family, or you found them when you needed help most

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Orphaned during a fire or illness; now you nurture community to prevent others from feeling lost - Fantasy: Parents killed by monsters or war; you train to prevent future tragedies - Horror/Mystery: Family vanished under mysterious circumstances; you investigate the supernatural - Modern: Grew up in foster care or lost family to crime; now you fight systemic injustice - Sci-Fi: Parents died in colony failure or ship disaster; you search for answers in abandoned stations

Integration Hooks: - The campaign reveals clues about your family’s fate - An NPC claims to know your parents or have information about them - Your keepsake turns out to be significant to the main plot - You discover a long-lost sibling or relative who opposes your goals

Example: Mira Ashwood (Fantasy) — Orphaned when bandits burned her village, Mira was raised by a wandering bard who taught her music and survival. She carries a silver locket she cannot open and travels seeking the bandit leader who destroyed her home. Unknown to her, the locket contains a map to a hidden family vault.

Disgraced Professional

Description: You were respected, successful, and skilled—until you lost it all. Whether through betrayal, a mistake, or standing against corruption, you fell from grace. Now you seek redemption, revenge, or a chance to rebuild what was taken.

Motivations: - Redemption: Proving you are still capable and worthy of trust - Vindication: Clearing your name and exposing those who wronged you - Revenge: Punishing those responsible for your fall - Rebuilding: Starting over and creating a new legacy

Secrets: - You were actually guilty of the crime you were accused of, but the reasons were complex - Someone you trusted framed you to cover their own misdeeds - You took the fall to protect someone else who would have suffered worse consequences - Evidence exists that could clear you, but obtaining it requires illegal or dangerous action

Relationships: - Former Colleague: Someone who still believes in you and offers quiet support - Betrayer: The person who destroyed your reputation—friend, rival, or superior - Victim: Someone hurt by your actions (real or perceived) who you want to make amends with - Party Connection: A party member hired you despite your reputation or believes in your innocence

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Lost your bakery or shop due to failed business or scandal; rebuild reputation through community service - Fantasy: Expelled from knightly order or mage guild for breaking oaths; seek to prove honor remains - Horror/Mystery: Discredited investigator who was right about supernatural threat; vindicate findings - Modern: Fired from law enforcement, medicine, or journalism for exposing corruption; work independently - Sci-Fi: Discharged from military or lost starship command; redeem yourself through heroic action

Integration Hooks: - The campaign uncovers evidence related to your disgrace - The betrayer reappears as an NPC ally or antagonist - Your former organization needs your expertise despite the past - A current plot connects to the events that destroyed your career

Example: Detective Marcus Vale (Modern) — A brilliant homicide detective fired for planting evidence to convict a guilty suspect. Marcus knew the killer would walk on a technicality, so he acted. Now a private investigator, he solves cases the police cannot touch, seeking redemption by catching criminals without breaking rules. He carries the case file of his final arrest—a reminder of his fall.

Reluctant Hero

Description: You never wanted adventure, danger, or responsibility. You were thrust into this life by circumstances—a disaster, a plea for help, or sheer misfortune. You stay because walking away feels worse than facing the danger.

Motivations: - Duty: Someone has to do this, and you are here now - Survival: Leaving might mean death for you or others - Growth: Despite reluctance, you are discovering strength you did not know you had - Return: You want to finish this and go back to normal life—if that is still possible

Secrets: - You have a way out but have not taken it because you care more than you admit - Someone you love thinks you are dead or missing; you have not contacted them for their safety - You are terrified and hide it behind competence or humor - You were running from something when this adventure began, and it is still chasing you

Relationships: - Innocent Dependent: Someone you protect who relies on you (sibling, child, friend) - Instigator: The person whose request or action dragged you into this - Fellow Reluctant: Another party member who also did not choose this life - Party Connection: You joined the party to protect someone or because refusing felt impossible

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Volunteered to organize festival or help neighbor; now responsible for entire community project - Fantasy: Ordinary villager thrust into quest when monsters attacked or prophecy named you - Horror/Mystery: Witness to supernatural event; now hunted or compelled to investigate - Modern: Civilian caught in conspiracy or disaster; forced to fight for survival - Sci-Fi: Colonist or passenger on ship when crisis erupted; became de facto leader or defender

Integration Hooks: - The campaign offers opportunities to return to normal life—test whether you take them - Your dependent or the person you left behind reappears in danger - You discover hidden strengths or destiny you could not have imagined - The party faces a crisis where your reluctant perspective saves everyone

Example: Finn Greenfield (Cozy) — A baker who agreed to help organize the village festival when the usual organizer fell ill. The festival grew into a regional event, and now Finn coordinates dozens of volunteers, manages budgets, and negotiates with merchants. He just wanted to bake pies, but he is good at this—and people are counting on him. He keeps a secret emergency recipe card in his pocket for stress relief.

Wanderer with a Secret

Description: You travel because staying in one place is dangerous—for you, for others, or both. You carry a secret that would change how people see you if they knew. You move often, form shallow connections, and guard your truth carefully.

Motivations: - Escape: Staying ahead of those hunting you or the consequences of your secret - Protection: Keeping others safe from what you carry or what follows you - Understanding: Searching for answers about your secret or a cure for your condition - Loneliness: Yearning for connection despite knowing the danger it brings

Secrets: - You possess a dangerous artifact, knowledge, or ability that others would kill for - You are cursed, infected, or changed by something supernatural or technological - You committed a crime or betrayal and are evading justice or revenge - You are not who you appear to be—false identity, hidden heritage, or concealed nature

Relationships: - Hunter: Someone tracking you who might be enemy, ally, or both - Confidant: One person who knows your secret and has not betrayed you—yet - Casualty: Someone hurt because they got too close to you - Party Connection: You told the party a version of your secret or they discovered it and chose to help anyway

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Hiding from abusive family or failed romance; seeking peaceful new start in village - Fantasy: Cursed by magic, hunted by enemies, or concealing forbidden bloodline - Horror/Mystery: Survivor of supernatural event; pursued by entity or organization covering it up - Modern: Witness protection, on the run from crime syndicate, or hiding corporate espionage - Sci-Fi: Carrying alien artifact, infected with xenobiological agent, or fleeing totalitarian regime

Integration Hooks: - The campaign forces you to reveal your secret to save the party - Your hunter catches up and the party must choose sides - Your secret becomes relevant to the main plot in unexpected ways - You discover others with similar secrets who offer understanding or danger

Example: Zara Nightwind (Fantasy) — A wandering merchant who sells exotic goods in each town before moving on. Zara is a former assassin fleeing the guild that trained her after refusing a kill. She carries the guild’s token—proof of membership—hidden in her wagon. If she destroys it, the guild stops hunting her, but she can never return or seek their protection. She has not decided which fate is worse.

Protector of the Innocent

Description: You have dedicated your life to defending those who cannot defend themselves. Whether driven by justice, vengeance, or compassion, you stand between predators and prey. You have seen suffering and refuse to look away.

Motivations: - Justice: Punishing wrongdoers and creating accountability - Prevention: Stopping harm before it occurs - Vengeance: Making those who hurt innocents pay for their crimes - Compassion: Easing suffering and offering protection to the vulnerable

Secrets: - You failed to protect someone important, and that failure defines you - Your methods are brutal or illegal, and you justify them by results - You are driven by revenge disguised as justice - You carry guilt for harm you caused before becoming a protector

Relationships: - Saved Individual: Someone you protected who inspired or changed you - Rival Protector: Someone with similar goals but opposing methods - Victim’s Family: People who lost someone you could not save - Party Connection: You protect a party member who reminds you of someone you failed

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Village mediator or caretaker who ensures everyone is safe and cared for - Fantasy: Oath-bound knight, vigilante warrior, or defender of frontier settlements - Horror/Mystery: Survivor who helps others escape supernatural threats - Modern: Social worker, vigilante, or activist fighting systemic harm - Sci-Fi: Colony defender, refugee advocate, or marine protecting civilian transports

Integration Hooks: - The campaign features innocents in danger, testing your limits - Your past failure reappears—chance for redemption or renewed trauma - You must choose between saving strangers or protecting the party - Your methods create moral dilemmas for the group

Example: Kael Ironshield (Fantasy) — A former city guard who left his post after corrupt superiors prevented him from protecting a noble family from assassins. Now a traveling mercenary, Kael only accepts contracts defending the helpless. He carries a notched sword and a guilt-worn prayer book. He refuses payment from those who cannot afford it and hunts down those who prey on the weak.

Scholar Chasing Mystery

Description: You are obsessed with uncovering a specific truth—a historical mystery, a scientific puzzle, or a supernatural enigma. Your research has consumed you, isolated you, and possibly endangered you. You cannot stop until you understand.

Motivations: - Knowledge: Understanding the truth regardless of cost - Vindication: Proving your theories against skeptics and opposition - Application: Using your discoveries to solve problems or save lives - Obsession: Unable to let go even when it becomes dangerous

Secrets: - Your research has uncovered something dangerous that others want suppressed - You stole or damaged something to further your investigation - Your findings challenge powerful institutions or beliefs - You are losing objectivity and possibly sanity in your pursuit

Relationships: - Academic Rival: Someone pursuing the same mystery with opposing theories - Mentor: The person who inspired your research or warned you to stop - Patron: Someone funding your work who might have ulterior motives - Party Connection: The party encountered evidence related to your mystery, pulling you together

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Researching local history, folklore, or genealogy to preserve community memory - Fantasy: Seeking lost magical knowledge, ancient artifacts, or forbidden lore - Horror/Mystery: Investigating supernatural phenomena despite psychological cost - Modern: Uncovering corporate conspiracy, historical coverup, or scientific breakthrough - Sci-Fi: Studying alien artifacts, lost colonies, or theoretical physics anomalies

Integration Hooks: - The campaign reveals pieces of your mystery - Your research becomes central to solving the main plot - Pursuing your mystery endangers the party or requires difficult choices - You discover the truth and must decide what to do with it

Example: Professor Asha Thornfield (Horror/Mystery) — A folklore scholar investigating recurring disappearances in small towns across three centuries. Asha has identified a pattern no one else sees—victims taken during lunar eclipses, always seven at a time. Her university dismissed her findings as coincidence. She continues alone, following the pattern to the next predicted disappearance. She carries a journal filled with notes, sketches, and dates that terrify her.

Survivor of Trauma

Description: You endured something that broke others. Whether violence, loss, or betrayal, the experience shaped you permanently. You survived, but you are not the same. You cope, grow, or struggle—sometimes all three.

Motivations: - Recovery: Healing from the trauma and reclaiming agency - Prevention: Ensuring others do not suffer similarly - Revenge: Punishing those responsible - Meaning: Finding purpose in the suffering

Secrets: - You are not as recovered as you pretend; you hide ongoing struggles - You blame yourself for what happened, even if rationally you know better - You made a terrible choice during the trauma that haunts you - Part of you has not processed what happened and exists in denial or dissociation

Relationships: - Fellow Survivor: Someone who shares the experience and understands - Therapist/Healer: Someone helping you process and recover - Perpetrator: The person or entity responsible for your trauma - Party Connection: A party member saw your vulnerability and offered support without judgment

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Survived fire, illness, or loss of loved one; now cherishes community and small joys - Fantasy: Survived battle, captivity, or magical curse; now stronger but scarred - Horror/Mystery: Survived supernatural encounter or cult ritual; psychologically marked - Modern: Survived violence, disaster, or systemic abuse; now fights for change - Sci-Fi: Survived colony disaster, alien attack, or ship malfunction; carries physical or mental scars

Integration Hooks: - The campaign features triggers related to your trauma—opportunities for growth or setback - You encounter someone else suffering similarly and must decide whether to help - The perpetrator reappears, forcing confrontation - Your trauma gives you unique insight that helps solve a crisis

Example: Iris Winterbourne (Horror/Mystery) — Sole survivor of a cult ritual that killed her friends. Iris escaped by pretending to be dead and hiding until dawn. She suffers from nightmares, hypervigilance, and flashbacks. Now she works with a paranormal investigation team, using her trauma to help other survivors. She carries a journal where she records her progress and setbacks. Some days are better than others.

Inheritor of Legacy

Description: You carry the weight of expectation. Whether family name, cultural tradition, or destiny, you were born into a role you did not choose. You walk a path carved by others, struggling to honor the past while forging your own identity.

Motivations: - Honor: Living up to the legacy and proving worthy - Rebellion: Rejecting the burden and defining yourself independently - Redemption: Restoring a tarnished legacy - Understanding: Discovering what the legacy truly means and choosing consciously

Secrets: - You do not want the legacy but feel trapped by obligation or expectation - You discovered something shameful about your legacy that changes how you see it - You are not the true inheritor—someone else has a stronger claim - The legacy is cursed, dangerous, or comes with hidden costs

Relationships: - Ancestor Figure: Dead or living, the person whose legacy you inherit - Rival Claimant: Someone else who wants or deserves the legacy - Mentor: Someone teaching you to fulfill or reject the legacy - Party Connection: A party member respects, fears, or resents your legacy

Genre Variants: - Cozy: Inheriting family business, recipe, or community role - Fantasy: Noble bloodline, magical gift, or prophesied destiny - Horror/Mystery: Cursed family history or inherited supernatural burden - Modern: Family business, political dynasty, or criminal empire - Sci-Fi: Genetic legacy, inherited starship, or destined role in colony leadership

Integration Hooks: - The campaign tests whether you embrace or reject the legacy - Someone challenges your claim to the inheritance - The legacy becomes crucial to solving the main plot - You discover the truth about your legacy and must redefine yourself

Example: Lord Theron Ashford (Fantasy) — Last heir to a noble house that lost its lands during a civil war. Theron was raised in exile, taught that reclaiming the family estate is his duty. But when he finally saw the lands, he found them thriving under new stewardship. Now he questions whether honoring his family means destroying the peace. He carries the family signet ring but has not decided whether to claim his birthright.

Quick Hooks Library

Quick hooks are one-sentence backstory seeds organized by genre. Roll randomly (d6 or d8) or choose one that resonates. Develop details during play or use as starting points for full backstory templates.

Cozy Hooks

  1. You learned your craft from a beloved relative who recently passed, leaving you their tools and unfinished projects.
  2. You moved to this village to escape a painful memory and start fresh, but the past keeps finding you.
  3. You accidentally discovered a secret in the town archives that could change everything if revealed.
  4. You are the only person who can recreate a lost family recipe, and everyone wants you to share it.
  5. You made a promise to someone on their deathbed that you are struggling to keep.
  6. You inherited a business or property that comes with unexpected complications or debts.

Fantasy Hooks

  1. You carry a weapon or item that once belonged to a legendary hero, and you do not know how you came to possess it.
  2. You witnessed something in the wilderness that no one believes—a creature, a ruin, or a phenomenon.
  3. You broke an oath or failed a quest, and you seek redemption through service.
  4. You have a recurring dream about a place you have never been but feel compelled to find.
  5. You owe a life debt to someone powerful and morally questionable, and they just called in the favor.
  6. You discovered you have magical ability or heritage you were not raised to understand.

Horror/Mystery Hooks

  1. You survived an event that killed everyone else, and you do not remember how you escaped.
  2. You have seen something that should not exist, and it has seen you too.
  3. You are investigating the disappearance of someone close to you, despite warnings to let it go.
  4. You possess evidence of a conspiracy or supernatural truth that powerful people want destroyed.
  5. You are haunted—literally or figuratively—by someone who died because of you.
  6. You discovered a family secret hidden for generations that explains your recurring nightmares.

Modern Hooks

  1. You blew the whistle on corruption and lost your career, safety, or relationships as a result.
  2. You are searching for someone who vanished under suspicious circumstances years ago.
  3. You witnessed a crime or accident that authorities covered up, and you cannot let it go.
  4. You inherited a storage unit, safe deposit box, or digital files containing secrets you do not understand.
  5. You owe money to dangerous people and are working off the debt through questionable jobs.
  6. You received a cryptic message from someone you thought was dead.

Sci-Fi Hooks

  1. You were part of a failed colony, expedition, or military operation and are one of the few survivors.
  2. You carry an alien artifact or piece of advanced technology that you do not fully understand.
  3. You discovered information about your genetic modification, cloning origin, or synthetic nature.
  4. You are searching for a lost ship, station, or colony that disappeared under mysterious circumstances.
  5. You deserted from military service, corporate indenture, or authoritarian regime and are hiding your identity.
  6. You have fragmented memories of a time or event that official records say never happened.

Integration and Development

Backstories are most powerful when integrated into gameplay:

Share with Your GM: Provide your backstory to the GM before or during Session Zero. GMs can weave backstories into campaigns, create meaningful NPCs, and design arcs that resonate with character histories.

Create Party Connections: Discuss backstories with other players. Establish relationships, shared history, or complementary motivations that tie the party together narratively.

Update During Play: Characters grow through experience. Update your backstory as you learn new truths, form new relationships, or redefine yourself. Add notes about significant events, changed beliefs, or resolved conflicts.

Use Downtime: Develop backstory elements during downtime between adventures. Write letters, visit family, research mysteries, or pursue personal goals. Backstory is not just past—it is ongoing character life.

Create Narrative Hooks: Give your GM tools to engage your character. Include NPCs who might reappear, unresolved conflicts that could escalate, or mysteries that drive investigation. Backstories provide fuel for future stories.

Chapter 23: PC Quick Reference

This chapter provides a streamlined checklist for creating player characters, consolidating the complete PC creation workflow into an easy-to-follow reference guide.

PC Creation Checklist

Follow these steps to create a complete player character. Reference chapters are noted for detailed guidance.

Step 1: Choose Concept and Archetype

Choose Your Genre: Cozy, Fantasy, Horror/Mystery, Modern, or Sci-Fi

Select Archetype (Chapter 20): - Review archetypes for your chosen genre - Pick one that matches your character concept - Note the archetype’s core identity and role

Customization Option: Use archetype as starting point and modify to fit your specific vision

Step 2: Select Core Skills

From Archetype (Chapter 20): - Note the 3-5 core skills listed in your archetype - Review skill descriptions in Mechanics Reference Chapters 06-11

Customization: - Swap 1-2 skills for others from the same genre chapter if desired - Ensure skills support your character concept and party role

Cross-Reference: Mechanics Reference Ch. 07 (Cozy), Ch. 08 (Fantasy), Ch. 09 (Horror/Mystery), Ch. 10 (Modern), Ch. 11 (Sci-Fi)

Step 3: Select Proficiencies

From Archetype or Template (Chapters 20-21): - Note the 2-3 proficiencies from your archetype - Review proficiency effects in Mechanics Reference Chapters 13-17

Template Option (Chapter 21): - Choose a template kit (Combat, Social, Technical, Exploration) - Use template proficiencies if they fit better than archetype defaults

Cross-Reference: Mechanics Reference Ch. 13 (Cozy), Ch. 14 (Fantasy), Ch. 15 (Horror/Mystery), Ch. 16 (Modern), Ch. 17 (Sci-Fi)

Step 4: Choose Starting Gear

From Archetype (Chapter 20): - Note starting gear listed in archetype entry - Add genre-appropriate equipment based on concept

From Template Kit (Chapter 21): - If using a template, note the detailed gear list provided - Templates offer more comprehensive equipment selections

GM Approval: Confirm unusual or expensive items with your GM

Step 5: Develop Backstory

Backstory Hook (Chapter 20): - Start with the hook provided in your archetype - Expand with personal details and history

Backstory Templates (Chapter 22): - Choose a template that resonates (Orphan, Disgraced Professional, Reluctant Hero, etc.) - Add motivations, secrets, and relationships

Quick Hooks (Chapter 22): - Use the quick hooks library for immediate play integration - Connect backstory to campaign and other PCs

Step 6: Finalize Stats and Details

Mechanical Review: - Confirm all skills and proficiencies are noted - Verify starting gear is appropriate for power level - Note any special abilities from proficiencies

Character Details: - Name, appearance, personality traits - Connections to other PCs or world - Character goals and motivations

Cross-Reference: Player’s Handbook Ch. 05 (Character Creation), Ch. 19 (Character Creation Overview)

Quick Tips for Players

Start with Fiction: Begin with who your character is, not their stats. Identity drives mechanics, not the other way around.

Party Integration: Create at least one relationship or shared connection with another PC during character creation.

Leave Hooks: Build unresolved mysteries, important relationships, or ongoing quests into your backstory. Give your GM problems to resolve, not perfect heroes with nothing to prove.

Mechanical Coherence: When someone reads your skill list, they should understand who your character is without reading the backstory.

Genre Appropriateness: Ensure skills, proficiencies, and gear match your campaign’s genre and tone.
