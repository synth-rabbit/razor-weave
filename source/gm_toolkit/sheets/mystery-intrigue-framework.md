# Mystery & Intrigue Framework

Structure mysteries and investigations that players can actually solve.

---

## Core Principles

### The Three Clue Rule
**Every crucial revelation needs at least 3 clues**
- Prevents investigation stalling
- Allows for missed clues
- Provides redundancy

### Node-Based Design
**Clues lead to locations/NPCs, not linear path**
- Multiple entry points
- Non-linear investigation
- Player agency matters

### Information, Not Puzzles
**Mystery is about discovering what happened**
- Not about guessing what GM is thinking
- Not about single solution puzzles
- About piecing together information

## Clue Types

### Physical Evidence
**What**: Tangible objects, traces, forensics
**Examples**:
- Murder weapon with fingerprints
- Torn piece of fabric
- Chemical residue
- Footprints or tracks
- Documents, letters, receipts

### Testimony
**What**: What NPCs say (truth or lies)
**Examples**:
- Witness statements
- Suspect alibis
- Rumors and gossip
- Expert opinions
- Confessions (partial or full)

### Behavioral
**What**: How NPCs act
**Examples**:
- Nervous behavior
- Avoiding certain topics
- Reactions to accusations
- Who talks to whom
- Changes in routine

### Contextual
**What**: Background knowledge, connections
**Examples**:
- Historical patterns
- Known relationships
- Financial records
- Expertise or skills required
- Access to locations

## Mystery Complexity Levels

### Simple Mystery (1-2 sessions)
**Structure**:
- 1 central question
- 3-5 nodes
- 6-10 clues total
- Single revelation

**Example**: Who stole the artifact?

---

### Standard Mystery (2-4 sessions)
**Structure**:
- 2-3 connected questions
- 5-8 nodes
- 12-18 clues total
- Multiple revelations building to conclusion

**Example**: Who was murdered, why, and who's next?

---

### Complex Mystery (4-8 sessions)
**Structure**:
- Multiple interwoven questions
- 8-12+ nodes
- 20+ clues total
- Layered revelations
- Red herrings

**Example**: Conspiracy with multiple guilty parties, hidden motives, and layers of deception

---

## Red Herrings

### Purpose
- Create uncertainty
- Make solution feel earned
- Add complexity

### Guidelines
**Good Red Herrings**:
- Plausible but ultimately incorrect
- Lead to interesting encounters
- Don't waste time
- Have their own resolution

**Bad Red Herrings**:
- Arbitrary and confusing
- Dead ends that frustrate
- Withhold information for no reason
- Punish clever thinking

## Clue Revelation Methods

### Automatic Clues
**Give freely when**:
- PCs look in right place
- Ask right question
- Use relevant skill

**Never gate progress behind rolls**

### Skill-Based Clues
**Provide additional context**:
- Automatic: Find the bloody knife
- Success: Identify it's expensive, custom-made
- Great success: Recognize smith who made it

### Purchased Information
**Cost resources**:
- Money to informants
- Favors owed
- Reputation
- Time

## Running Investigations

### When Players Are Stuck

**Don't**:
- Wait for them to guess correctly
- Gate progress behind single roll
- Punish for not finding specific clue

**Do**:
- Have NPC provide hint
- Let them find clue another way
- Advance threat to create urgency
- Time skip to next clue appearing
- Consolidate multiple clues into one

---

### When Players Jump to Conclusion

**If Correct**:
- Great! Move to confrontation/proof gathering
- Don't artificially extend
- Let them feel clever

**If Incorrect**:
- Don't tell them they're wrong
- Let them pursue it
- Provide evidence that contradicts
- Reveal through consequences

---

### When Players Go Off-Track

**If Interesting**:
- Go with it
- Move clues to where they're looking
- Revise truth if better story

**If Derailing**:
- Gentle redirection through NPCs
- Event brings focus back
- Clear dead end with quick resolution

## Mystery Scenes

### Interview/Interrogation
**Setup**: Who, where, what they know
**Complications**: Lies, omissions, secrets
**Revelation**: Key clue or misdirection
**Lead**: Where to go next

### Crime Scene Investigation
**Setup**: Describe scene in detail
**Clues**: What's there to find
**Complications**: Time limit, interference
**Lead**: What clues point to

### Research/Records
**Setup**: What information exists
**Clues**: What can be learned
**Complications**: Missing info, misinformation
**Lead**: New angle to pursue

### Surveillance/Stakeout
**Setup**: What/who being watched
**Clues**: What behaviors observed
**Complications**: Being noticed, losing target
**Lead**: Where they go, who they meet

## Mystery Checklist

**Before First Session**:
- ☐ Truth fully determined
- ☐ 3 clues for each revelation
- ☐ 3-5 starting nodes identified
- ☐ NPCs with secrets prepared
- ☐ Optional: timer clock prepared

**During Investigation**:
- ☐ Give clues freely when PCs look
- ☐ Multiple paths forward visible
- ☐ Advance clock periodically
- ☐ Let players be clever
- ☐ Adapt to player theories

**At Conclusion**:
- ☐ Let players explain theory
- ☐ Reveal truth
- ☐ Show how clues fit
- ☐ Confrontation/resolution
- ☐ Consequences and rewards

## Genre-Specific Tips

### Cozy Mystery
- Low stakes (stolen items, small crimes)
- Everyone has alibis and secrets
- Resolution restores harmony
- Focus on character and community

### Fantasy Mystery
- Magic complicates investigations
- Supernatural elements hide truth
- Divination provides clues not solutions
- Monsters as suspects or tools

### Horror Mystery
- Truth is disturbing
- Investigation dangerous
- Some knowledge harmful
- Solving may not help

### Modern Mystery
- Technology provides clues and obstacles
- Forensics and records
- Jurisdiction and law
- Media and public pressure

### Sci-Fi Mystery
- Advanced tech creates new evidence
- AI and hacking
- Alien perspectives
- Physics/science crucial
