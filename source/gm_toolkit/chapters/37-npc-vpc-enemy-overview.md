# Chapter 37: NPC, VPC, and Enemy Design Overview {#chapter-37}

This chapter provides the framework for creating compelling non-player characters, virtual player characters, and enemies that support your campaign without stealing the spotlight from player characters.

## Character Types for GMs {#character-types}

As a GM, you create three distinct types of characters, each with specific design principles and narrative purposes:

**Non-Player Characters (NPCs)**: The supporting cast—allies, rivals, mentors, antagonists, and informants who populate your world and create story hooks. NPCs have defined roles and goals but less mechanical detail than PCs, making them efficient to create and run.

**Virtual Player Characters (VPCs)**: Companions that fill party gaps in solo play or small groups. They provide mechanical support and narrative presence without driving decisions. Well-designed VPCs react rather than lead, complement rather than compete.

**Enemies & Threats**: Opposition in combat, social, or exploration scenes. They have motivations (even if simple), weaknesses (exploitable through clever play), and tactics (patterns players can learn and counter). Meaningful enemies are more than stat blocks.

## GM Character Philosophy {#gm-character-philosophy}

Every GM character follows the same three pillars as PC creation, but with different priorities:

**Purpose-Driven Identity**: Start with what the character contributes to play. NPCs need personality and goals more than complete stat blocks. A memorable voice, mannerism, or moral compass makes an NPC stick in players' minds more than detailed mechanics.

**Scalable Mechanical Coherence**: Create detail proportional to narrative importance. Quick-stat background characters need only name + role + one trait. Recurring characters deserve full personalities, skills, goals, and secrets. Don't over-invest in characters who may never return.

**Narrative Function**: Every character serves a story purpose. Understanding that function ensures the character contributes to play rather than drifts in the background. Design around what they enable for the PCs, not what they can do independently.

## Non-Player Characters (NPCs) {#npcs}

NPCs form the supporting cast of your campaign. They should enhance PC agency, not replace it.

### Core Principles {#npc-principles}

**Goals and Secrets**: NPCs come alive through wanting things and hiding things. Goals create hooks ("I need you to find my missing daughter"). Secrets create complications ("The daughter isn't missing—she's hiding from her father's gambling debts").

**Personality Over Stats**: A distinctive voice, mannerism, or moral compass makes an NPC memorable. "The baker who hums while nervous and feeds everyone" is more memorable than "NPC with Cooking +2."

**Limited Spotlight**: NPCs should be helpful without being servile, competent without stealing spotlight, and grateful without being obsequious. They empower protagonists rather than replace them.

### NPC Roles {#npc-roles}

**Ally**: Supports PCs with resources, information, or assistance. Has own goals that usually align with party interests but create complications when they don't.

**Rival**: Competes with PCs for similar goals without being outright enemies. Raises stakes by making success uncertain.

**Mentor**: Guides and teaches. Has limitations—age, injury, political constraints—that prevent solving problems directly.

**Antagonist**: Opposes PCs with conflicting goals. Has complex motivations, can be reasoned with, believes they're justified.

**Informant**: Provides information transactionally or reluctantly. Controls story pacing through what they know and when they share it.

## Virtual Player Characters (VPCs) {#vpcs}

VPCs fill party gaps without overshadowing players. They're companions, not additional protagonists.

### Core Principles {#vpc-principles}

**Reactive, Not Proactive**: VPCs follow PC leads. They offer suggestions but defer to player decisions. They complement party strengths and cover weaknesses without driving the narrative.

**Mechanical Support**: VPCs provide skills the party lacks (healing, technical expertise, combat support) but at slightly lower effectiveness than a dedicated PC would.

**Behavioral Scripts**: Rather than full personalities, VPCs use simple behavioral scripts that make them predictable and manageable. Scripts define how they react to common situations without requiring constant GM attention.

**Spotlight Management**: VPCs shine when filling their niche role, then step back. They never solve the central problem or make the critical decision.

## Enemies & Threats {#enemies}

Enemies provide opposition that makes PC victories meaningful. They should challenge players, not frustrate them.

### Core Principles {#enemy-principles}

**Motivation and Weaknesses**: Even simple enemies have reasons for their actions and exploitable weaknesses. This creates opportunities for clever play beyond "roll initiative."

**Tactical Variety**: Different enemy types use different tactics. Minions swarm, elites coordinate, bosses adapt. Patterns create gameplay depth and reward player learning.

**Threat Levels**: Minions provide numbers and pressure. Elites create tactical challenges. Bosses drive major confrontations. Mix threat levels for dynamic encounters.

**Story Integration**: The best enemies connect to the larger narrative. They have goals that create conflict, allegiances that complicate situations, and backstories that make defeat or negotiation meaningful.

See Chapter 41 (Enemy Design Framework) and Chapter 42 (Enemy Packs) for detailed enemy creation and genre-specific stat blocks.

## Fiction-First GM Character Design {#fiction-first-gm}

For all GM character types, start with narrative purpose:

1. **Function**: What does this character enable for the PCs? What role do they play in the story?
2. **Concept**: One sentence describing who they are and what they want.
3. **Personality**: 1-3 distinctive traits that guide roleplay.
4. **Goal**: What they want right now (creates immediate hooks).
5. **Secret** (NPCs) or **Weakness** (Enemies): One hidden element that creates complications or opportunities.
6. **Mechanics**: Only as much detail as their narrative importance requires.

This keeps preparation efficient and ensures every character serves a purpose at the table.
